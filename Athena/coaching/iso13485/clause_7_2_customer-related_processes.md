# ISO 13485:2016 — Clause 7.2: Customer-Related Processes

# ISO 13485:2016 Clause 7.2: Customer-Related Processes

*Educational content only. Always refer to the actual ISO 13485:2016 standard for official requirements.*

---

## What This Clause Is About

Medical device companies can't just build what they think customers want — they have to systematically figure out what customers actually require, including requirements that customers don't always spell out. This clause makes sure your organization captures, reviews, and communicates requirements before you commit to supplying a product, so you're not discovering mismatches after manufacturing has started or, worse, after a device is already in the field.

---

## What It Requires (The Essentials)

**1. Determine all product requirements before you commit.**
This means written customer requirements, but also regulatory requirements for the intended market, requirements the customer didn't state but that are necessary for intended use (a surgeon expects a sterile drape to actually be sterile, even if the purchase order doesn't say "must be sterile"), and any internal requirements your company adds.

**2. Review those requirements before accepting an order.**
Before you say "yes, we can do that," someone with authority needs to confirm your organization can actually meet all the requirements you've identified. This review must be documented. If there's a difference between what a customer asked for verbally versus what's in their written purchase order, you resolve it before accepting the order — not after.

**3. Handle changes through a controlled process.**
If requirements change after you've accepted an order, that change needs to go through a formal process. Relevant documents get updated, and the right people get told. An email chain where someone says "actually, can we change the label size?" and a team member says "sure" is not a controlled change.

**4. Communicate with customers.**
This includes giving customers information about the product, handling inquiries and orders, managing feedback (especially complaints), and handling advisory notices — communications you send when there's a safety-related issue with a device already in the field.

## What This Looks Like in Practice

Imagine a 45-person company called Meridian Surgical that makes single-use laparoscopic instruments. Their Sales team receives a request for quote (RFQ) from a hospital system in Germany.

The Sales Manager doesn't just email back a price. She opens the **Customer Requirements Capture Form** in their QMS and works through a checklist: What are the stated product specs? What regulatory requirements apply (EU MDR in this case)? What are the labeling requirements for the German market? Are there sterilization validation requirements tied to the packaging the customer wants?

She loops in Regulatory Affairs because the customer wants to add their own hospital logo to the label — that's a labeling change that needs review. RA confirms it's acceptable under their EU Declaration of Conformity as long as certain fields stay unchanged.

Before the quote goes out, a **Contract Review Record** gets completed and signed off. It documents every requirement identified and confirms Meridian can meet them. If the hospital later sends a formal purchase order that says something different from the RFQ — say, they specified 10mm diameter but the PO says 12mm — the Customer Service Coordinator flags the discrepancy, contacts the customer for clarification, and documents the resolution before the order is accepted in their ERP system.

When a complaint comes in three months later about packaging integrity, the same Customer-Related Process framework routes that feedback into their complaint handling procedure, which feeds back into CAPA and design controls if needed.

## Common Mistakes and Audit Findings

**1. Undocumented verbal agreements.**
A sales rep verbally agrees to a custom configuration during a sales call and internal teams start working on it. Nothing is written down, no contract review happens. Auditors look for records. "We talked about it" is not a record.

**2. Skipping the review when orders look routine.**
Teams often skip formal contract review for repeat customers, assuming nothing has changed. Regulatory requirements change. Customer specifications get revised. A blanket purchase order that looks the same as last year's might reference updated specifications. Every order needs review — the effort scales with risk and complexity, but it can't be skipped entirely.

**3. Treating customer complaints as one-off customer service issues.**
Complaint handling is part of this clause. Early-career professionals sometimes handle a complaint by apologizing and sending a replacement, without routing it into the formal complaint handling and MDR/vigilance reporting workflow. That's a serious finding.

**4. No defined process for communicating regulatory/advisory notices.**
If a company issues a field safety corrective action (a recall or safety notice), there needs to be a defined process for how that gets communicated to customers. Companies that haven't thought this through before they need it are not ready for an audit — or a real safety event.

## Key Terms to Know

**Customer requirements** — What the customer explicitly asks for, documented in a specification, contract, or purchase order.

**Implied requirements** — Things the customer expects but didn't write down, based on the intended use of the product. A sterile device needs to be sterile. A pediatric device needs to be safe for children.

**Contract review** — The documented process of checking that all requirements are understood and that your organization can meet them before accepting an order.

**Regulatory requirements** — Legal and regulatory obligations that apply to the product, independent of what the customer asks for. These vary by market (FDA 21 CFR Part 820, EU MDR, Health Canada, etc.).

**Advisory notice** — A communication sent to customers after a product has been distributed, to provide information about risks or required actions. Often associated with recalls or field safety corrective actions.

**Complaint** — Any written, electronic, or oral communication that alleges a deficiency in identity, quality, durability, reliability, safety, effectiveness, or performance of a device after it's been released for distribution.

**FSCA (Field Safety Corrective Action)** — An action taken to reduce a risk of death or serious deterioration in health associated with a device already in the market. The EU equivalent of a recall.

## Check Your Understanding

**1. A customer's purchase order arrives and it specifies a different torque specification than what was in the original RFQ. What should happen before the order is accepted?**
The discrepancy must be identified, clarified with the customer, and documented. The contract review record should reflect the resolved requirement before the order is accepted into your system.

**2. Why can't you rely only on what the customer writes in a purchase order to define product requirements?**
Purchase orders capture stated requirements but miss implied requirements (based on intended use) and regulatory requirements (which apply regardless of what the customer specifies). Clause 7.2 requires you to determine all three categories.

**3. True or False: A verbal change request from a customer is acceptable as long as you note it in the project file.**
False. Changes to requirements need to go through a documented, controlled change process, and relevant documents must be updated. Verbal agreements documented informally don't meet this bar.

**4. Scenario: A hospital calls to complain that the packaging on your company's catheter was difficult to open under sterile field conditions. The customer service rep apologizes, sends a replacement, and closes the ticket. What's missing?**
The complaint needed to be entered into the formal complaint handling system, evaluated for whether it constitutes a reportable event (MDR/vigilance), and potentially fed into CAPA. Replacing the product and closing the ticket doesn't fulfill the QMS requirement.

**5. What is the purpose of a contract review, in plain terms?**
To confirm — in writing, before committing — that your company understands every requirement it needs to meet and that it actually has the ability to meet them.

## How This Connects to Your Career

Customer-related processes sit at the intersection of Sales, Regulatory, Quality, and Operations — which means when something goes wrong here, everyone points fingers and no one owns it. A QA/RA professional who understands Clause 7.2 can build the bridges between those teams: designing contract review workflows that Sales will actually use, making sure complaint handling doesn't stop at customer service, and ensuring regulatory requirements for new markets get captured before a product ships. Auditors routinely find gaps here because companies treat it as a sales administration task rather than a quality system requirement. When you know the difference, and you can explain it to a Sales Manager without making them feel attacked, that's a skill that makes you genuinely useful in any device company.
