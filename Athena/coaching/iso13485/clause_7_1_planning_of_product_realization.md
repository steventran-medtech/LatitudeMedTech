# ISO 13485:2016 — Clause 7.1: Planning of Product Realization

# ISO 13485:2016 Clause 7.1: Planning of Product Realization

*Educational content only. Always refer to the actual ISO 13485:2016 standard for official requirements.*

---

## What This Clause Is About

Before you build a medical device, someone has to think through *how* you're going to build it — what processes you need, what records you'll keep, what risks you have to manage, and how you'll prove the product meets requirements. Clause 7.1 is the planning requirement that forces that thinking to happen up front, in a structured way, rather than improvising as you go. Without it, product development becomes chaotic, and you end up missing critical controls that regulators will absolutely find later.

---

## What It Requires (The Essentials)

**1. Define quality objectives and product requirements before you start.**
Before development begins, the team must establish what the product needs to do, what standards apply, and what "success" looks like. Vague objectives — "it should work well" — don't count.

**2. Establish the processes, documents, and resources you'll need.**
You have to plan *what* processes are required (design, manufacturing, sterilization, etc.) and identify what documentation, equipment, and people those processes need to function.

**3. Plan for verification, validation, monitoring, inspection, and testing.**
This is a big one. Your plan must spell out how you'll confirm that the product and its processes actually work. Verification checks that outputs meet inputs (did we build it to spec?). Validation checks that the product works for its intended use in the real world.

**4. Define records that prove it all happened.**
ISO 13485 is a documented system. Your planning output needs to identify what records you'll generate to provide evidence that product realization went as planned.

**5. Align the plan with your overall QMS.**
Your product realization plan can't exist in isolation. It has to be consistent with the rest of your Quality Management System — risk management procedures, design controls, supplier requirements, and so on.

## What This Looks Like in Practice

Imagine a 45-person company — call them Apex Surgical — that makes single-use electrosurgical instruments. They've just kicked off development of a new bipolar forceps.

The **Director of R&D** and **Quality Engineer** sit down at project initiation and create a document called the **Quality Plan** (sometimes also called a **Product Development Plan** or **Design and Development Plan**). This document doesn't have to be fancy, but it has to answer specific questions:

- What are the intended uses and user needs for this forceps?
- What design inputs (performance specs, standards like IEC 60601-1, regulatory requirements) apply?
- What design and development stages will this project go through, and who is responsible for each?
- What verification testing (bench testing to spec) and validation activities (usability studies, animal studies) are planned?
- What risk management activities apply, and when? (Usually references the **Risk Management Plan**, a separate document per ISO 14971.)
- What manufacturing processes need to be developed or validated (e.g., cable assembly, sterilization validation)?
- What records will be generated at each stage?

This Quality Plan becomes a living reference throughout the project. The **Project Manager** uses it in design reviews to confirm the team hasn't skipped anything. The **Regulatory Affairs Specialist** refers to it when building the technical file. When a notified body auditor comes in, they'll ask to see this document early — it shows them the team actually planned the work, not just executed it.

## Common Mistakes and Audit Findings

**1. Creating the plan after the fact.**
Teams complete development, then write a Quality Plan to describe what they already did. Auditors are good at spotting this — revision histories, file creation dates, and inconsistencies in document cross-references give it away immediately. This is a major finding.

**2. Planning documents that are too vague to be useful.**
"Perform testing as needed" is not a plan. Auditors look for specificity: what tests, against what acceptance criteria, performed by whom, at what stage. Vague plans fail both the audit and the project.

**3. Failing to connect the plan to risk management.**
Clause 7.1 explicitly requires risk management activities to be part of planning. Many early-career QAs treat the Risk Management Plan as a separate standalone document and forget to reference it — or integrate it — in the product realization plan. Auditors will look for this connection.

**4. Not updating the plan when scope changes.**
A plan that reflects the original project but not a significant design change is worse than no plan, because it creates a false record. When development pivots, the Quality Plan must be updated.

## Key Terms to Know

**Product Realization** — The complete set of processes that take a product from concept through design, manufacturing, and delivery to the customer.

**Quality Plan** — A document (required or referenced by Clause 7.1) that specifies the procedures, resources, and sequence of activities relevant to a specific product or project.

**Verification** — Confirming through objective evidence that a specified requirement has been met. Example: bench testing that a forceps delivers the correct energy output per its design specification.

**Validation** — Confirming through objective evidence that a product or process consistently meets its intended use requirements. Example: a usability study confirming surgeons can safely operate the device.

**Design Input** — The physical, functional, performance, safety, and regulatory requirements the product must meet. These are the "givens" you design to.

**Design Output** — The result of the design process — drawings, specifications, software, manufacturing procedures — that describe the finished product.

**Risk Management** — A systematic process (governed by ISO 14971 for medical devices) for identifying, evaluating, controlling, and monitoring risks associated with a device.

## Check Your Understanding

**1. What is the primary purpose of Clause 7.1?**
*Answer: To ensure that before product development or manufacturing begins, the organization plans the processes, resources, documentation, and controls needed to produce a conforming product — rather than improvising along the way.*

**2. What is the difference between verification and validation?**
*Answer: Verification confirms a product meets its specified requirements (did we build it right?). Validation confirms it meets the needs of the intended use in real-world conditions (did we build the right thing?)*

**3. True or False: A Quality Plan can be written after product development is complete, as long as it accurately reflects what was done.**
*Answer: False. The whole point of planning is that it happens before and guides the work. Backdating or retroactively creating plans is a serious audit finding and a documentation integrity issue.*

**4. Scenario: A team is developing a Class II wound care monitor. During development, the marketing team expands the intended use to include pediatric patients. What should happen to the Quality Plan?**
*Answer: The Quality Plan must be updated to reflect the expanded intended use. This likely triggers additional design inputs, new or updated risk management activities, and potentially different verification and validation requirements.*

**5. Scenario: An auditor asks a QA technician where they can see evidence that risk management was integrated into the product realization plan for a recently launched product. The QA technician hands them the Risk Management File. Is this sufficient?**
*Answer: Probably not on its own. The auditor wants to see that the Quality Plan or product realization planning documents explicitly reference and integrate risk management activities — not just that a Risk Management File exists separately. The connection between planning and risk management needs to be visible.*

## How This Connects to Your Career

Understanding Clause 7.1 makes you someone who can set a project up to succeed — not just execute tasks someone else defined. Quality Engineers and RA professionals who can write and manage a Quality Plan, connect it to design controls and risk management, and keep it updated through a real development cycle are genuinely hard to find at the early-career level. Most companies have people who know how to fill out forms; far fewer have people who understand *why* the forms exist and how they fit together. When you internalize Clause 7.1, you start thinking like someone who owns the system, not just someone who works inside it — and that's exactly the mindset that gets you promoted.
