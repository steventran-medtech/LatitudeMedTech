# ISO 13485:2016 — Clause 4.2.5: Records Control

# ISO 13485:2016 Clause 4.2.5: Records Control

*Educational content only. Always refer to the actual ISO 13485:2016 standard for official requirements.*

---

## What This Clause Is About

Medical device companies generate enormous amounts of evidence — test results, training logs, complaint records, audit findings — and regulators need to trust that evidence is real, complete, and findable. Clause 4.2.5 sets the rules for how your company manages that evidence so records don't get lost, altered, or destroyed before they should be. Without a solid records control system, you can't prove your quality system actually works.

---

## What It Requires (The Essentials)

**1. Records must be legible, identifiable, and retrievable.**
A record no one can find or read is useless. Whether your records are paper or electronic, they need to be organized so you can pull the right one during an audit — quickly.

**2. You must define retention periods.**
How long do you keep a device history record? A training log? ISO 13485 requires that you document your retention periods. The standard also tells you that records related to a medical device must be kept for at least the lifetime of the device or the period specified by regulation (whichever is longer) — and for a minimum of two years from product release.

**3. Records must be protected from damage, loss, and deterioration.**
This means physical protection (no storing paper records in a leaky basement) and logical protection (backups for electronic systems, access controls so records can't be casually deleted or edited).

**4. You need a procedure that covers all of this.**
The clause requires a documented procedure — written rules your team actually follows — that addresses identification, storage, protection, retrieval, retention, and disposal of records.

**5. Records cannot be altered without traceability.**
If something needs to be corrected, you can't just white it out or delete the original. Corrections must be traceable — who changed what, and when.

## What This Looks Like in Practice

Imagine a 60-person orthopedic implant company in Memphis. Their Quality Manager, Diane, owns the Records Control Procedure (SOP-QA-007). Here's how records control plays out day-to-day.

Every time a lot of bone screws is manufactured and released, the production team generates a **Device History Record (DHR)** — a package of documents proving that lot was made according to spec. It includes batch records, inspection results, and the release signature from QA. Once finalized, this DHR gets scanned and uploaded to their document management software (they use Greenlight Guru), filed under the product number and lot number, with a retention flag set for 15 years based on their procedure.

The paper originals go into labeled binders in a locked filing room. Access is logged.

When an auditor from a notified body visits and asks for the DHR for a specific lot shipped two years ago, the QA Specialist — let's call him Marcus — can pull the electronic record in under three minutes and produce the paper backup if needed. That's records control working.

Now here's the less glamorous part: Last year, a technician noticed a transcription error on a completed batch record. Under their procedure, she drew a single line through the error, wrote the correction next to it, initialed it, and dated it. She did not use correction fluid. She did not erase. That correction is now traceable. If someone finds it during an audit five years from now, they can see exactly what happened.

## Common Mistakes and Audit Findings

**1. Retention periods aren't defined — or defined inconsistently.**
Companies often have a general statement like "keep records for 5 years" but haven't mapped out specific retention rules for each record type. An auditor will ask: what's your retention period for supplier qualification records? If you can't answer with a documented source, that's a finding.

**2. Electronic records don't have adequate backup or access controls.**
Storing critical records in a shared drive folder with no backup schedule and no restrictions on who can delete files is a real and common problem. Regulators want to see that electronic records are protected from loss and unauthorized modification.

**3. Records are incomplete at the time of creation.**
A training record signed weeks after the training actually happened, or a batch record with blank fields filled in later, raises serious data integrity concerns. Records should be created contemporaneously — at the time the activity occurs.

**4. Corrections are made improperly.**
White-out on a GMP record is an immediate red flag. So is a single strikethrough with no initials or date. Early-career professionals often don't realize how closely auditors scrutinize corrections — it's a quick way to spot data integrity problems.

## Key Terms to Know

**Record** — Evidence that something happened. Unlike documents (which tell people what to do), records prove that something was done. A completed inspection form is a record.

**Device History Record (DHR)** — The collection of records showing that a specific lot or unit of a medical device was manufactured according to its Device Master Record.

**Retention Period** — How long a record must be kept before it can be legally and procedurally disposed of.

**Legibility** — The record can be read and understood. Faded ink, illegible handwriting, or corrupted files all fail this requirement.

**Retrievability** — The ability to find and access a record when you need it. Good filing systems, clear naming conventions, and functional search tools all support this.

**Data Integrity** — The assurance that records are accurate, complete, and unaltered (or that any alterations are fully traceable). This is a major regulatory focus.

**Contemporaneous** — Created at the time the activity happened, not reconstructed from memory later.

**Audit Trail** — A secure, time-stamped record of who accessed, created, or modified an electronic record. Required in validated electronic systems.

## Check Your Understanding

**1. What is the minimum retention period ISO 13485:2016 specifies for medical device records?**
*Answer: At least the lifetime of the device or the period required by applicable regulatory requirements, with a minimum of two years from the date of product release.*

**2. What's the difference between a document and a record?**
*Answer: A document tells people what to do (procedures, work instructions). A record is evidence that something was done (completed forms, signed logs, test results).*

**3. A technician makes an error on a paper batch record. What is the correct way to handle this?**
*Answer: Draw a single line through the error so the original is still readable, write the correction, and add initials and date. Never use correction fluid or erase.*

**4. Scenario: You're preparing for an ISO 13485 audit and an auditor asks to see the DHR for a lot shipped 18 months ago. You search your system and can't find it. What's the problem, and what clause does it relate to?**
*Answer: The record either wasn't retained properly or the retrieval system is inadequate — both are failures under Clause 4.2.5. The company may also be out of compliance with the minimum retention requirement.*

**5. Scenario: Your company stores all quality records on a shared network drive. Anyone in the company can access, edit, or delete any file. Is this acceptable under ISO 13485:2016? Why or why not?**
*Answer: No. Clause 4.2.5 requires that records be protected from damage, loss, and deterioration. A system with no access controls or backup fails to protect records from unauthorized modification or accidental deletion.*

## How This Connects to Your Career

Records control sounds administrative, but it sits at the intersection of everything regulators actually care about: can you prove your product is safe? Early-career professionals who understand this clause can walk into an audit situation, find what the auditor needs, and demonstrate that their company's data has integrity. That makes you an asset, not a liability, in an audit room. More practically, QA roles at every level involve creating and managing records daily — knowing the rules around corrections, retention, and access controls keeps you from creating the very findings that cost companies months of remediation work.
