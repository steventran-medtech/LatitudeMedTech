# ISO 13485:2016 — Clause 4.2.5: Records Control

## What This Clause Is About

Every medical device company generates records — test results, training logs, complaint files, audit reports — and those records only have value if you can find them, trust them, and keep them for as long as you need them. Clause 4.2.5 sets the rules for how your organization controls those records so they stay legible, retrievable, and protected from loss or damage throughout their required retention period.

---

## What It Requires (The Essentials)

**1. Records must be established and maintained to provide evidence of conformity.**
This means a record isn't just nice to have — it's proof. Proof that a product passed inspection, that an employee completed training, that a supplier was qualified. Without the record, the activity effectively didn't happen from a regulatory standpoint.

**2. Records must remain legible, readily identifiable, and retrievable.**
A blurry photocopy in an unlabeled box doesn't meet this requirement. Whether your records are paper or electronic, anyone who needs them should be able to find the right record quickly and read it clearly.

**3. You must define and document your controls in a procedure.**
The standard requires a documented procedure that covers how records are identified, stored, protected, retrieved, retained, and disposed of. These six controls are often called the "six controls of records."

**4. Retention times must be defined.**
You need to specify how long each type of record is kept. The standard requires that records be kept for at least the lifetime of the device as defined by the organization, or a minimum of two years from the device release date — but many regulations (FDA, EU MDR) require longer periods, so you always check applicable regulations on top of the standard.

**5. Records must be protected from deterioration and loss.**
This applies to both paper (moisture, fire, pests) and electronic records (system crashes, accidental deletion, cybersecurity). Backups and access controls aren't optional extras — they're part of this clause.

## What This Looks Like in Practice

Picture a 60-person Class II device company making infusion pumps. Their QMS records include DHRs (Device History Records — the paper trail showing each specific unit was built correctly), complaint files, CAPA records, supplier audit reports, and employee training records.

The QA Manager owns a procedure called **SOP-0012: Control of Quality Records**. That document lists every record type the company generates, assigns a retention period to each, and specifies where each record lives — either a physical filing cabinet in the QA office or a folder structure in their eQMS (electronic Quality Management System) called MasterControl.

When a manufacturing technician completes a batch record, they sign and date it, then hand it to the Document Control Specialist, who scans it, names it according to the file naming convention in SOP-0012, and uploads it to MasterControl under the corresponding lot number. The original paper copy goes into a labeled binder in a locked, climate-controlled room.

The retention schedule in SOP-0012 says complaint records are kept for the useful life of the device plus two years (to align with FDA 21 CFR Part 820 requirements), while training records are kept for five years after an employee leaves. When a record hits its disposal date, the Document Control Specialist logs it in a **Record Disposal Log**, gets QA Manager approval, and either shreds paper records or archives electronic ones per their IT policy.

During their last notified body audit, the auditor asked to see three specific complaint records from 18 months ago. The Document Control Specialist pulled all three within four minutes using MasterControl's search function. That's what "readily retrievable" looks like in real life.

## Common Mistakes and Audit Findings

**1. No defined retention periods for specific record types.**
Having a procedure that says "records will be retained per regulatory requirements" without actually listing the periods is a common finding. Auditors want to see a retention schedule — a table or list that tells you exactly how long each record type is kept.

**2. Electronic records without adequate backup or access controls.**
Teams store records in a shared drive on someone's laptop, or in a folder with no backup and no access restrictions. If that laptop dies, those records are gone. Auditors and FDA investigators will ask about your backup procedures and who has edit access to records.

**3. Illegible or altered records without proper correction methods.**
When someone makes an error on a paper record, they scratch it out and write over it — sometimes making the original entry unreadable. The correct method is a single line through the error, the correction written next to it, initialed and dated, with the reason for the change noted. Covering entries with correction fluid (white-out) is a red flag.

**4. Records that exist but can't be found in a reasonable time.**
Having records isn't enough if no one can locate them. During audits, if it takes 45 minutes to find a record that should be easily accessible, that's a finding against retrievability — even if the record exists.

## Key Terms to Know

**Record:** Documented evidence that something happened. Unlike a document (which gives instructions), a record captures what actually occurred.

**DHR (Device History Record):** The complete record showing that a specific device unit or lot was manufactured according to its Device Master Record. Required by FDA 21 CFR Part 820.

**Retention Period:** How long a record must be kept before it can be disposed of.

**Legibility:** The record can be clearly read. Faded ink, blurry scans, or overwritten entries fail this test.

**Retrievability:** The record can be located and accessed within a reasonable time when needed.

**eQMS (Electronic Quality Management System):** Software used to manage and store QMS documents and records electronically (examples: MasterControl, Veeva, Greenlight Guru).

**Record Disposal:** The controlled process of destroying or archiving records that have reached the end of their retention period, with documentation that disposal occurred.

## Check Your Understanding

**1. What is the difference between a document and a record?**
A document provides instructions or requirements (like an SOP). A record provides evidence that something happened (like a completed inspection form).

**2. What are the six controls your records procedure must address?**
Identification, storage, protection, retrieval, retention, and disposition of records.

**3. A technician makes a mistake filling out a batch record and uses correction fluid to cover it. What's wrong with this?**
Correction fluid makes the original entry unreadable, which is not acceptable. The correct method is a single line through the error, with the correction, initials, date, and reason written nearby.

**4. Scenario: An auditor asks to see training records for an employee who left the company three years ago. Your procedure says training records are kept for five years after departure. You can't find the records. What clause does this implicate, and what's the likely finding?**
This implicates Clause 4.2.5. The likely finding is a nonconformity related to retrievability and/or records retention — the records should exist and be accessible per your own procedure.

**5. Scenario: Your company stores all complaint records in a folder on the QA Manager's personal work laptop with no backup. Is this adequate under 4.2.5? Why or why not?**
No. This does not adequately protect records from deterioration or loss. A single point of failure with no backup puts required records at risk. The company needs a backup system and appropriate access controls.

## How This Connects to Your Career

Records control is one of the first things early-career QA professionals own in a real job — you'll be filing DHRs, maintaining training logs, managing retention schedules, and responding when an auditor or investigator asks for a specific record. Companies that get cited for records issues during FDA inspections or notified body audits suffer real consequences: warning letters, audit failures, delayed product launches. When you understand Clause 4.2.5 well enough to build a clean retention schedule, set up a retrievable filing system, and train your team on proper record corrections, you become the person who prevents those problems — and that makes you visibly valuable from your first year on the job.
