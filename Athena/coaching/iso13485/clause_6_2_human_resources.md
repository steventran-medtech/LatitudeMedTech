# The Untrained Technician Who Built 847 Defective Pacemaker Leads

*ISO 13485:2016 · Clause 6.2: Human Resources*

---

## The Incident

In 2019, FDA investigators cited a mid-size cardiac device manufacturer during a routine inspection of their assembly operations. The company produced implantable pacemaker leads — thin, insulated wires that carry electrical signals from the pacemaker to the heart muscle. Inspectors found that multiple assembly technicians performing critical crimping and insulation stripping operations had no documented evidence of completed training. Worse, one technician had been independently performing these tasks for over four months before anyone checked whether they'd finished the qualification process. The result: 847 leads assembled during that window had to be quarantined and evaluated for potential release of incompletely verified product.

The FDA issued a 483 observation citing the company's failure to ensure personnel performing work affecting product quality were adequately trained and that competency was verified before independent work began. The company faced a Class II recall evaluation, corrective action costs exceeding $2M, and a six-month production slowdown. This wasn't a training program that didn't exist — the program existed. It just wasn't being enforced or tracked at the operator level.

## What ISO 13485:2016 §6.2 Actually Requires

Clause 6.2 covers human resources — specifically, making sure the right people with the right skills are doing the right jobs. Here's what you actually need to do:

1. **Define competency requirements.** For every role that affects product quality or the QMS, document what education, training, skills, and experience are needed before someone works independently.

2. **Train people and verify it worked.** Training isn't just attending a class. You have to confirm the person can actually do the job — through testing, observation, or supervised work periods.

3. **Keep training records.** Document who was trained, on what, when, and what the outcome was. These records must be maintained.

4. **Connect training to awareness.** Employees need to understand *why* their job matters — how it links to product quality and patient safety. This isn't a motivational speech; it's a documented element.

5. **Identify and close gaps.** When processes change, when people move roles, or when problems surface, you must reassess whether competency requirements are still being met.

## What Went Wrong — Clause Analysis

The pacemaker lead manufacturer had a training procedure. That's not the gap. The gap was in **competency verification and record control**.

Clause 6.2 doesn't just require training — it requires that the organization *evaluate the effectiveness* of actions taken and retain records as evidence. What inspectors found was a disconnect between the training procedure (which required sign-off by a supervisor after observed proficiency) and actual practice on the floor, where supervisors were signing off based on time-on-the-job rather than demonstrated competency.

The specific failure: no objective evidence that the technician in question had been evaluated against the defined competency criteria for crimping operations. The training record existed — but it documented attendance at an orientation session, not verification of skill.

This is a classic compliance gap. The QMS said the right things. The shop floor did something different. And nobody was auditing the gap between the two until an FDA investigator walked in.

## A Second Scenario

NeuroSync Devices is a 60-person startup making implantable neurostimulators for chronic pain management. They've just hired three new manufacturing engineers to support a production scale-up ahead of their 510(k) clearance.

Under time pressure, the QA manager — fresh in the role herself — decides the new hires can start building prototype units after a one-day walkthrough with a senior engineer. No competency matrix exists for the role. No formal evaluation is conducted. Training records are emails in someone's inbox.

Six weeks later, during an internal audit, a QA engineer named Marcus pulls the training files for the engineering team. He finds three people doing work that directly affects device performance with no documented competency baseline. He escalates.

Here's what Marcus does right: He doesn't just flag it as a documentation problem. He maps each person's role against the process steps that affect critical characteristics — electrode placement tolerances, solder joint inspection. He drafts a competency matrix for those roles, defines what "competent" looks like (pass a written test *and* supervised build of three acceptable units), and works with the QA manager to run all three engineers through the evaluation retroactively before any prototype units leave the building.

He also opens a CAPA to address why the onboarding process didn't trigger the competency requirement — because the gap isn't those three people, it's the system that allowed it to happen.

## What Auditors Look For

These are the specific things FDA investigators and notified body auditors consistently flag under Clause 6.2:

1. **"Training records were available, but no evidence of competency evaluation was present."** Attendance records aren't enough. Auditors want to see that performance was assessed against defined criteria.

2. **"No documented competency requirements for [specific role]."** If you can't show a job description or role matrix that defines required education, experience, and skills, you have a gap — even if everyone in the role is highly qualified.

3. **"Personnel performing critical operations were found to be in training status with no documented supervision."** On-the-job trainees doing unsupervised work on released product is a recurring 483 observation.

4. **"Training records for [process change] were not updated to reflect the revised procedure."** When you revise an SOP, everyone affected needs documented retraining. Auditors cross-reference document revision dates against training logs.

## Key Terms

**Competency** — The demonstrated ability to apply knowledge and skills to perform a task correctly. Not the same as "has been trained." *Example: A sterile fill technician who has attended gowning training but hasn't been observed and approved by a supervisor is trained, not yet competent.*

**Competency Matrix** — A document that maps roles to required skills and tracks who has been verified competent in each area. *Example: A matrix for catheter assembly listing each operator, each critical task, and pass/fail status.*

**Training Record** — Documented evidence that training occurred and competency was evaluated. Must identify the person, the content, the date, and the outcome. *Example: A signed form showing a technician passed a torque wrench calibration quiz and a supervised practical.*

**Effectiveness Evaluation** — Confirmation that training actually changed behavior or knowledge, not just that it happened. *Example: A post-training test score or a supervisor observation checklist completed 30 days after training.*

**Role-Based Training** — Training tied to a specific job function rather than general company orientation. *Example: Only QC inspectors receive training on the visual inspection SOP; assembly technicians do not.*

**CAPA (Corrective and Preventive Action)** — A formal process for identifying, investigating, and fixing quality problems — including systemic training failures. *Example: Opening a CAPA when an audit reveals multiple roles lack competency documentation, rather than just fixing the individual records.*

**Qualification Period** — A supervised period before an employee works independently. *Example: A new device assembler who must complete 10 supervised builds before being cleared for unassisted production.*

## Check Your Understanding

**1. In the pacemaker lead case, the company had a training procedure. Why were they still cited?**
*Answer: Because having a procedure isn't compliance. The procedure required supervisor sign-off based on demonstrated proficiency, but supervisors were approving based on time served rather than evaluated competency. No objective evidence existed that the defined criteria were actually met.*

**2. Marcus at NeuroSync found three engineers without competency records. He treats it as a systemic issue, not just a paperwork fix. Why does that matter?**
*Answer: If he only fixed the three records, the root cause — an onboarding process that didn't trigger competency requirements — would produce the same problem with the next hire. A CAPA addresses the system. Fixing individual records only addresses the symptom.*

**3. What is the difference between training effectiveness and training completion?**
*Answer: Completion means the person attended or finished the training activity. Effectiveness means the training achieved its intended result — the person can now perform the task correctly. ISO 13485 requires you to evaluate effectiveness, not just document completion.*

**4. A process engineer updates an SOP for a soldering operation. What Clause 6.2 obligation does that trigger?**
*Answer: Everyone performing that operation must be retrained on the revised SOP, and new training records must document that retraining occurred. Auditors will check revision dates against training logs.*

## Career Impact

Early-career QA professionals often treat Clause 6.2 as an HR problem — someone else's paperwork. That's a career-limiting view. Human resources competency is the operational backbone of your QMS. When a product escapes with a defect, the first question investigators ask is who built it and whether they were qualified. If you own the training program — if you built the competency matrix, closed the gaps before the audit, and connected retraining to document revision — you become the person in the room who can answer that question confidently. That's the difference between a company that survives an FDA inspection and one that spends the next six months in remediation. Learning to manage this clause well, early, makes you visibly competent to the people deciding who gets more responsibility.
