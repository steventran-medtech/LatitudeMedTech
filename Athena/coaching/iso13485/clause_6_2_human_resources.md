# ISO 13485:2016 — Clause 6.2: Human Resources

# ISO 13485:2016 Clause 6.2: Human Resources

*Educational content only. Always refer to the official ISO 13485:2016 standard for authoritative requirements.*

---

## What This Clause Is About

Medical devices can hurt people if the people building, testing, or overseeing them don't know what they're doing. Clause 6.2 requires companies to make sure every person doing work that affects product quality is actually qualified to do it — and to prove that qualification with records. It closes the gap between "we hired someone" and "we know they're competent."

---

## What It Requires (The Essentials)

**1. Define what competence looks like for each role.**
Before you can say someone is qualified, you need to know what qualified means. The standard requires companies to determine the necessary competence for personnel whose work affects product quality. This means documenting the education, training, skills, and experience required for each relevant role.

**2. Evaluate whether people actually have that competence.**
Hiring someone with the right degree isn't enough. You need to verify they can actually do the job. This evaluation happens at onboarding, when roles change, and after new processes are introduced.

**3. Address gaps.**
If someone isn't fully competent yet, you need a plan — training, mentoring, reassignment, whatever is appropriate. Then you evaluate whether that action actually worked.

**4. Keep records.**
This is non-negotiable. You need documented evidence of education, training, skills, and experience. No records means no proof. No proof means a finding.

**5. Make sure people understand why their work matters.**
Employees need to be aware of how their role contributes to meeting quality objectives and what happens when quality requirements aren't met. This isn't optional feel-good content — it's explicitly required.

## What This Looks Like in Practice

Imagine a 40-person Class II device company that makes orthopedic surgical instruments. Here's how their HR and quality team handles Clause 6.2:

**Job Descriptions with Competency Criteria**
Every role has a formal job description that lists required education, experience, and skills. The QA Specialist role, for example, requires a bachelor's degree in a science or engineering field, two years of industry experience, and familiarity with 21 CFR Part 820. These aren't just HR documents — they feed directly into the QMS.

**Training Plans and the Training Matrix**
The Quality Manager maintains a *Training Matrix* — a spreadsheet that maps every role to the procedures and training modules they're required to complete. A new Manufacturing Technician gets assigned a training plan on day one: SOPs for assembly, work instructions for inspection, and a general QMS awareness module. Their supervisor signs off on each item when complete.

**Competency Evaluations**
For roles where reading a procedure isn't enough — like soldering, sterile technique, or final inspection — the team uses a *Practical Skills Assessment*. A trainer watches the employee perform the task and documents the result. This is the difference between "trained" and "competent."

**Training Records in the Document Control System**
All completed training is logged in their eQMS (they use a system called MasterControl, though any document control system works). Each employee has a training record that QA can pull during an audit in under five minutes. Auditors love this. Missing records are findings.

**Annual Awareness Review**
Once a year during team meetings, the Quality Manager reviews the company's quality objectives, any significant nonconformances from the year, and how each department's work ties into product quality. This satisfies the "awareness" requirement and keeps quality from feeling abstract.

## Common Mistakes and Audit Findings

**1. Training records exist but competency evaluations don't.**
Companies often document that someone *attended* training but never verify the person actually learned anything. "Attended" is not the same as "competent." Auditors distinguish between these, and so should you.

**2. Job descriptions haven't been updated after a role changed.**
When someone takes on new responsibilities — especially in small companies where people wear many hats — the documented competency requirements often don't get updated. You end up with records that describe a job that doesn't exist anymore.

**3. No records for long-tenured employees.**
Companies sometimes have employees who've been there 15 years and everyone "knows" they're competent. But if there's no documentation, there's no evidence. Auditors don't take institutional memory as proof.

**4. Training completed but effectiveness never verified.**
The standard implies that when you take action to address a competency gap, you need to verify it worked. Sending someone to a webinar and checking a box doesn't close the loop. Did it actually improve their performance? You need something — a test score, a re-evaluation, a supervisor sign-off — to show it did.

## Key Terms to Know

**Competence:** The demonstrated ability to apply knowledge and skills to achieve intended results. Not just having a credential — actually being able to do the work.

**Training Matrix (or Competency Matrix):** A document that maps job roles to required training and qualifications. Tells you who needs to know what.

**Training Record:** Documented evidence that a person completed specific training. The QMS artifact that proves training happened.

**Effectiveness Evaluation:** A check to confirm that training or corrective action actually improved competency. The "did it work?" step.

**Awareness:** Understanding of how one's work relates to quality objectives and what the consequences of not meeting requirements are. Distinct from technical competency.

**eQMS:** Electronic Quality Management System. Software used to manage QMS documents, records, and processes (e.g., MasterControl, Veeva, Greenlight Guru).

**Onboarding Training:** Structured training given to new employees to bring them up to the required competence level for their role before they work independently.

## Check Your Understanding

**1. What four types of qualification does ISO 13485 say you should consider when determining competence?**
*Answer: Education, training, skills, and experience.*

**2. Why isn't a signed attendance sheet enough to satisfy the competency requirement?**
*Answer: Attendance shows someone was present for training, but not that they understood it or can apply it. Competency requires evidence of demonstrated ability, not just participation.*

**3. Scenario: A machinist has worked at your company for 12 years and is excellent at her job. During an audit, the auditor asks for her training records and competency evaluations. Your training files only go back 5 years, and her onboarding records were lost in an office move. What's the problem, and what should you do going forward?**
*Answer: Missing records are a nonconformance — the company can't demonstrate documented evidence of her qualifications, even if her actual competence is not in doubt. Going forward, conduct and document a retrospective competency evaluation for her current role and maintain those records in the QMS.*

**4. What does the "awareness" requirement in Clause 6.2 actually require employees to understand?**
*Answer: How their work contributes to achieving quality objectives, and the consequences — for product quality and potentially patient safety — of not following quality requirements.*

**5. Scenario: Your company hires a new Regulatory Affairs Specialist who has a strong pharma background but limited medical device experience. After three months, she completes a medical device regulations training course. What step do companies often skip, and why does it matter?**
*Answer: Evaluating the effectiveness of the training. Completing the course doesn't confirm she can apply the knowledge to her specific role. A follow-up evaluation — a practical test, a supervisor assessment, or observed performance on a real submission — is needed to close the loop.*
