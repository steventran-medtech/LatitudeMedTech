# The QA Manager Who Reported to Sales — And the 483 That Followed

*ISO 13485:2016 · Clause 5.5: Responsibility and Authority*

---

## The Incident

In 2019, FDA investigators inspected a mid-sized orthopedic device manufacturer in the Southeast — roughly 85 employees, making powered surgical instruments under a 510(k) clearance. During the inspection, investigators discovered that the company's Quality Manager reported directly to the Vice President of Sales. On paper, this looked like a reporting structure decision. In practice, it meant the person responsible for product release decisions was organizationally subordinate to the person whose compensation depended on shipping product on time.

The 483 observation was blunt: the firm had not established independence of the quality function from operations and commercial activities. The Quality Manager had approved a device lot for distribution despite open corrective actions related to sterilization validation. When investigators asked why, the answer traced back to pressure from the sales team facing a hospital contract deadline.

The consequence: a voluntary recall of the affected lot, an FDA warning letter citing inadequate management responsibility, and an 18-month consent decree that cost the company an estimated $4.2 million in remediation. The root cause wasn't a procedure failure — it was an org chart.

## What ISO 13485:2016 §5.5 Actually Requires

Clause 5.5 covers three things you have to get right. *(Always verify requirements against the actual ISO 13485:2016 standard — this is a coaching summary, not a compliance substitute.)*

**1. Define responsibilities and authorities in writing.** Every role that touches quality — not just the QA team — needs a documented description of what they're responsible for and what decisions they're authorized to make. Job descriptions, org charts, and procedure ownership all count.

**2. Communicate those responsibilities.** Writing them down isn't enough. People in the organization need to know what their role requires and where it connects to quality outcomes.

**3. Appoint a Management Representative.** This is a specific individual from management — not a title you give someone in addition to 12 other jobs without any authority. This person has three accountable duties: ensuring the QMS is established and maintained, reporting QMS performance to top management, and ensuring quality awareness throughout the organization.

**4. Protect the quality function's independence.** The Management Representative must have the organizational authority to actually stop a shipment, escalate a finding, or reject a supplier — without needing sign-off from someone whose bonus depends on the opposite decision.

## What Went Wrong — Clause Analysis

The orthopedic company's failure maps directly to §5.5.2, which requires that the Management Representative have the authority and responsibility to ensure QMS effectiveness — independent of commercial pressures.

The gap wasn't that no one had written "QA Manager" on an org chart. The gap was structural: the written responsibilities said the QA Manager had release authority, but the reporting line undermined that authority in every real-world decision. When the QA Manager approved a lot with open corrective actions, the organizational pressure created by reporting to Sales made that decision almost inevitable.

An auditor looking at this would ask: "Who does the Quality function report to, and does that relationship compromise their ability to make independent quality decisions?" Here, the answer was yes, and the documentation actually confirmed it — the org chart showed the QA Manager three levels below the VP of Sales with no dotted line to the CEO or a quality committee.

The warning letter specifically cited failure to define and communicate the authority of the Management Representative in a way that allowed them to fulfill §5.5.2 requirements. That's a precise clause failure, not a vague systems problem.

## A Second Scenario

MedCore Systems is a 55-person contract manufacturer in Minnesota making infusion pumps for an OEM client. Their QMS was built three years ago during their ISO 13485 certification push, and the org chart hasn't been updated since.

When their original Quality Director left the company, the CEO handed her responsibilities to the Engineering Manager — informally, without updating job descriptions, procedure ownership records, or the QMS manual. The Engineering Manager is sharp and well-intentioned, but his job description still says he's responsible for design validation and supplier tooling. It says nothing about QMS performance reporting, CAPA oversight, or management review.

A year later, a notified body auditor asks to see the Management Representative appointment letter and the current org chart. The appointment letter names the departed Quality Director. The org chart shows the Engineering Manager as "Acting QA Lead" in pencil — literally. The auditor issues a major nonconformance: the Management Representative role is not formally assigned, and the person performing the function lacks documented authority.

The fix isn't complicated, but it requires the right QA engineer to push it through: update the appointment letter with CEO signature, revise the Engineering Manager's job description to include all §5.5.2 responsibilities, update the org chart and QMS manual, and present the changes at the next management review as a documented correction. Done right, this takes two weeks. Ignored, it delays their surveillance audit and risks suspension of their certificate.

## What Auditors Look For

**1. Org chart doesn't match the QMS manual.** FDA investigators and notified body auditors routinely compare the org chart in the QMS manual against current HR records. If the names or reporting lines don't match, that's a finding.

**2. Management Representative has no documented appointment.** The role must be formally assigned — a memo, a job description, or a procedure — with the signature of top management. An informal arrangement doesn't satisfy §5.5.2.

**3. QA reports to Operations or Sales.** This structural conflict is a frequent 483 trigger. The observation usually reads: "The firm's quality function does not have sufficient independence from production/commercial activities to ensure unbiased quality decisions."

**4. Responsibilities defined but not communicated.** Having a great job description in an HR folder no one has read doesn't count. Auditors ask employees what they're responsible for. When the answer doesn't match the documented role, that's a gap in §5.5.1 communication.

## Key Terms

**Management Representative** — A specific person from management formally designated to own the QMS. Example: at a 30-person catheter manufacturer, this is typically the Quality Director, formally appointed in writing by the CEO.

**Responsibility** — What a role is accountable for achieving. Example: the Production Supervisor is responsible for ensuring device builds follow the Device History Record.

**Authority** — The power to take action or make decisions within a defined scope. Example: the QA Manager has authority to place a product hold without VP approval.

**Organizational Independence** — The quality function's ability to make decisions free from commercial or operational pressure. Example: QA reports to the CEO, not to the VP of Manufacturing.

**QMS Manual** — A document describing the scope and structure of the QMS, including roles and their relationships. Example: a spinal implant company's QMS manual defines who owns each procedure and their reporting structure.

**Reporting Structure** — The formal hierarchy that defines who a role reports to. Example: if the Regulatory Affairs Manager reports to Legal rather than Quality, that affects which decisions they can escalate independently.

**Documented Job Description** — A written record of a role's responsibilities, authority, and qualifications. Must be current and controlled under your document control procedure.

## Check Your Understanding

**1. In the orthopedic company case, what was the root cause of the 483 observation?**
The Quality Manager's reporting structure — directly under the VP of Sales — compromised the independence of quality decision-making. This is a §5.5.2 failure.

**2. MedCore's Engineering Manager is doing QA work but his job description doesn't say so. Why is this a problem under §5.5?**
Clause 5.5.1 requires that responsibilities and authorities be defined and documented. If the person performing Management Representative functions isn't formally appointed with documented authority, the role doesn't satisfy §5.5.2 — regardless of how competently the work is being done.

**3. Your company just promoted someone to Quality Director. What three documents need to be updated to ensure §5.5 compliance?**
At minimum: (1) the Management Representative appointment letter, (2) the org chart in the QMS manual, and (3) the Quality Director's job description reflecting all §5.5.2 responsibilities.

**4. An auditor asks your CAPA Coordinator what she's authorized to do when a supplier ships nonconforming material. She says, "I usually ask my manager." Is this a problem?**
Yes. It suggests her documented authority isn't clear or hasn't been effectively communicated — a gap in §5.5.1.

## Career Impact

Early in your career, you'll be tempted to treat org charts and job descriptions as HR paperwork. They're not — they're the architecture of your QMS. When responsibilities are blurry or authority is undermined by reporting structure, quality decisions get politicized. The QA professionals who understand this clause deeply are the ones who can walk into a management review and say, "Our current structure creates a compliance risk, and here's how to fix it" — with documentation to back it up. That kind of structural thinking is what separates a QA engineer who processes paperwork from one who actually protects patients and earns a seat at the leadership table.
