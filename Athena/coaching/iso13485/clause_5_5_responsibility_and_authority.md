# ISO 13485:2016 — Clause 5.5: Responsibility and Authority

# ISO 13485:2016 Clause 5.5: Responsibility and Authority

*Educational content for learning purposes only. Always refer to the actual ISO 13485:2016 standard for official requirements.*

---

## What This Clause Is About

When something goes wrong in a medical device company — a nonconforming product ships, a CAPA sits unresolved for months, a design change bypasses review — the root cause is often the same: nobody was clearly in charge. Clause 5.5 exists to prevent that. It requires top management to define who is responsible for what, make sure people have the authority to actually do their jobs, and communicate that structure throughout the organization.

---

## What It Requires (The Essentials)

**1. Define responsibilities and authorities — in writing.**
Top management must document who is responsible for what. "We all know what each other does" is not acceptable. If it's not documented, it doesn't exist as far as an auditor is concerned.

**2. Communicate those responsibilities throughout the organization.**
People need to know their own responsibilities *and* understand how their role connects to others. A production technician who doesn't know that QA has the authority to stop a production line is a liability.

**3. Appoint a Management Representative.**
This is a specific role the standard requires. Someone in management — with a documented appointment — must be responsible for making sure the QMS is established, implemented, and maintained, and for reporting on QMS performance to top management. This person is your internal QMS champion.

**4. The Management Representative must have authority, not just a title.**
It's not enough to assign the title. That person needs real organizational authority to enforce QMS requirements across departments.

**5. Internal communication about the QMS must happen.**
Top management is responsible for making sure people across the organization are aware of how the QMS is performing and what their role in it is. This doesn't have to be elaborate — but it has to be deliberate.

## What This Looks Like in Practice

Acuity Surgical Devices is a 60-person company making laparoscopic instruments. Here's how they handle Clause 5.5:

The **Organizational Chart** (document QMS-ORG-001) is maintained by HR and reviewed annually. It shows every department, reporting lines, and role titles. But the org chart alone doesn't tell you who can approve a design change or who owns supplier qualification — so they pair it with a **Responsibility Matrix** (QMS-ORG-002), sometimes called a RACI chart. That document maps key QMS processes to role-level responsibilities: who is Responsible, Accountable, Consulted, and Informed.

The CEO signed a **Management Representative Appointment Letter** designating the VP of Quality as the Management Representative. That letter lives in the QMS and is referenced in the Quality Manual. The VP of Quality has a standing agenda item at monthly leadership meetings to report on CAPA status, audit findings, and quality metrics — that's how management review input gets surfaced.

For communication, Acuity runs a 30-minute "QMS Update" at their all-hands meeting each quarter. The VP of Quality walks through any major changes to procedures, open corrective actions that affect multiple departments, and any upcoming audits. It's not glamorous, but it satisfies the intent of the clause and creates a paper trail (meeting minutes are filed).

New employees receive a QMS orientation during onboarding that covers where to find procedures, who owns what process, and who to contact with a quality concern. The training record goes into the HR system.

## Common Mistakes and Audit Findings

**1. The org chart is outdated.**
Companies grow, people leave, roles change — and the org chart doesn't get updated. An auditor who finds a chart with departed employees or missing departments will note it immediately. This is a low-effort, high-visibility item. Keep it current.

**2. The Management Representative has the title but no real authority.**
If your Management Representative has to ask the COO's permission to put a product hold in place, something is structurally broken. Auditors will ask to see the appointment letter *and* will probe whether the role has operational teeth. Paper authority and real authority need to match.

**3. Responsibilities exist at the department level but not the role level.**
"Quality is responsible for document control" is too vague. Who in Quality? What exactly are they responsible for? When there's no role-level clarity, tasks fall through the cracks, especially during staff transitions.

**4. No evidence of internal QMS communication.**
This one catches people off guard. Auditors may ask: "How does top management communicate the importance of the QMS to employees?" If the answer is "we talk about it," you have a problem. You need records — meeting minutes, training logs, emails, whatever format you use — showing that communication actually happened.

## Key Terms to Know

**Top Management** — The person or group of people who directs and controls an organization at the highest level. In a small company, this might be the CEO and a few VPs.

**Management Representative** — A member of management formally appointed to be responsible for the QMS. Think of this person as the QMS owner and internal champion.

**Responsibility** — Who is assigned to do something. You are responsible for the task.

**Authority** — The power to make decisions or take actions. You can have responsibility without authority, which is a recipe for problems.

**Organizational Chart (Org Chart)** — A diagram showing the structure of an organization, reporting relationships, and role titles.

**RACI Matrix** — A tool that maps processes or tasks to roles: who is Responsible, Accountable, Consulted, and Informed. Useful for preventing "I thought someone else was handling that."

**Quality Management System (QMS)** — The set of policies, processes, and procedures a company uses to ensure its medical devices consistently meet customer and regulatory requirements.

## Check Your Understanding

**1. What is the purpose of appointing a Management Representative?**
To designate one person in management who is formally accountable for establishing, implementing, and maintaining the QMS, and for reporting on QMS performance to top management.

**2. Why isn't an org chart alone sufficient to satisfy Clause 5.5?**
An org chart shows reporting lines and titles, but it doesn't define who owns specific QMS processes or who has authority to make specific decisions. You need a supplementary document — like a RACI matrix or job descriptions — to fill that gap.

**3. Scenario: Your company just promoted someone internally, creating a new "Quality Systems Manager" role. What Clause 5.5-related actions should be taken?**
Update the org chart, update any RACI matrices or responsibility documents that reference affected roles, update job descriptions, and communicate the change internally. If the new role touches the Management Representative function, revisit the appointment letter.

**4. True or False: The Management Representative must be a dedicated full-time QA employee.**
False. The standard requires that this person be a member of management — but in smaller companies, the role is often held by someone who also carries other responsibilities, like a VP of Quality and Regulatory.

**5. Scenario: During a notified body audit, the auditor asks how employees learn about changes to quality procedures. Your company has no records of any internal communications about QMS updates. Is this a problem?**
Yes. Clause 5.5 requires that top management ensure the QMS is communicated throughout the organization. Without records — meeting minutes, training logs, email announcements — you cannot demonstrate this happened. Expect a nonconformity.

## How This Connects to Your Career

Early in your career, you'll probably be handed tasks without a lot of context about why they exist or who else is involved. Understanding Clause 5.5 gives you a framework for asking the right questions: Who owns this process? Do I have the authority to make this call, or do I need to escalate? Is this role-level responsibility documented somewhere? Those questions make you more effective immediately and signal to your manager that you understand how a QMS actually functions — not just the paperwork, but the organizational logic behind it. When you can walk into a gap (an undefined responsibility, an outdated org chart, a Management Representative with no real authority) and explain why it's a risk and how to fix it, you become someone companies want to keep.
