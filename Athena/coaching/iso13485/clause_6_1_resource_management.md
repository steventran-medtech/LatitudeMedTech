# ISO 13485:2016 — Clause 6.1: Resource Management

# ISO 13485:2016 Clause 6.1: Resource Management

*Educational content only. Always refer to the actual ISO 13485:2016 standard for official requirements.*

---

## What This Clause Is About

Medical device companies fail not because they lack good intentions, but because they don't have enough of the right resources — people, infrastructure, or working environment — to do quality work consistently. Clause 6.1 requires your company to figure out what resources it needs to run its Quality Management System (QMS) and actually provide them. Think of it as the standard holding leadership accountable for backing up their quality commitments with real investment.

---

## What It Requires (The Essentials)

**1. Determine what resources you need.**
This isn't guesswork. Your company must look at what it takes to establish, maintain, and continuously improve the QMS, and then identify the specific resources required to do that — people, equipment, facilities, software, and so on.

**2. Account for regulatory and customer requirements.**
Resources aren't just about keeping internal processes running. You need enough of the right resources to meet the requirements of applicable regulations (like FDA 21 CFR Part 820 or EU MDR) and to fulfill customer commitments. A three-person QA team trying to support a 200-SKU product line is a resource problem, and this clause is where that gets addressed.

**3. Provide the resources you've identified.**
Identifying needs isn't enough — management must actually provide them. This is a direct link to top management responsibility (Clause 5). If your gap analysis says you need a validation engineer and leadership says "not in the budget," that's a clause 6.1 risk.

**4. Maintain documented evidence.**
You need records showing that resource decisions were made and acted on. This typically shows up in management review outputs, budget approvals, headcount plans, and training records.

## What This Looks Like in Practice

Imagine a 75-person orthopedic implant company called Summit Surgical Devices. They manufacture bone screws and plates and sell into the U.S. and EU markets.

Every year, Summit's QA Manager, the VP of Operations, and the CFO sit down for an annual management review. One agenda item is always "Resource Assessment." The QA Manager comes prepared with a **Resource Needs Summary** — a simple one-page document listing current QA/RA headcount, open projects, upcoming audits, and known gaps.

This year's summary flags three things: (1) the company is launching a new product line requiring IQ/OQ/PQ validation work, but there's no dedicated validation engineer; (2) their calibration vendor is backlogged and several pieces of inspection equipment are overdue; and (3) the cleanroom HVAC system hasn't been serviced since Q1.

The management review minutes — a controlled document in their QMS — capture the discussion and the decisions: hire a contract validation engineer for Q3, escalate calibration scheduling to the facilities manager, and schedule HVAC service within 30 days. The CFO signs off on the contract engineer budget. These outputs feed into an **Action Item Log** with owners and due dates.

Six months later, when a notified body auditor reviews their management review records, they can trace the resource gap to the decision to the follow-up. That's exactly what clause 6.1 is designed to produce.

## Common Mistakes and Audit Findings

**1. Treating resource management as a checkbox, not a process.**
Many early-career QA professionals document a resource assessment once during initial certification and never update it. Auditors look for evidence that resource needs are reassessed when things change — new products, regulatory changes, headcount reductions. A static document with no revision history is a red flag.

**2. Confusing "we identified the gap" with "we addressed it."**
Noting in a management review that you're short-staffed in QA isn't compliance. The standard requires you to *provide* resources. If the same gap appears in three consecutive management reviews with no resolution, expect a finding.

**3. Forgetting about the work environment as a resource.**
Clause 6.1 connects to Clause 6.4, which covers work environment and contamination control. Early-career professionals often think of "resources" as people and money. Auditors also look at whether your physical environment — temperature controls, cleanliness, lighting for inspection tasks — is resourced appropriately for your device type.

**4. No documented link between resource decisions and quality outcomes.**
If you can't show an auditor *how* you decided what resources were needed and *what evidence* drove that decision, you have a documentation problem. Management review minutes that just say "resources are adequate" without supporting data will not satisfy a sharp auditor.

## Key Terms to Know

**QMS (Quality Management System):** The entire set of policies, processes, procedures, and records a company uses to ensure products are safe and effective.

**Management Review:** A formal meeting where top leadership evaluates the QMS — its performance, gaps, and what needs to change. Required by Clause 5.6.

**Infrastructure:** The physical and technical assets needed to run your processes — buildings, equipment, software, utilities.

**Work Environment:** The conditions under which work is performed, including temperature, cleanliness, lighting, and noise. Relevant when these conditions affect product quality.

**Resource Gap:** The difference between the resources your QMS needs and what's currently available.

**Continual Improvement:** An ongoing effort to enhance QMS performance over time — not a one-time fix. Resource management supports this by ensuring improvement activities are actually funded and staffed.

**Documented Evidence:** Records that prove a requirement was met. In clause 6.1 context: meeting minutes, action logs, budget approvals, org charts.

## Check Your Understanding

**1. What is the core obligation of Clause 6.1?**
*Answer:* Companies must determine what resources they need for the QMS and then actually provide those resources — not just identify gaps.

**2. Why does Clause 6.1 connect to top management responsibility?**
*Answer:* Providing resources requires authority over budgets and headcount — decisions only leadership can make. Management commitment (Clause 5) is meaningless without resource follow-through.

**3. Scenario: Your company's management review identifies that the QA team is understaffed. Leadership acknowledges the gap but takes no action. Is this a compliance issue?**
*Answer:* Yes. Identifying a resource need without providing it fails the requirement. If this pattern repeats across multiple reviews, it's strong evidence of a systemic nonconformance.

**4. Which of the following counts as a "resource" under Clause 6.1: (a) a calibrated torque wrench, (b) a trained inspector, (c) a temperature-controlled storage room, (d) all of the above?**
*Answer:* (d) All of the above. Resources include people, equipment, and the work environment.

**5. Scenario: A notified body auditor asks to see evidence that your company assessed resource needs in the past 12 months. What documents would you pull?**
*Answer:* Management review records (including the agenda, minutes, and outputs), any resource gap assessments or headcount plans, and the action item log showing how gaps were addressed.

## How This Connects to Your Career

Understanding Clause 6.1 makes you more than a document handler — it makes you someone who can talk to leadership about quality in terms they care about: headcount, budget, and risk. Early-career QA professionals who grasp resource management can contribute meaningfully to management reviews, flag resourcing problems before they become audit findings, and help build the business case for quality investments. Employers notice when junior staff can connect QMS requirements to operational decisions. That skill — translating regulatory requirements into plain business language — is one of the fastest ways to grow from an entry-level QA role into a position where you're influencing how the company operates.
