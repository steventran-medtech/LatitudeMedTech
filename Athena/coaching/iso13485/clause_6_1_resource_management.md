# ISO 13485:2016 — Clause 6.1: Resource Management

## What This Clause Is About

Every QMS runs on resources — people, equipment, infrastructure, and money. Clause 6.1 requires your company to figure out what resources it needs to run the QMS effectively, meet regulatory requirements, and satisfy customers, then actually provide those resources. The problem it solves is simple: a QMS on paper means nothing if the company hasn't committed the people, tools, and environment needed to make it work.

---

## What It Requires (The Essentials)

Clause 6.1 is short — intentionally so. It sets the framework, and the details land in subclauses 6.2 through 6.4. Here's what the clause itself requires you to understand:

**1. Determine what resources you need.**
Your company must identify the resources required to implement and maintain the QMS and continually improve its effectiveness. This isn't a one-time exercise — it's ongoing. As your products, processes, or regulations change, your resource needs change too.

**2. Meet customer and regulatory requirements.**
Resources aren't just about keeping your internal QMS ticking. They must be sufficient to ensure your devices meet customer expectations and applicable regulatory requirements — things like FDA 21 CFR Part 820, EU MDR, or other market-specific rules.

**3. Actually provide those resources.**
Identifying needs isn't enough. The standard requires that the organization *provide* the resources it determines are necessary. This is where management commitment becomes real — if leadership signs off on a QMS but refuses to staff it properly, that's a nonconformance waiting to happen.

## What This Looks Like in Practice

Imagine a 45-person company called Meridian Surgical that makes single-use laparoscopic tools. Their QA team consists of a QA Manager, two QA Engineers, and one Regulatory Affairs Specialist.

Each year, during the **Management Review** meeting (usually held in Q4), the QA Manager presents a **Resource Assessment** — an internal document that maps current QMS activities against available headcount, equipment, and budget. She pulls data from complaint trends, audit schedules, new product pipelines, and any upcoming regulatory changes (say, a transition deadline for a new EU MDR technical file).

The assessment might flag something like: "We're launching two new product lines next year and taking on a EU MDR submission. Our current team cannot handle complaint investigations, design reviews, and the MDR submission simultaneously without adding capacity."

That document goes to the VP of Operations and CFO as a formal input. Leadership then decides — hire a contract RA consultant, bring on a junior QA associate, or adjust the product launch timeline. Whatever they decide, it's documented in the **Management Review Minutes**, which become a QMS record.

The key document chain here:
- Resource Assessment (input)
- Management Review Minutes (decision record)
- Job descriptions and org chart (evidence of defined roles)
- Training records (evidence that hired/assigned people are actually qualified)

This is what auditors look for — not just that you identified a need, but that you acted on it and documented the loop closing.

## Common Mistakes and Audit Findings

**1. Treating resource planning as a formality.**
Many teams fill out a resource section in the management review template without real analysis. An auditor will ask: "How did you determine this staffing level was adequate?" If the answer is essentially "we've always had this many people," that's a weak response and can lead to an observation or finding.

**2. No documented link between resource gaps and corrective actions.**
If your management review identifies a resource gap but there's no CAPA (Corrective and Preventive Action) or action item tracking it to resolution, you've identified a problem and then done nothing visible about it. Auditors follow the paper trail — or notice when it's missing.

**3. Assuming "resources" only means headcount.**
Resources include equipment, software, physical workspace, and the work environment. A common gap: companies document human resource needs but never assess whether their lab equipment, calibration schedules, or document control software can support increased production or new product types.

**4. Conflating resource provision with resource adequacy.**
You can technically "provide" a resource — say, you hired someone — but if that person isn't trained or qualified for the role, the resource isn't adequate. Clause 6.1 requires resources that are sufficient to meet requirements, not just present.

## Key Terms to Know

**QMS (Quality Management System):** The documented set of processes, procedures, and responsibilities that a company uses to achieve and maintain product quality. Clause 6.1 is specifically about resourcing this system.

**Resource:** In this context — personnel, infrastructure, equipment, software, physical work environment, or financial means needed to operate the QMS and produce conforming devices.

**Management Review:** A formal, periodic meeting where top management evaluates the performance of the QMS. Resource needs are a required input to this process (covered in Clause 5.6).

**Continual Improvement:** An ongoing effort to enhance processes and outcomes. Clause 6.1 requires resources to support this — not just to maintain the status quo.

**Regulatory Requirements:** The legal and technical rules your device must comply with in each market — FDA regulations in the US, EU MDR in Europe, etc. Resources must be sufficient to meet these.

**Nonconformance:** A failure to meet a specified requirement. Inadequate resources that lead to QMS gaps can themselves be documented as nonconformances during audits.

**CAPA (Corrective and Preventive Action):** A formal process for identifying, investigating, and fixing quality problems. Relevant here when resource gaps are identified and need to be addressed systematically.

## Check Your Understanding

**1. What does Clause 6.1 require beyond simply identifying resource needs?**
*Answer:* The clause requires the organization to actually *provide* the resources identified as necessary — identification alone doesn't satisfy the requirement.

**2. Which of the following counts as a "resource" under Clause 6.1?**
a) Only employees
b) Employees, equipment, and work environment
c) Only budget and headcount
d) Only physical infrastructure

*Answer: b.* Resources include personnel, equipment, infrastructure, software, and the work environment.

**3. Scenario: During a management review, a QA team identifies that they need a dedicated complaint handling specialist but no action is taken and nothing is documented. An auditor reviews the management review minutes six months later. What finding might result?**
*Answer:* The auditor could issue a finding that the company identified a resource need but failed to act on or document a response — a gap between identifying requirements (Clause 6.1) and following through, potentially also touching Clause 5.6 (Management Review outputs).

**4. Why isn't it enough to simply hire someone to close a resource gap?**
*Answer:* A person must be qualified and trained for the role to constitute an adequate resource. Providing an unqualified person doesn't meet the requirement for resources sufficient to meet QMS and regulatory needs.

**5. Scenario: A growing device company is expanding into the EU market. Their current RA team has no MDR experience. Under Clause 6.1, what should happen?**
*Answer:* The company should formally assess the resource gap (likely during management review), determine what's needed — training, new hires, or external consultants — and provide those resources with documented evidence of the decision and action taken.

## How This Connects to Your Career

Early in your career, Clause 6.1 might feel abstract — it doesn't give you a checklist to execute. But understanding it teaches you to think like a systems-level QA professional, not just a task-completor. When you can walk into a management review and present a credible, evidence-based resource assessment, you're demonstrating business judgment alongside technical knowledge. Employers — and auditors — notice the difference between a QA professional who reacts to problems and one who anticipates resource constraints before they become nonconformances. That skill is what gets you from QA Associate to QA Engineer to Manager.
