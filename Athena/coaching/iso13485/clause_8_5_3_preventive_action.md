# ISO 13485:2016 — Clause 8.5.3: Preventive Action

## What This Clause Is About

Most quality systems spend a lot of energy reacting to problems after they happen. Clause 8.5.3 asks you to think ahead — to spot potential problems before they cause a nonconformity, a customer complaint, or a product failure. The goal is to eliminate the *root causes* of problems that haven't occurred yet, not just fix symptoms after the fact.

---

## What It Requires (The Essentials)

ISO 13485:2016 Clause 8.5.3 requires your organization to:

1. **Define a process for taking preventive action.** This can't be informal or ad hoc. You need a documented procedure that describes how your team identifies potential problems, investigates their causes, and takes action to prevent them from ever occurring.

2. **Identify potential nonconformities and their causes.** A nonconformity is any failure to meet a requirement — a product defect, a process deviation, a documentation error. You need a structured way to spot these *before* they happen, not after.

3. **Evaluate the need for action.** Not every potential problem requires a formal preventive action. You need to assess the risk — how likely is it? How severe would the impact be? Your response should be proportional to the risk.

4. **Implement action and record the results.** Whatever you decide to do, document it. Record what potential problem you identified, what you did about it, and whether it worked.

5. **Review the effectiveness of the preventive action taken.** Doing something isn't enough. You have to go back and verify that the action actually eliminated or reduced the risk.

*Note: These are summaries to help you build understanding. Always refer to the actual ISO 13485:2016 standard for the complete, official requirements.*

## What This Looks Like in Practice

Imagine you work at a small orthopedic implant company with about 80 employees. Your Quality Engineer notices something during a routine review of incoming inspection data: over the past six months, titanium screws from one supplier have been trending toward the lower boundary of the acceptable dimensional tolerance — they haven't failed yet, but they're getting close.

She flags this in the **Quality Management Review** meeting. Because there's no actual failure, this doesn't trigger a Corrective Action (that's Clause 8.5.2 — for problems that *have* occurred). Instead, the team opens a **Preventive Action Request (PAR)** using the company's CAPA procedure, *SOP-QA-007: Corrective and Preventive Action*.

The Quality Engineer is assigned as the owner. She conducts a basic risk assessment — if these screws go out of tolerance, they could fail to engage bone properly, which is a serious patient safety risk. The risk is significant, so action is warranted.

After investigating root cause, she finds the supplier recently changed their machining process without notifying the company. The preventive actions include: updating the supplier agreement to require process change notifications, adding a tighter incoming inspection step for that specific component, and scheduling a supplier audit.

All of this gets documented in the company's **CAPA tracking log** in their eQMS (electronic quality management system). Thirty days later, she reviews the next batch of incoming inspection data and confirms dimensions have stabilized. The PAR is closed with documented evidence of effectiveness.

That's preventive action working the way it's supposed to.

## Common Mistakes and Audit Findings

**1. Confusing preventive action with corrective action.**
This is the most common mix-up for early-career professionals. Corrective action responds to a problem that already happened. Preventive action addresses a potential problem that *hasn't* happened yet. Auditors will ask: "What was the evidence that this problem hadn't occurred yet?" If you can't answer that clearly, you've probably mislabeled a corrective action.

**2. Treating preventive action as purely reactive.**
Some companies only open preventive actions after complaints, audits, or near-misses — which defeats the purpose. Strong programs proactively mine data sources like trend reports, supplier scorecards, and process monitoring data to find early warning signs.

**3. No documented effectiveness check.**
Teams take action, close the record, and move on. Auditors frequently find PARs with no evidence that anyone ever went back to verify the action worked. If effectiveness isn't verified and documented, the record is incomplete.

**4. Scope is too vague.**
Records that say something like "improve supplier communication" without specifying *what* will be done, *by whom*, and *by when* are a red flag in audits. Preventive actions need to be specific and measurable enough that you can actually verify whether they worked.

## Key Terms to Know

**Nonconformity:** Failure to meet a requirement — could be a product defect, a process deviation, or a documentation gap.

**Potential nonconformity:** A situation that hasn't failed yet but shows signs it could. This is what preventive action targets.

**Root cause:** The fundamental reason a problem exists or could exist. Not the symptom — the underlying cause. Fixing only the symptom means the problem comes back.

**Preventive Action Request (PAR):** The formal record your company opens to document a preventive action. Some companies combine this with corrective actions into a single CAPA record.

**CAPA:** Corrective and Preventive Action. A combined system — though the two are conceptually distinct. Corrective = fix what went wrong. Preventive = stop something from going wrong.

**Risk assessment:** A structured evaluation of how likely a potential problem is and how serious the consequences would be. Informs how much effort your preventive action should require.

**Effectiveness verification:** The step where you go back after taking action and confirm the action actually addressed the potential problem. Required — not optional.

## Check Your Understanding

**1. What is the key difference between corrective action and preventive action?**
*Answer:* Corrective action addresses a problem that has already occurred. Preventive action addresses a potential problem — something that hasn't happened yet but could.

**2. Does ISO 13485 require you to take preventive action on every potential risk you identify?**
*Answer:* No. You're required to evaluate the need for action and respond proportionally to the level of risk. Not every potential issue warrants a formal preventive action.

**3. Scenario: During a management review, your team notices that customer complaints about packaging seal integrity have increased by 15% over three months but haven't exceeded your alert threshold yet. Should this trigger a corrective action or a preventive action — and why?**
*Answer:* This is a borderline case, but because no confirmed nonconformity has occurred yet, it would typically be addressed as a preventive action. The trend is a warning sign. If the threshold is exceeded or a confirmed failure occurs, it would escalate to corrective action.

**4. What are two data sources a medical device company could use to proactively identify potential nonconformities?**
*Answer:* Any two of: supplier performance data, incoming inspection trends, process monitoring data, customer complaint trends, employee observations, internal audit results, equipment calibration trends.

**5. Scenario: A PAR was opened six months ago. The assigned owner documented the actions taken but never went back to check whether the actions worked. What's missing, and why does it matter?**
*Answer:* The effectiveness verification is missing. Without it, there's no evidence the preventive action actually addressed the root cause. The potential problem could still exist — and the record is incomplete from an audit standpoint.

## How This Connects to Your Career

Quality professionals who understand preventive action think differently than those who only know how to react to problems. When you can look at a trend report, a supplier scorecard, or a process metric and say "this is heading somewhere bad — let's act now," you're demonstrating exactly the kind of judgment that QA managers and regulatory directors trust. Employers in the medical device industry are looking for people who can protect the company from problems, not just document them after they've already cost money, delayed a product launch, or worse, harmed a patient. Mastering Clause 8.5.3 is one of the clearest ways to show that you're thinking proactively — and in this industry, that matters.
