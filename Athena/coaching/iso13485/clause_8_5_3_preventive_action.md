# ISO 13485:2016 — Clause 8.5.3: Preventive Action

# ISO 13485:2016 Clause 8.5.3: Preventive Action

*Educational content only. Always refer to the actual ISO 13485:2016 standard for official requirements.*

---

## What This Clause Is About

Most quality problems are predictable if you're paying attention. Clause 8.5.3 requires your company to have a systematic process for spotting potential problems — ones that haven't happened yet — and taking action before they become real failures. It's the difference between fixing a flat tire on the side of the road and noticing the tread wearing down before your next long drive.

---

## What It Requires (The Essentials)

**1. You need a documented procedure.**
The company must have a written process describing how preventive action works — who triggers it, how potential problems get evaluated, and how actions get tracked to completion.

**2. You must identify potential nonconformities.**
A nonconformity is when something doesn't meet a requirement. Preventive action is about finding situations where a nonconformity *could* happen if nothing changes. Sources can include trend data, audit findings, supplier performance, customer complaints, or process monitoring results.

**3. You must evaluate the need for action.**
Not every risk signal needs a formal preventive action. The team needs to assess whether the potential issue is significant enough to act on — and document that evaluation either way.

**4. Actions taken must be documented and reviewed for effectiveness.**
If you decide to act, you record what you did. Later, you verify that it actually worked. A preventive action that sits in a spreadsheet with no follow-up isn't a preventive action — it's a note.

**5. Relevant information must be communicated.**
Management and affected functions need to know what preventive actions are in play. This typically flows through management review.

## What This Looks Like in Practice

Meridian Surgical Devices is a 60-person company that makes laparoscopic instruments. Their Quality Engineer, Priya, runs the CAPA system using a cloud-based QMS platform.

During a quarterly trend review, Priya notices that three incoming inspection records over the past two months flagged the same supplier's stainless steel tubing as slightly outside the lower end of the dimensional tolerance — not enough to fail, but consistently close to the line. No nonconformity has been written yet. Nothing failed. But the trend is real.

Priya opens a **Preventive Action Request (PAR)** in the QMS. She documents the trend data, links the relevant inspection records, and assigns an initial risk evaluation. She routes it to the Supplier Quality Engineer and the Materials Manager for review.

Together, they determine that if the supplier's process continues drifting, they'll start getting actual rejects within a few production cycles — and that tubing is used in a Class II device. They decide the risk warrants action.

The team contacts the supplier, requests a process capability study (a statistical measure of how consistently a process hits its target), and schedules an on-site audit. They update their **Approved Supplier List** notes with a watch flag and set a 90-day review point.

At the next **Management Review**, Priya presents the open PAR as part of the quality metrics report. Three months later, the supplier submits a corrective action plan on their end, capability data improves, and Priya documents the effectiveness check — trend is stable, no rejects. The PAR is closed.

That's the whole loop: signal → evaluation → action → verification → closure.

## Common Mistakes and Audit Findings

**1. Confusing preventive action with corrective action.**
This is the most common mistake. Corrective action (Clause 8.5.2) responds to something that already went wrong. Preventive action responds to something that *might* go wrong. Early-career professionals often log a preventive action after a complaint already happened — that's corrective territory.

**2. Never opening preventive actions at all.**
Auditors notice when a company's CAPA log has hundreds of corrective actions and zero preventive actions. It signals that the organization is purely reactive. Notified bodies specifically look for evidence that the system is proactive, not just reactive.

**3. No documented effectiveness check.**
Opening a PAR and completing the action isn't enough. You must verify it worked. A common audit finding is preventive actions closed without any evidence that the underlying risk was actually reduced.

**4. Vague root cause analysis on potential issues.**
Just writing "risk of supplier nonconformance" isn't useful. Auditors want to see *why* the potential problem could occur and *what specific mechanism* you're addressing. "Supplier process drift observed in dimensional data over 60-day period; capability index below 1.33 threshold" is far more defensible.

## Key Terms to Know

**Preventive Action** — A planned action taken to eliminate the *potential* cause of a nonconformity before it actually occurs.

**Nonconformity** — Failure to meet a requirement (a spec, a regulation, a procedure, etc.).

**CAPA** — Corrective and Preventive Action. The combined system most companies use to manage both types. Preventive action is the "PA" half.

**PAR (Preventive Action Request)** — The actual record or form used to document a preventive action. Different companies name it differently, but this is a common term.

**Trend Analysis** — Reviewing data over time to spot patterns. In quality, this means looking at complaint rates, inspection failures, supplier data, and process metrics to catch problems developing before they peak.

**Effectiveness Check** — The follow-up step where you verify that the action you took actually resolved the potential problem. Required — not optional.

**Process Capability** — A statistical measure of how consistently a process produces output within its required specifications. Often expressed as Cpk. A low Cpk signals a process at risk of producing nonconforming output.

**Management Review** — A required periodic meeting where leadership reviews quality system performance, including CAPA status and trends.

## Check Your Understanding

**1. What is the key difference between corrective action and preventive action?**
*Answer:* Corrective action addresses a problem that has already occurred. Preventive action addresses a potential problem that hasn't happened yet but could if nothing changes.

**2. Name two sources of information that might trigger a preventive action.**
*Answer:* Any two of the following count: trend data from inspections or complaints, audit findings, supplier performance data, process monitoring results, customer feedback, or risk analysis outputs.

**3. If your company's CAPA log shows 87 corrective actions and 0 preventive actions over two years, what will an auditor likely conclude?**
*Answer:* That the quality system is purely reactive and the preventive action process is not functioning effectively — a significant finding against Clause 8.5.3.

**4. Scenario: A production supervisor mentions in a team meeting that she's seen three operators struggling with a new assembly step, but no products have failed inspection yet. Should this trigger a preventive action? Why or why not?**
*Answer:* Yes, this is a strong candidate for a preventive action. The operator difficulty is a signal of potential process nonconformity. Waiting for actual failures would shift this to corrective action territory. Document the observation, evaluate the risk, and determine whether action (retraining, procedure update, ergonomic change) is warranted.

**5. Scenario: You close a preventive action after implementing a new supplier audit schedule. Six months later, the same supplier starts producing out-of-spec parts. What went wrong in your PA process?**
*Answer:* The effectiveness check either wasn't performed or wasn't rigorous enough. The action taken (audit schedule) may not have addressed the actual root cause of the potential problem. The PA should be reopened and the root cause re-evaluated.
