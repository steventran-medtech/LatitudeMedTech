# ISO 13485:2016 — Clause 8.2: Monitoring and Measurement

# ISO 13485:2016 Clause 8.2: Monitoring and Measurement

*Educational content only. Always refer to the actual ISO 13485:2016 standard for official requirements.*

---

## What This Clause Is About

Medical device companies need evidence that their products and processes are actually performing the way they're supposed to — not just assumed to be. Clause 8.2 sets up the framework for gathering that evidence systematically: measuring customer satisfaction, auditing your own quality system, monitoring your processes, and inspecting your products. Without this clause, you're flying blind.

---

## What It Requires (The Essentials)

**1. Customer feedback isn't optional — it's a system.**
You must have a documented process for gathering and analyzing information about whether you're meeting customer requirements. This doesn't mean you wait for complaints. It means you proactively collect data — complaint rates, returned product trends, service call logs, customer surveys — and analyze it on a schedule.

**2. Internal audits must follow a defined program.**
You can't audit your own QMS randomly or only when something goes wrong. You need a written audit schedule, trained auditors, documented audit plans, and records of findings and corrective actions. The standard also requires that auditors don't audit their own work — independence matters.

**3. Processes need to be monitored, not just documented.**
Having a procedure isn't enough. You need to show that your processes are actually achieving their planned results. If your sterilization process is supposed to hit a specific cycle time and temperature, you need data showing it does — consistently.

**4. Product requires inspection and testing at appropriate stages.**
Before product is released, you need evidence it meets requirements. This means incoming inspection of materials, in-process checks during manufacturing, and final inspection before shipment. Records must show who performed the inspection and what the results were.

**5. Nothing ships without evidence of conformity.**
Product can't be released until all planned inspections and tests are completed — and completed successfully. Someone with the authority to release product must sign off, and that authorization needs to be traceable in your records.

## What This Looks Like in Practice

Imagine a 60-person company that makes infusion pumps. Here's how their team handles Clause 8.2 day-to-day:

The **Quality Manager** owns a document called the *Internal Audit Schedule* — a spreadsheet updated annually that maps out which departments get audited and when. Auditors are cross-trained employees (a Manufacturing Engineer auditing the Purchasing department, for example) who've completed internal auditor training. After each audit, they fill out an *Audit Report Form* that documents what was checked, what was found, and whether a CAPA (Corrective and Preventive Action) was opened.

The **Customer Quality Engineer** runs a monthly report from the complaint handling system that captures complaint volume, complaint categories, and any MDRs (Medical Device Reports filed with FDA). That data feeds into a *Customer Feedback Analysis* document that goes to the quarterly Management Review meeting.

On the production floor, line operators fill out a *Device History Record (DHR)* for every batch — capturing in-process checks like torque values on connectors, visual inspection results, and software version verification. A final inspection tech signs the DHR before product is cleared for release. Nothing ships until that signature is there.

It's not glamorous. It's a lot of forms and schedules. But that paper trail is exactly what an auditor or investigator needs to see.

## Common Mistakes and Audit Findings

**1. Customer feedback monitoring that's really just complaint tracking.**
Companies often confuse "we log complaints" with "we have a customer feedback system." Auditors look for evidence that you're also collecting *positive* and *neutral* signals — delivery performance, service response times, customer satisfaction data. If your feedback process only activates when something breaks, it's incomplete.

**2. Internal audit programs that exist on paper but slip in practice.**
A common finding: the audit schedule says six departments get audited annually, but records only show three were completed. Regulators don't accept "we were busy." If you can't execute the schedule, revise it — but close all planned audits.

**3. Process monitoring data that nobody analyzes.**
Teams collect data (cycle times, yields, defect rates) but store it in spreadsheets that never get reviewed. The standard requires you to *use* monitoring data to confirm processes are achieving results. Data sitting in a folder isn't monitoring — it's storage.

**4. DHRs with missing signatures or incomplete entries.**
This is one of the most common 483 observations from FDA. A blank field on a Device History Record — even an "N/A" that should be explained — can hold up a product release or trigger an inspection finding. Early-career folks often underestimate how strictly completeness is evaluated.

## Key Terms to Know

**Customer feedback:** Information from customers about their experience with your product or service — includes complaints, but also returns, surveys, and service data.

**Internal audit:** A structured, documented review of your own QMS processes to verify they're working as intended and meeting requirements.

**Audit finding:** A documented result from an audit — either a nonconformity (something not meeting requirements) or an observation (a potential concern worth watching).

**Device History Record (DHR):** The batch-level record that documents the manufacturing history of a specific device or lot — inspections, test results, and release authorization.

**Process monitoring:** The ongoing collection and review of data to confirm a process consistently produces the intended result.

**Conformity:** Meeting a specified requirement — whether a product specification, regulatory requirement, or procedural step.

**Product release:** The formal decision and authorization that a product meets all requirements and can be shipped to customers.

**Management Review:** A scheduled meeting where leadership reviews QMS performance data — including monitoring and measurement outputs — to make decisions about resources and improvements.

## Check Your Understanding

**1. Why does the standard require auditors to be independent from the work they're auditing?**
*Answer:* People have blind spots about their own work and may unconsciously overlook problems they created. Independence helps ensure audits are objective.

**2. What's the difference between process monitoring and product inspection?**
*Answer:* Process monitoring tracks how a process is performing over time (e.g., tracking yield rates). Product inspection evaluates a specific device or lot against defined requirements at a point in time.

**3. Scenario: Your audit schedule shows the design department should be audited in Q2. It's now Q4 and that audit hasn't happened. What should you do?**
*Answer:* Don't just skip it. Conduct the overdue audit as soon as possible, document the delay and reason in the audit records, and evaluate whether the schedule needs to be revised. Open a CAPA if the gap represents a systemic problem.

**4. True or false: A complaint rate of zero means your customer feedback system is working well.**
*Answer:* False. Zero complaints might mean customers are satisfied — or it might mean your complaint intake process is broken and customers aren't reporting issues. A functioning feedback system actively collects multiple types of data, not just complaints.

**5. Scenario: A production technician skips an in-process inspection step because the line is behind schedule and initials the DHR as completed. What's the problem?**
*Answer:* This is a falsification of records and a serious compliance violation, beyond just a QMS issue. The DHR must accurately reflect what was actually done. The in-process inspection either needs to be completed or the nonconformance documented and dispositioned.
