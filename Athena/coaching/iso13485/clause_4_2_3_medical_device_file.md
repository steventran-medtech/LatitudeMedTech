# ISO 13485:2016 — Clause 4.2.3: Medical Device File

## What This Clause Is About

When regulators, auditors, or your own engineers need to understand a specific medical device — what it is, how it was designed, how it's made, and whether it's safe — they need one place to go. Clause 4.2.3 solves that problem by requiring your company to maintain a Medical Device File (MDF) for each device type or product family. Think of it as the device's complete biography, kept current and organized so anyone who needs it can find what they're looking for.

---

## What It Requires (The Essentials)

The clause doesn't prescribe a rigid format, but it does require that your Medical Device File contain enough information to demonstrate conformity with requirements. Here's what you actually need to understand:

**1. One file per device type or product family.**
You don't need a separate MDF for every individual unit that rolls off the line, but you do need one for each distinct device type. If your company makes three different catheter designs, you likely have three MDFs — or clearly defined sections within a structured system.

**2. The file must include or reference key documentation.**
This typically covers: intended use and indications for use, design specifications, manufacturing specifications, risk management records (your ISO 14971 file), clinical evaluation records, labeling, and regulatory submission records. The standard says "include or reference" — so you can point to where documents live rather than copying everything into one folder, as long as you can actually retrieve them.

**3. It must be maintained and kept current.**
A snapshot from the design phase that never gets updated is not compliant. When design changes happen, risk management gets updated, or labeling is revised, the MDF needs to reflect that. This is where many companies struggle.

**4. It must support traceability.**
Someone auditing your device should be able to trace from the final product back through design outputs, design inputs, verification and validation, and risk management without hitting dead ends. The MDF is the spine that holds that traceability together.

**5. Access must be controlled.**
Your MDF is a living set of controlled documents. It falls under your document control system (Clause 4.2.4), which means version control, approval records, and restricted editing access all apply.

## What This Looks Like in Practice

Imagine a 60-person company in Minneapolis that makes a powered surgical handpiece. Their Regulatory Affairs Specialist — call her Maria — owns the Medical Device File for that product line.

The MDF itself isn't a single massive PDF. It's an index document, maybe called *MDF-0042 Surgical Handpiece Series*, that lives in the company's electronic document management system (EDMS). The index lists every relevant document by title, document number, and version — and links directly to each one.

When Maria onboards a new QA Coordinator, she walks him through the index. He can see: the 510(k) summary and FDA clearance letter, the Device Description document, the Risk Management File (cross-referencing their ISO 14971 process), the Design History File sections covering verification and validation, the Labeling package including IFU and device labels, and the manufacturing specifications tied to the device master record.

When engineering proposes a material change to the handpiece grip, Maria gets pulled into the change control process early. Her job is to make sure the MDF index gets updated after the change is approved — new document versions get logged, the risk management file gets reviewed for impact, and nothing falls through the cracks. The MDF doesn't change itself. Someone has to own that.

## Common Mistakes and Audit Findings

**1. Confusing the MDF with the Design History File.**
The Design History File (DHF) documents that the design was developed according to your design controls plan. The MDF is broader — it includes the DHF but also encompasses post-market data, risk management, regulatory records, and more. Treating them as identical leaves gaps that auditors find quickly.

**2. The file exists, but it's out of date.**
This is the most common real-world finding. A device gets a labeling revision, but the MDF index still references the old label version. Or a design change was implemented two years ago and the risk management file was never updated. Notified bodies and FDA investigators will pull thread on this.

**3. "Reference" without actual retrieval.**
Saying a document is "referenced" in the MDF when no one can actually find it is a nonconformity waiting to happen. If your index points to a network drive folder that's been reorganized, or a document number that doesn't match what's in the EDMS, you have a broken link.

**4. No clear ownership.**
When every department assumes someone else is maintaining the MDF, nobody is. Without a named owner and a defined review trigger (like a change control or annual review), the file drifts out of compliance quietly.

## Key Terms to Know

**Medical Device File (MDF):** The organized collection — or indexed reference system — of documents that describe a specific device, covering its design, manufacturing, risk management, labeling, and regulatory history.

**Device Type / Product Family:** A grouping of devices that share the same basic design, intended use, and manufacturing process. One MDF typically covers one device type or product family.

**Design History File (DHF):** The subset of design and development documentation showing that the device was designed according to an approved plan. It's part of the MDF, not a replacement for it.

**Device Master Record (DMR):** The set of documents describing how to manufacture a device — specifications, procedures, work instructions, and quality requirements. Also included in or referenced by the MDF.

**Risk Management File:** Documentation required by ISO 14971 that covers hazard identification, risk estimation, risk control measures, and residual risk evaluation. An essential part of the MDF.

**Traceability:** The ability to connect a finished device back through its design, testing, manufacturing, and risk management history — and forward to any post-market data. The MDF is what makes this possible.

**EDMS (Electronic Document Management System):** Software used to store, version-control, and manage access to quality system documents. Most MDFs exist within an EDMS rather than as physical binders.

## Check Your Understanding

**1. What is the primary purpose of the Medical Device File?**
To provide a single organized source — or structured reference system — for all documentation that describes a device type, enabling anyone to demonstrate conformity and trace the device's history.

**2. Does your company need a separate MDF for every unit manufactured?**
No. The MDF is per device type or product family, not per individual unit produced.

**3. Scenario: Your company revises the Instructions for Use (IFU) for a pulse oximeter. What MDF-related action is required?**
The MDF index must be updated to reference the new IFU version, and the change should be processed through your document control and change control procedures. The old version should be superseded in the EDMS.

**4. What's the difference between the Design History File and the Medical Device File?**
The DHF documents that the design process was followed correctly and is a component of the MDF. The MDF is broader, also covering manufacturing specifications, risk management, labeling, and regulatory records.

**5. Scenario: During an audit, a notified body auditor asks to see the risk management records for your device. Your MDF index references a folder location that no longer exists in your EDMS. What type of finding is this likely to result in?**
A nonconformity — specifically for failure to maintain accessible, controlled records and for a broken reference in the MDF. This could also trigger findings under document control (Clause 4.2.4) and risk management.
