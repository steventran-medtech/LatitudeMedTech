# ISO 13485:2016 — Clause 5.2: Customer Focus

## What This Clause Is About

Clause 5.2 puts the customer at the center of top management's responsibilities. It requires company leadership to make sure customer requirements — including regulatory requirements — are understood and actually met. Without this, companies can drift toward building what's technically feasible rather than what customers need and regulators require.

---

## What It Requires (The Essentials)

The clause is short, but it carries real weight. Here's what top management must ensure:

**1. Customer requirements are determined.**
Before you can meet requirements, you have to know what they are. This means systematically figuring out what customers (hospitals, clinicians, patients) actually need from your device — not just what they say they want, but also unstated expectations and regulatory requirements that apply.

**2. Customer requirements are met.**
Knowing requirements isn't enough. The organization must have processes that turn those requirements into product reality. This links directly to design controls, purchasing controls, and production processes.

**3. The goal is enhancing customer satisfaction.**
Clause 5.2 isn't a checkbox exercise. The intent is for leadership to actively drive an organization that cares about whether customers are satisfied with the outcome. This connects to Clause 8.2.1, which deals with feedback and complaint handling.

**4. Regulatory requirements relevant to customers are included.**
Customer requirements in ISO 13485 explicitly include applicable regulatory requirements. If a hospital in the EU expects your device to meet MDR requirements, that's a customer requirement. If a U.S. hospital expects FDA 510(k) clearance, that's a customer requirement too.

## What This Looks Like in Practice

Imagine a 60-person company making single-use surgical irrigation devices. Their customers are primarily hospital OR managers and purchasing departments, but the actual users are surgeons and scrub techs.

Here's how Clause 5.2 plays out in their day-to-day work:

The **VP of Quality** owns a procedure called *SOP-QMS-005: Customer Requirements Review*. This procedure defines how the company captures and documents customer requirements at the start of a new product development project and during contract review for new customers.

When the sales team lands a new hospital system account, the **Regulatory Affairs Specialist** reviews the contract and purchase order against a checklist that includes: applicable device classifications, labeling requirements, required certifications (ISO 13485, CE mark, 510(k)), and any customer-specific performance specs. This checklist becomes part of the **contract review record** filed in the QMS.

In design and development, requirements captured from customer interviews and market research are entered into a **Design Input** document. The design team traces every input back to an identified customer or regulatory requirement — so nothing gets built on assumptions.

The **Quality Manager** reviews customer complaint and feedback data monthly and reports trends to the leadership team during the **Management Review** meeting. If complaints are rising around a particular product feature, leadership is expected to act — not just note it.

The whole system only works because leadership actually looks at this data and makes decisions. Clause 5.2 is less about a specific document and more about whether leadership is engaged or just going through the motions.

## Common Mistakes and Audit Findings

**1. Treating customer requirements as only what's written in the purchase order.**
Early-career professionals often focus on documented specs and miss implied requirements — like sterility expectations for surgical devices, or packaging requirements for cold-chain products. Auditors will ask how you identify *all* applicable requirements, not just the ones typed into a contract.

**2. Separating regulatory requirements from customer requirements.**
These are not two separate lists. A notified body auditor will specifically check whether your customer requirement review process includes the regulatory requirements your customers expect you to meet. If your contract review SOP doesn't mention regulatory compliance, that's a gap.

**3. Capturing requirements but not verifying they were met.**
Some companies do a solid job documenting customer requirements during design input but then have no traceability showing those requirements were actually verified before product release. The clause requires meeting requirements, not just recording them.

**4. Top management isn't actually involved.**
This clause is in Section 5 (Management Responsibility) for a reason. A common finding is that QA staff are doing all the work while leadership is absent from the loop. If your management review records show that customer satisfaction data was presented but no decisions were made and no actions were taken, that's a red flag for auditors.

## Key Terms to Know

**Customer requirements** — What customers need from your product, including stated needs, implied needs, and applicable regulatory requirements.

**Regulatory requirements** — Legal and regulatory obligations that apply to your device in the markets where it's sold (e.g., FDA regulations, EU MDR, Health Canada).

**Top management** — The person or group that directs and controls the organization at the highest level. In a small company, this might be the CEO and VP of Quality together.

**Contract review** — A formal process of checking that customer orders and contracts are achievable and that all requirements are understood before you accept work.

**Customer satisfaction** — The degree to which customers feel their requirements were met. In ISO 13485, this includes collecting and analyzing feedback — complaints, surveys, returned products, service calls.

**Design input** — The documented requirements that form the basis for device design. These must trace back to customer and regulatory requirements.

**Management review** — A periodic meeting where top management evaluates QMS performance, including customer feedback and whether customer requirements are being met.

## Check Your Understanding

**1. What does Clause 5.2 require top management to ensure?**
That customer requirements (including regulatory requirements) are determined and met, with the aim of enhancing customer satisfaction.

**2. Why are regulatory requirements considered part of customer requirements under ISO 13485?**
Because customers expect and depend on your device meeting the regulatory standards applicable in their market. A hospital purchasing a CE-marked device expects it to comply with EU MDR — that's a real expectation, not just a bureaucratic formality.

**3. Scenario: A QA analyst at your company says, "Clause 5.2 is handled — we have a contract review procedure." Is that sufficient? What else should you verify?**
Not necessarily. You'd also want to verify that the procedure includes regulatory requirements, that there's evidence the procedure is being followed, that outputs connect to design inputs and production controls, and that top management is actually reviewing customer satisfaction data and acting on it.

**4. Which other ISO 13485 clause is most directly connected to Clause 5.2's goal of enhancing customer satisfaction?**
Clause 8.2.1 — Feedback. This clause requires the organization to collect and analyze post-market information, including complaints, as an early warning system for product problems.

**5. Scenario: During an audit, the notified body auditor asks for evidence that your company is meeting customer requirements. What records would you pull?**
Contract review records, design input documents with traceability to customer and regulatory requirements, verification and validation records, and management review minutes showing customer satisfaction data was reviewed and acted on.

## How This Connects to Your Career

Understanding Clause 5.2 makes you more useful in cross-functional conversations — with sales, engineering, and leadership — because you can explain *why* customer requirements need to be formally captured and traced, not just handed off verbally. Early-career QA/RA professionals who understand this clause can spot gaps during contract reviews, flag missing regulatory requirements before they become audit findings, and connect the dots between what customers need and what the QMS is supposed to deliver. Employers value people who can bridge the gap between regulatory compliance and business reality, and Clause 5.2 sits right at that intersection.
