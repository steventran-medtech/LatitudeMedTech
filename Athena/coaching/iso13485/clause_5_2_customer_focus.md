# ISO 13485:2016 — Clause 5.2: Customer Focus

# ISO 13485:2016 Clause 5.2: Customer Focus

*Educational content only. Always refer to the official ISO 13485:2016 standard for complete requirements.*

---

## What This Clause Is About

Medical device companies can easily get consumed by internal processes and lose sight of who they're actually building products for. Clause 5.2 puts the responsibility on top management to make sure customer requirements — and applicable regulatory requirements — are understood and consistently met. It's a leadership commitment clause, not a checklist.

---

## What It Requires (The Essentials)

Clause 5.2 is short, but it carries real weight. Here's what top management must ensure:

1. **Customer requirements are determined.** Before you can meet requirements, you have to know what they are. This means actively identifying what customers need — not guessing.

2. **Regulatory requirements are identified.** "Customer" in this context isn't just the hospital buying your device or the surgeon using it. Regulatory bodies set requirements too, and those count. In the US, that means FDA regulations. In Europe, that means the EU MDR and harmonized standards.

3. **Requirements are met with the aim of enhancing customer satisfaction.** Meeting requirements is the floor, not the ceiling. The standard explicitly connects this to customer satisfaction — meaning management should care about outcomes, not just compliance checkboxes.

4. **This is a top management responsibility.** This clause lives in Section 5, which is entirely about leadership. A quality manager can support this work, but the accountability sits with executives and senior leaders.

## What This Looks Like in Practice

Imagine a 75-person company called Summit Surgical that makes laparoscopic trocars. Here's how Clause 5.2 plays out in their day-to-day operation.

**Capturing customer requirements:** Summit's product managers work with the sales team to document requirements in something called a **Customer Requirements Specification (CRS)** before any new product development begins. This document captures inputs from surgeons, hospital procurement teams, and distributor feedback. The VP of Operations reviews and approves it — that top management involvement is deliberate and documented.

**Regulatory requirements:** Summit's regulatory affairs manager maintains a **Regulatory Requirements Matrix** — a living spreadsheet that maps their products to applicable FDA 21 CFR Part 820 requirements, ISO standards, and EU MDR obligations. This matrix is reviewed at management reviews (that's Clause 5.6) and updated when regulations change.

**Closing the loop:** Summit tracks customer satisfaction through post-market surveillance data — complaint rates, surgeon survey results from trade shows, and field service reports. The Quality Director presents a summary at quarterly management review meetings. If complaint trends show a specific issue (say, trocar seal failures in high-BMI patients), that data feeds directly back into product improvement discussions.

The connection between "what customers told us they needed" and "what we actually built and delivered" is traceable. That traceability is what auditors look for.

## Common Mistakes and Audit Findings

**1. Treating this clause as a formality.** Early-career professionals sometimes see Clause 5.2 and think "we have a customer satisfaction survey, we're covered." Auditors will ask how survey results actually influenced decisions. If there's no evidence the data was reviewed and acted upon, that's a gap.

**2. Confusing "customer" with "end user only."** Your customers include hospitals, distributors, and patients — but regulatory requirements are also part of what Clause 5.2 covers. Companies that focus only on direct customer feedback and ignore regulatory input are leaving a significant gap.

**3. No documented evidence of top management involvement.** This clause requires *top management* to ensure these things happen. If your management review minutes, approval signatures, or meeting records don't show leadership engagement, you'll have trouble demonstrating compliance during an audit.

**4. Disconnected processes.** Customer requirements captured during sales never make it into design inputs. Post-market complaints aren't fed back into product planning. These silos are a systemic problem that Clause 5.2 is designed to prevent — and auditors are trained to spot them.

## Key Terms to Know

**Customer requirements:** Stated or implied needs from the people and organizations that receive your product or service. Can include performance specs, packaging needs, labeling expectations, and delivery requirements.

**Regulatory requirements:** Rules established by government agencies or recognized standards bodies that your product and processes must comply with. In the US, FDA. In Europe, EU MDR. Globally, country-specific regulations vary.

**Top management:** The person or group that directs and controls an organization at the highest level. In a small device company, this might be the CEO and VP of Operations. In a larger company, it's typically the executive leadership team.

**Customer satisfaction:** The degree to which customer expectations are met or exceeded. ISO 13485 requires this to be *monitored* — which means you need a method and documented results.

**Post-market surveillance (PMS):** The ongoing process of collecting and analyzing data about a device after it's been released to market. Complaints, adverse events, field corrective actions, and literature reviews are all PMS inputs.

**Applicable regulatory requirements:** The specific subset of all regulations that apply to your particular device, based on its classification, intended use, and the markets where it's sold.

**Management review:** A formal, scheduled meeting where top management evaluates the QMS performance. Clause 5.2 outputs (customer satisfaction data, regulatory changes) are inputs to this review.

## Check Your Understanding

**1. Who is responsible for ensuring customer focus under Clause 5.2?**
**Answer:** Top management. This is not delegable to a quality manager alone, though they can support the process.

**2. A company sells surgical drapes to hospitals. What sources should they use to identify customer requirements?**
**Answer:** Inputs could include hospital purchasing specifications, clinical feedback from OR nurses, distributor requirements, packaging and sterility needs, and applicable regulatory standards like FDA requirements for sterile devices.

**3. True or False: If a company meets all customer requirements, it has fully satisfied Clause 5.2.**
**Answer:** False. The clause also requires that applicable *regulatory* requirements are determined and met. Customer requirements alone are not sufficient.

**4. Scenario: An auditor asks the Quality Director, "How do you know your customers are satisfied?" The director points to a survey sent last year that showed 85% satisfaction. The auditor follows up: "What did you do with that information?" There's no documented answer. What's the likely finding?**
**Answer:** A nonconformity or observation for failing to demonstrate that customer satisfaction data was analyzed and used to drive action or improvement. Collecting data without acting on it doesn't satisfy the intent of the clause.

**5. Scenario: A regulatory affairs specialist learns that the EU MDR updated labeling requirements for Class IIa devices. Under Clause 5.2, what should happen next?**
**Answer:** The updated regulatory requirement should be captured in the company's regulatory requirements documentation, reviewed by top management (likely at a management review or an ad-hoc leadership meeting), and translated into any necessary changes to product labeling, procedures, or design files.

## How This Connects to Your Career

Understanding Clause 5.2 makes you more than a compliance technician — it makes you someone who can connect business strategy to quality systems. Early-career professionals who understand that customer focus is a *leadership* requirement can speak credibly with executives, not just quality teams. When you can walk into a management review and explain how complaint data connects to regulatory obligations and customer expectations, you demonstrate systems thinking. That's exactly what QA/RA managers look for when they're promoting people or hiring for more senior roles. Companies that struggle with customer focus tend to struggle with everything downstream — design controls, corrective actions, post-market surveillance — so being fluent here gives you leverage across almost every other clause in the standard.
