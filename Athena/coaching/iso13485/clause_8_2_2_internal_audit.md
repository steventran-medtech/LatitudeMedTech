# ISO 13485:2016 — Clause 8.2.2: Internal Audit

# ISO 13485:2016 Clause 8.2.2: Internal Audit

*Educational content only. Always refer to the actual ISO 13485:2016 standard for official requirements.*

---

## What This Clause Is About

Companies can't improve what they don't examine. Clause 8.2.2 requires your organization to systematically check whether its Quality Management System (QMS) is actually working the way it's supposed to — on paper and in practice. Think of it as a scheduled, structured self-inspection: you're looking for gaps before a regulator or notified body finds them first.

---

## What It Requires (The Essentials)

**1. You must have a planned audit program.**
Internal audits can't be random or reactive. You need a documented schedule — typically an annual audit plan — that covers all parts of your QMS over a defined cycle. Higher-risk processes get audited more frequently.

**2. Auditors must be independent from the work they're auditing.**
Someone can't audit their own work. If the Design Engineer wrote the design controls procedure, they shouldn't be the one auditing design controls. This independence is non-negotiable.

**3. Audits must follow a documented procedure.**
You need a written procedure that defines how audits are planned, conducted, recorded, and followed up. The procedure itself is a requirement, not just good practice.

**4. Findings must be documented and acted on.**
Every audit produces records — at minimum, an audit report. If you find nonconformities (problems), the affected area must take corrective action within a defined timeframe. You can't audit, find problems, and then do nothing.

**5. Management must see the results.**
Audit results feed into Management Review (Clause 5.6), so leadership is aware of systemic issues — not just individual findings.

## What This Looks Like in Practice

Meridian Surgical is a 45-person company making electrosurgical units. Their QA Manager, Dana, owns the internal audit program.

Every November, Dana drafts the **Annual Internal Audit Schedule** for the following year. She maps out all the QMS clauses and supporting processes — purchasing, production, complaints, CAPA, design controls — and assigns audit slots across the year. Complaint handling gets audited twice because it had findings last year.

Before each audit, Dana (or a trained internal auditor) creates an **Audit Plan** that specifies: the scope (what's being audited), the criteria (which procedure or clause applies), the auditee (the department being audited), and the date. The auditee gets this plan at least a week in advance.

During the audit, the auditor uses an **Audit Checklist** — a list of questions tied directly to the procedure requirements — to guide interviews and records review. They're looking at actual work, not just asking "do you follow your procedure?" They pull production records, check training files, verify that the procedure on the floor matches the approved version in the document control system.

After the audit, the auditor writes an **Audit Report** summarizing what was reviewed, any findings (nonconformities or observations), and the objective evidence behind each finding. The auditee signs off acknowledging the findings.

If there's a nonconformity — say, three production records missing required operator signatures — the production supervisor opens a **CAPA (Corrective and Preventive Action)** to investigate why it's happening and fix it. Dana tracks closure in the **CAPA Log** and verifies that the corrective action actually worked before closing it out.

At the quarterly Management Review, Dana presents a summary: how many audits completed, findings by category, CAPA closure rates. Leadership uses this to decide where to invest QMS resources.

## Common Mistakes and Audit Findings

**1. Audit schedules that exist but aren't followed.**
Companies build a solid annual plan and then let audits slip — production gets busy, the auditor leaves, priorities shift. A notified body auditor will look at your schedule and your actual completion records. Gaps are a finding. Build buffer time into your schedule and treat audit dates like regulatory submission deadlines.

**2. Auditors auditing their own area.**
This happens most often at small companies where roles overlap. The QA Specialist who wrote the complaint procedure shouldn't audit complaint handling. If you're short on trained auditors, look into cross-functional auditing (training someone from purchasing to audit production, and vice versa) or bring in a contract auditor.

**3. Findings with no teeth — observations that never become CAPAs.**
Some teams document findings as "observations" to avoid opening a CAPA, even when the issue is systemic. Auditors — both internal and external — see through this. If something is broken, it needs corrective action, not a footnote.

**4. Audit records that don't include objective evidence.**
Writing "procedure not followed" in an audit report isn't enough. You need to cite specific records: which document, which lot number, which date. Vague findings can't be properly investigated or corrected.

## Key Terms to Know

**Internal Audit:** A planned, independent examination of your own QMS to verify it meets requirements and is effectively implemented.

**Audit Program:** The overall plan for all internal audits across a defined time period — usually one year. Includes scope, frequency, and responsible auditors.

**Audit Criteria:** The specific requirements being evaluated — a procedure, a clause of ISO 13485, a regulatory requirement. This is what you measure the actual practice against.

**Objective Evidence:** Documented, verifiable facts — records, measurements, observations — that support an audit finding. Opinions don't count.

**Nonconformity:** A failure to meet a specified requirement. Could be a missing record, an unapproved deviation, or a process not matching its procedure.

**Observation:** A potential weakness that isn't yet a nonconformity — a heads-up, not a violation. Different companies handle these differently; know your own procedure.

**Corrective Action:** Steps taken to eliminate the root cause of a nonconformity so it doesn't happen again. Not the same as a quick fix.

**Auditee:** The person or department being audited.

## Check Your Understanding

**1. Can the person who wrote a procedure audit compliance to that same procedure?**
No. ISO 13485:2016 requires auditor independence. Auditing your own work compromises objectivity.

**2. What document drives your audit program for the year?**
The Annual Internal Audit Schedule (or Audit Program), which defines what will be audited, when, and by whom.

**3. A production audit finds that three batch records are missing required supervisor signatures. What happens next?**
A nonconformity is documented in the audit report with specific objective evidence. A CAPA is opened to investigate the root cause and implement a fix. The QA team tracks and verifies closure.

**4. Scenario: Your company completed only 6 of 12 planned internal audits last year due to staffing issues. A notified body audit is next month. What's the risk?**
Significant finding risk. Incomplete execution of the audit program is a nonconformity against Clause 8.2.2. You'd need to explain the gaps, show what corrective actions you're taking, and demonstrate the remaining audits are being rescheduled.

**5. Scenario: An auditor notes that the approved version of the labeling procedure (Rev. D) on the document control server doesn't match the printed copy (Rev. C) on the production floor. Is this a nonconformity or observation?**
This is a nonconformity — a specific, documented failure to control documents per your procedure (and Clause 4.2). It requires a CAPA, not just a note.

## How This Connects to Your Career

Early-career QA professionals who understand internal auditing become indispensable faster than those who don't. Being trained as an internal auditor — even at a junior level — exposes you to every corner of the QMS: design controls, purchasing, production, complaints. You build a system-level view of the company that most engineers and operations staff never develop. Hiring managers specifically look for people who've participated in or led internal audits because it signals you can think critically about processes, communicate findings without creating defensiveness, and drive accountability. It's also one of the fastest ways to prepare yourself for an external audit — when an FDA investigator or notified body auditor walks in the door, you'll recognize exactly what they're doing because you've done it yourself.
