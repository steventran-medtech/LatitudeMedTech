# ISO 13485:2016 — Clause 8.5.2: Corrective Action

# ISO 13485:2016 Clause 8.5.2: Corrective Action

*Educational content for learning purposes. Always refer to the actual ISO 13485:2016 standard for official requirements.*

---

## What This Clause Is About

When something goes wrong in your quality system — a product failure, a process breakdown, a customer complaint — you need a disciplined way to figure out why it happened and make sure it doesn't happen again. Clause 8.5.2 establishes that process: corrective action. It's not about putting out fires; it's about eliminating the root cause so the same fire doesn't start again next month.

---

## What It Requires (The Essentials)

**1. You need a documented procedure.**
Your company must have a written procedure that describes how corrective actions are handled — who initiates them, how investigations are conducted, and how you verify the fix worked.

**2. Investigate the root cause, not just the symptom.**
The standard requires you to determine the *cause* of the nonconformity — not just describe what went wrong. If a catheter tip was delaminating, "the tip delaminated" is a description. "The bonding adhesive cure time was reduced during a process change without proper validation" is a root cause.

**3. Evaluate whether action is actually needed.**
Not every problem requires a full corrective action. You're required to evaluate the significance of the issue — but if you decide action is needed, you must follow through completely.

**4. Implement and record what you did.**
Actions taken must be documented. Saying you "retrained" someone is not enough — you need records showing who was retrained, on what, and when.

**5. Verify the corrective action worked.**
This is the step teams most often skip. After you've implemented a fix, you must go back and confirm it actually resolved the problem. That verification needs to be documented too.

## What This Looks Like in Practice

A mid-size company making surgical stapling devices gets a complaint from a hospital: three staplers from the same lot failed to fire correctly during surgery. The Quality Engineer (QE) opens a **Corrective and Preventive Action (CAPA) record** in the company's quality management software (in this case, Greenlight Guru).

The QE works with the Manufacturing Engineer to run a **root cause analysis**. They use a fishbone diagram and 5-Why analysis, tracing the problem back to a raw material supplier who changed the spring steel alloy without notifying the company — a supplier control breakdown, not an assembly error.

The QE documents the investigation in the **CAPA form**, which includes sections for problem description, root cause, action plan, and verification. The action plan includes three items: issue a supplier corrective action request (SCAR) to the spring vendor, update the supplier agreement to require change notification, and add incoming inspection for spring force measurement.

The **Quality Manager** reviews and approves the CAPA. Actions are assigned owners with due dates.

Six months later, the QE revisits the CAPA to verify effectiveness. They pull inspection records from the past 90 days showing zero spring force failures on incoming lots. They also confirm the updated supplier agreement is signed. The CAPA is closed and documented.

That closed CAPA will almost certainly be pulled during the next ISO 13485 surveillance audit. The auditor will read it start to finish and ask questions.

## Common Mistakes and Audit Findings

**1. Root cause is actually a restatement of the problem.**
"Root cause: product was nonconforming" tells an auditor nothing. This is one of the most common findings. Root cause analysis requires actual analysis — tracing the failure back to a systemic or process-level cause.

**2. CAPAs left open indefinitely.**
Teams get busy. Action items slip. When an auditor sees a CAPA opened eighteen months ago with no closure or documented progress, it signals that your quality system isn't functioning. Set realistic due dates and track them.

**3. Verification of effectiveness is missing or superficial.**
"Retrained operator — issue resolved" does not constitute verification. You need objective evidence — data, records, follow-up audits — showing the problem hasn't recurred. This is one of the top FDA 483 observations related to CAPA.

**4. CAPA scope is too narrow.**
If a labeling error occurred on one product line, the corrective action that only fixes that one product line may miss the systemic issue. Good CAPA practice asks: could this same root cause affect other products, processes, or sites?

## Key Terms to Know

**Nonconformity** — A failure to meet a specified requirement. Could be a product defect, a process that isn't followed, or a documentation gap.

**Corrective Action** — Action taken to eliminate the *cause* of a nonconformity to prevent recurrence. Distinct from just fixing the immediate problem.

**Root Cause** — The fundamental reason a nonconformity occurred. Removing the root cause is the whole point of corrective action.

**CAPA** — Corrective and Preventive Action. The combined process (and often the form or record) used to manage both corrective and preventive actions. ISO 13485 treats them separately; most companies manage them in one system.

**Effectiveness Verification** — Objective evidence that the corrective action actually worked — that the root cause has been eliminated and the problem hasn't recurred.

**SCAR (Supplier Corrective Action Request)** — A formal request sent to a supplier requiring them to investigate and correct a quality problem on their end.

**483 Observation** — A written notice from an FDA investigator identifying conditions that may constitute violations. Unresolved CAPA weaknesses are a common source of these.

## Check Your Understanding

**Q1. What is the difference between fixing a nonconformity and corrective action?**
*Answer:* Fixing a nonconformity addresses the immediate problem (reworking a defective product, for example). Corrective action addresses the root cause — the underlying reason it happened — to prevent it from happening again.

**Q2. Does every nonconformity require a formal corrective action?**
*Answer:* No. ISO 13485 requires you to *evaluate* the need for corrective action based on the significance of the issue. Minor, isolated issues may be handled through other processes. But that evaluation must be documented.

**Q3. What makes an "effectiveness verification" acceptable to an auditor?**
*Answer:* Objective evidence — data, records, or audit results — showing the problem has not recurred after the corrective action was implemented. A general statement that the issue is resolved is not sufficient.

**Scenario Q4:** A company receives a complaint that a device label was missing the lot number. They reprint the labels and close the CAPA. Is this adequate? Why or why not?
*Answer:* No. Reprinting labels fixes the immediate problem but doesn't address root cause. Why did the label go out without a lot number? Was it a printing system error? A process gap? An inspection failure? Those questions must be answered, acted on, and verified before the CAPA should be closed.

**Scenario Q5:** Your CAPA has been open for 14 months. Actions were implemented 10 months ago. What's missing?
*Answer:* Effectiveness verification. After implementation, you need to go back — typically after enough time has passed to observe whether the problem has recurred — and document that the corrective action actually worked. Without that step, the CAPA should not be closed.

## How This Connects to Your Career

CAPA is the clause auditors spend the most time on — both FDA investigators and notified body auditors. If you can walk into a room, open a CAPA record, and confidently explain the root cause analysis methodology used, why the actions taken addressed that root cause, and how effectiveness was verified, you are already ahead of most early-career QA professionals. Companies hire and promote people who can run a CAPA investigation without hand-holding, because weak CAPA systems create regulatory risk. Learning this clause deeply — not just the procedure, but the *thinking* behind good root cause analysis — is one of the highest-return investments you can make in your first three years in this industry.
