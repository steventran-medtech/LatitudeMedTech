# ISO 13485:2016 — Clause 8.2.1: Feedback

# ISO 13485:2016 Clause 8.2.1: Feedback

*Educational content only. Always refer to the actual ISO 13485:2016 standard for official requirements.*

---

## What This Clause Is About

Medical devices go out into the real world and get used by real people — and things happen. Clause 8.2.1 requires your company to actively collect and analyze information from post-production use of your devices. The goal is simple: if your product is causing problems or your customers are unhappy, you need to know about it and do something with that information before it becomes a safety issue or a regulatory crisis.

---

## What It Requires (The Essentials)

**1. You must have a documented procedure.**
This isn't optional. You need a written process that describes how feedback is collected, who receives it, how it's reviewed, and what triggers further action.

**2. Feedback must be used as an early warning signal.**
The standard specifically says feedback should feed into your risk management activities and your post-market surveillance system. That means someone needs to actually *analyze* the data, not just file it away.

**3. All sources count.**
Feedback isn't just formal complaints. It includes customer calls, service reports, sales team observations, distributor reports, social media mentions, and data from your own post-market surveillance studies. Your procedure needs to cast a wide net.

**4. There must be a link to Clause 8.2.2 (Complaints).**
Not all feedback is a complaint, but some feedback will trigger the complaint-handling process. Your team needs to know how to triage incoming information and escalate appropriately.

**5. The results must be reviewed.**
Collecting data nobody reads is worthless. Feedback analysis results should flow into your Management Review (Clause 5.6) and your CAPA system when trends or problems emerge.

## What This Looks Like in Practice

Imagine a small company — call them PulseTrack Medical — that makes a wearable cardiac monitor. They have about 60 employees and sell through hospital systems and cardiology clinics.

PulseTrack has a procedure called **SOP-720: Post-Market Feedback Collection and Analysis**. Ownership sits with the **Quality Engineer**, who is supported by their **Customer Service Manager** and the **Field Service team**.

Here's their workflow:

- **Customer Service** logs every call, email, and complaint into a simple CRM (customer relationship management) tool. They use a form called **FM-720-01: Feedback Log** that captures the device model, lot number, what happened, and how the customer described it.
- **Field Service technicians** submit a **Field Service Report (FSR)** after every site visit. That report has a checkbox: *"Any user concerns or product performance issues noted?"* Those reports flow to Quality weekly.
- **Sales reps** are trained during onboarding that if a physician or nurse mentions a product issue — even casually — they fill out a **FM-720-02: Sales Feedback Form** and send it to the Quality inbox.

Once a month, the Quality Engineer pulls all feedback from the past 30 days and runs a **Feedback Trend Analysis**. They look for patterns: Are multiple customers reporting the same error code? Are there more gel adhesion complaints this quarter than last? That analysis is documented in a **Monthly Feedback Summary Report**.

If anything looks like a potential safety issue or a pattern that could indicate a design or manufacturing problem, it gets escalated to a **CAPA**. The Monthly Feedback Summary also goes into the annual **Management Review package**, where leadership reviews it alongside complaint rates, nonconformance data, and audit results.

This isn't glamorous work, but it's exactly what auditors look for — and it's what catches problems before they become field safety corrective actions.

## Common Mistakes and Audit Findings

**1. Treating feedback and complaints as the same thing.**
Many early-career QA professionals set up a complaint-handling process and think that covers feedback. It doesn't. Feedback is broader. A customer saying *"the screen is hard to read in bright light"* isn't a complaint under most definitions, but it's still feedback that could signal a usability problem. Auditors will ask to see your feedback *sources* and your *triage criteria*.

**2. No evidence of analysis.**
Companies collect feedback but can't show they did anything with it. If your auditor asks "what did your feedback tell you this year and how did you use it?" and the answer is "we filed it," that's a finding. You need records showing analysis, conclusions, and decisions.

**3. Siloed information.**
Field service knows about recurring calibration issues. Sales knows customers are frustrated with the setup instructions. Quality doesn't know either thing because there's no defined channel for that information to reach them. This is extremely common at small companies and a frequent observation during notified body audits.

**4. No connection to risk management.**
ISO 13485 explicitly requires that feedback feed into risk management. If your Risk Management File (per ISO 14971) hasn't been touched since product launch, and you've had two years of post-market feedback, that's a gap. Auditors — especially those assessing MDR compliance in the EU — will look for this linkage.

## Key Terms to Know

**Feedback:** Any post-production information about your device, from any source — positive, negative, or neutral.

**Post-Market Surveillance (PMS):** The ongoing, systematic collection and review of device performance data after market release. Feedback is one input into your PMS system.

**Complaint:** A written, electronic, or oral communication alleging a deficiency related to the identity, quality, durability, reliability, safety, effectiveness, or performance of a device. Not all feedback is a complaint, but complaints are a subset of feedback.

**Trend Analysis:** Looking at feedback data over time to identify patterns — for example, noticing that returns for a specific failure mode have increased 30% over three months.

**CAPA (Corrective and Preventive Action):** A structured process for investigating the root cause of a problem and implementing fixes to prevent recurrence. Feedback analysis often triggers CAPAs.

**Management Review:** A formal, periodic leadership meeting required by ISO 13485 where the QMS is evaluated. Feedback data is a required input.

**Risk Management File:** The set of documents (per ISO 14971) that records how risks for a device were identified, evaluated, and controlled. Post-market feedback should update this file when new hazards or risk estimates emerge.

## Check Your Understanding

**1. What is the difference between feedback and a complaint?**
*Answer: Feedback is any post-production information about device performance or use, from any source. A complaint is a specific subset — an allegation of a deficiency related to the device's identity, quality, safety, or performance. All complaints are feedback, but not all feedback is a complaint.*

**2. Why isn't it enough to just collect feedback?**
*Answer: The standard requires analysis and action. Feedback must be evaluated for trends and potential safety signals, and the results must feed into risk management, CAPA, and management review. Collection without analysis doesn't satisfy the clause.*

**3. Which of the following is a valid feedback source: (a) a customer complaint call, (b) a sales rep's notes from a clinic visit, (c) a field service report, (d) all of the above?**
*Answer: (d) All of the above.*

**4. Scenario: A field service tech mentions to the Sales Manager that three different hospitals have reported the same battery drain issue over the past month. The Sales Manager doesn't report it to Quality because "it's not a formal complaint." What's wrong here?**
*Answer: This is exactly the kind of feedback that should be captured and routed to Quality. The absence of a formal complaint doesn't mean the information can be ignored. A recurring issue like this could signal a design or manufacturing problem and should be analyzed and potentially trigger a CAPA.*

**5. Scenario: During a notified body audit, the auditor asks to see evidence that post-market feedback has been used to update risk management. Your feedback logs exist, but the Risk Management File hasn't been reviewed since the device launched three years ago. Is this a problem?**
*Answer: Yes. ISO 13485 requires that feedback feed into risk management activities. If the Risk Management File hasn't been updated to reflect post-market experience, that's a significant gap — and in the EU under MDR requirements, it's very likely to generate a nonconformity finding.*
