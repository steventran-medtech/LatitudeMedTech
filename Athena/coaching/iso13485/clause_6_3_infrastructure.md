# ISO 13485:2016 — Clause 6.3: Infrastructure

# ISO 13485:2016 Clause 6.3: Infrastructure

*Educational content for learning purposes. Always refer to the actual ISO 13485:2016 standard for official requirements.*

---

## What This Clause Is About

Medical devices are only as reliable as the environment in which they're made. Clause 6.3 requires your company to identify, provide, and maintain the physical resources — buildings, equipment, utilities, software — needed to make products that meet specifications and regulatory requirements. Without this clause, a company could build a world-class process on paper and then run it in a facility or with equipment that quietly undermines product quality.

---

## What It Requires (The Essentials)

**1. Identify the infrastructure you need.**
You can't maintain what you haven't defined. The standard requires you to determine what infrastructure is necessary to achieve product conformity. That means thinking through buildings, workspaces, process equipment, utilities (power, water, HVAC), and supporting services like IT systems.

**2. Provide that infrastructure.**
Identifying it isn't enough — you have to actually have it. If your cleanroom classification is required by your device's manufacturing process, that cleanroom needs to exist and be qualified.

**3. Maintain it — and document the maintenance.**
This is where most companies feel the pressure. You need a system for maintaining infrastructure, and you need records proving you did it. A broken centrifuge that sat unrepaired for six weeks, or an HVAC system with no service log, is a direct compliance gap.

**4. Address the impact on product quality.**
The standard specifically calls out that infrastructure maintenance needs to consider the effect on product quality. Routine preventive maintenance (PM) isn't just good operations — it's a quality requirement here.

## What This Looks Like in Practice

Imagine a 60-person medical device company that manufactures disposable surgical kits. Here's how their team actually handles Clause 6.3.

The **Facilities Manager** owns an **Infrastructure Master List** — a controlled document that catalogs every piece of equipment and facility system that touches product quality. This includes the laminar flow hoods in assembly, the torque wrenches used to tighten components, the calibrated temperature loggers in the storage warehouse, and the building's HVAC system that maintains the cleanroom classification.

Each item on the list has a **Preventive Maintenance (PM) schedule** tied to it — monthly, quarterly, or annual intervals based on manufacturer recommendations and internal risk assessment. The PM schedule lives in their **CMMS** (Computerized Maintenance Management System), which automatically generates work orders when maintenance is due. Completed work orders become the maintenance records.

When a piece of equipment goes down unexpectedly — say, a label printer malfunctions — the Facilities Manager opens a **corrective maintenance work order** and, if the downtime could affect released product, notifies the QA Engineer to evaluate whether a nonconformance report needs to be opened.

The **QA Manager** reviews infrastructure records during the annual **Management Review** (Clause 9.3) to look for trends: Are certain equipment types breaking down more than expected? Is PM being completed on time? This closes the loop and feeds back into resource planning.

A new QA Coordinator on the team would likely own the task of tracking PM completion rates, chasing overdue work orders, and updating the Infrastructure Master List when new equipment is purchased or retired.

## Common Mistakes and Audit Findings

**1. The Infrastructure Master List exists but isn't current.**
Companies set up their equipment list during initial certification and then forget to update it. New equipment gets purchased, old equipment is retired, and nobody updates the controlled document. An auditor walking through your facility and spotting equipment that isn't on your list is an easy finding.

**2. PM is scheduled but records don't exist.**
Having a PM schedule in a procedure is not the same as having evidence it was performed. Auditors will ask to see the actual records. "We do it, we just don't always document it" is not an acceptable answer in a QMS.

**3. Software used in the QMS isn't addressed.**
Clause 6.3 covers IT systems and software when they're part of infrastructure supporting product quality — think your document control system, your CMMS, or your label printing software. Companies often focus only on physical equipment and overlook their validated software tools.

**4. No link between infrastructure failures and product impact.**
When a piece of equipment breaks down or a facility parameter goes out of range (like a cleanroom temperature spike), companies sometimes fix the problem without asking whether any product was affected during the failure period. That's a missed step with real regulatory consequences.

## Key Terms to Know

**Infrastructure**: The system of physical resources a company needs to operate — buildings, workspaces, equipment, utilities, and supporting services like IT.

**Preventive Maintenance (PM)**: Scheduled servicing of equipment or facilities to prevent failures before they happen. Driven by a calendar or usage interval.

**Corrective Maintenance**: Unplanned repairs made after a failure occurs.

**CMMS (Computerized Maintenance Management System)**: Software used to track equipment, schedule PM, and store maintenance records. Common examples include Maintenance Connection or eMaint.

**Qualification**: A documented process for confirming that equipment or a facility system performs as expected. Often includes Installation Qualification (IQ), Operational Qualification (OQ), and Performance Qualification (PQ).

**Controlled Document**: Any document that's subject to your document control procedure — meaning it has a version number, requires approval before use, and is managed through a formal change process.

**Management Review**: A periodic, structured meeting (required by Clause 9.3) where top management reviews the health of the QMS, including resources and infrastructure performance.

## Check Your Understanding

**1. What are the three core actions Clause 6.3 requires related to infrastructure?**
*Identify, provide, and maintain the infrastructure needed to achieve product conformity.*

**2. Why isn't a PM schedule in a procedure enough to satisfy an auditor?**
*A procedure documents the intent, but auditors require evidence that PM was actually performed. Records of completed maintenance are required.*

**3. A company buys a new automated assembly machine. What steps should the QA team take to stay compliant with Clause 6.3?**
*Add the machine to the Infrastructure Master List, establish a PM schedule for it, complete any required qualification activities, and ensure it's covered by the maintenance tracking system.*

**4. Scenario: During a third-party audit, the auditor reviews your Infrastructure Master List and then walks your production floor. She points to two pieces of equipment on the floor that aren't on the list. What clause is this a finding against, and why?**
*Clause 6.3, because the company hasn't properly identified all infrastructure required for product conformity. The list is incomplete and not current.*

**5. Scenario: A cleanroom HVAC unit fails on a Tuesday afternoon and is repaired by Wednesday morning. Production ran for four hours during the failure. What should QA do beyond closing the maintenance work order?**
*QA should evaluate whether the HVAC failure caused a deviation from the cleanroom's required environmental conditions, assess any product produced during that window for potential impact on conformity, and open a nonconformance report if product quality could have been affected.*

## How This Connects to Your Career

Infrastructure might feel like "facilities stuff" that belongs to someone else, but QA professionals who understand Clause 6.3 are the ones who catch problems before auditors do. Early in your career, you'll likely be the person tracking PM completion, maintaining equipment lists, or writing the procedure that ties maintenance records to your QMS. Companies that struggle with infrastructure findings often have a gap between operations and quality — and you can be the person who closes it. Demonstrating that you understand how physical resources connect to product quality and regulatory compliance signals real operational maturity to employers, and it's the kind of cross-functional knowledge that moves people from entry-level roles into QMS ownership positions.
