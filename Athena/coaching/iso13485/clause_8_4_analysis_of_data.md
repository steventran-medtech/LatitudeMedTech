# ISO 13485:2016 — Clause 8.4: Analysis of Data

# ISO 13485:2016 Clause 8.4: Analysis of Data

*Educational content for learning purposes. Always refer to the official ISO 13485:2016 standard for complete requirements. This does not constitute regulatory advice.*

---

## What This Clause Is About

Medical device companies collect enormous amounts of data — from customer complaints, audits, supplier performance, and more — but data sitting in a folder does nothing for you. Clause 8.4 requires you to actually analyze that data and use what you learn to confirm your QMS is working, spot problems, and identify where things could get better. Think of it as the clause that forces the organization to pay attention to what its own records are telling it.

---

## What It Requires (The Essentials)

**1. You must collect and analyze appropriate data.**
The standard specifies the minimum data sources you need to pull from: customer feedback, conformance to product requirements (meaning how often your product passes or fails), characteristics and trends of processes and products, and supplier performance. You can't just analyze one or two of these and call it done.

**2. The analysis must demonstrate that your QMS is suitable and effective.**
This is the "so what" requirement. You can't just run numbers — you have to be able to say what the numbers mean about whether your quality system is actually functioning. If your complaint rate went up 30% last quarter, your analysis needs to capture that and trigger a response.

**3. Trends must be identified, including negative trends.**
This is where many companies fall short. A single bad result might not signal a problem. But three bad results in a row absolutely does. You need a method — a control chart, a dashboard, a spreadsheet with thresholds — that lets you see direction, not just snapshots.

**4. Analysis outputs must feed into improvement activities.**
The data analysis isn't an endpoint. Its outputs are supposed to connect to corrective action (Clause 8.5.2), preventive action (Clause 8.5.3), and management review (Clause 5.6). If your analysis never triggers anything downstream, it's probably not working.

**5. Records of analysis must be maintained.**
You need documented evidence that the analysis happened, who did it, and what conclusions were drawn. An undocumented analysis is an analysis that didn't happen in the eyes of an auditor.

## What This Looks Like in Practice

Imagine a 60-person company that makes surgical retractors. Their Quality Engineer — let's call her Maria — runs a monthly data analysis report that gets presented at the quarterly management review.

Maria pulls from four sources each month:

- **Customer Complaint Log** (a controlled spreadsheet in their document system) — she categorizes complaints by product line and failure mode, and tracks the rate per units shipped.
- **Incoming Inspection Records** — she calculates first-pass yield for each of their three major suppliers.
- **Nonconforming Product Log** — she tracks internal rejections by work station to see if any area is drifting.
- **CAPA Log** — she tracks open vs. closed corrective actions and how long each is taking.

She compiles this into a two-page document called the **Monthly Quality Metrics Summary**, which has a simple trend chart for each data source. The form has a field called "Trend Assessment" where she writes whether each metric is stable, improving, or degrading — and a field called "Actions Triggered" where she notes if anything needs a CAPA or supplier review.

When the complaint rate for one retractor model spiked in February, Maria's trend chart flagged it because she had set a threshold — three consecutive months above 0.5% — in the procedure. That triggered a formal CAPA, and the root cause turned out to be a sterilization packaging change from their contract packager. Without the structured analysis, that spike might have been noticed eventually, or it might not.

The procedure that governs all of this is called **QP-008 Analysis of Data**, and it describes what data sources are included, who is responsible, the frequency, and what thresholds trigger escalation.

## Common Mistakes and Audit Findings

**1. Analyzing data in silos, then never connecting the dots.**
A company tracks complaints separately, supplier issues separately, and internal rejections separately — but no one is ever looking at all three together. An auditor will ask you to show how your analysis is comprehensive, and "we have three different spreadsheets" is not a satisfying answer.

**2. Reporting numbers without trend analysis.**
Showing that you had 12 complaints last quarter is data. Showing that complaints went from 4 to 8 to 12 over three quarters — and that you noticed and acted — is analysis. Notified bodies cite this frequently: "records of data analysis present metrics but no evidence of trend evaluation."

**3. Analysis that never triggers anything.**
If your quarterly metrics summary shows degrading supplier performance for six months in a row and there's no corrective action, no supplier audit, no formal response of any kind, an auditor will conclude that your analysis process is cosmetic. The whole point is to drive action.

**4. Vague procedures that don't define thresholds or responsibilities.**
A procedure that says "the quality team will analyze data periodically" is not sufficient. Auditors want to see who, what sources, how often, and what constitutes a signal that something needs escalation.

## Key Terms to Know

**Analysis of data** — The systematic review of collected quality data to draw conclusions about QMS performance and identify opportunities to act.

**Trend** — A consistent directional pattern in data over time, either improving or degrading. One bad data point is an outlier. Three in the same direction is a trend.

**Conformance to product requirements** — Whether your product meets its defined specifications. Tracked through inspection results, test data, and nonconformances.

**Customer feedback** — Any information from customers about their experience with your product, including complaints, compliments, service calls, and returns.

**Supplier performance data** — Objective measurements of how well your suppliers are delivering — on-time delivery rates, incoming inspection pass rates, complaint rates tied to supplier components.

**Management review input** — Clause 5.6 requires management review to include analysis of data outputs. Clause 8.4 generates the content that feeds into that review.

**CAPA (Corrective and Preventive Action)** — The formal process for investigating and fixing problems (corrective) or heading off problems before they occur (preventive). Data analysis is one of the key triggers for initiating a CAPA.

## Check Your Understanding

**1. What are the four minimum data sources Clause 8.4 requires you to analyze?**
*Answer: Customer feedback, conformance to product requirements, characteristics and trends of processes and products, and supplier performance.*

**2. What's the difference between reporting data and analyzing data?**
*Answer: Reporting gives you numbers. Analysis interprets those numbers — identifying trends, comparing against thresholds, and drawing conclusions about whether the QMS is functioning effectively.*

**3. Scenario: Your company's monthly report shows complaint rates of 0.3%, 0.6%, and 0.9% over three consecutive months for one product. The quality manager says, "It's still under 1%, so we're fine." What should concern you about this response?**
*Answer: The consistent upward trend is the problem, not just the absolute number. Even if the current value is below a threshold, a tripling of the complaint rate over three months should trigger investigation — this is exactly what trend analysis is designed to catch.*

**4. Why does it matter that data analysis outputs connect to management review and CAPA?**
*Answer: Because analysis without action has no value to the QMS. The standard requires that analysis drive improvement. If it's disconnected from CAPA and management review, it becomes a compliance exercise rather than a real tool.*

**5. Scenario: During an audit, the auditor asks to see your data analysis procedure. Your procedure says "quality data will be reviewed as needed by the quality department." What's the auditor likely to flag, and why?**
*Answer: The procedure is too vague. It doesn't specify which data sources are included, who exactly is responsible, how frequently analysis occurs, or what thresholds trigger escalation. Auditors need to see that your process is defined well enough to be consistently followed.*
