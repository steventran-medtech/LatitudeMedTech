# ISO 13485:2016 — Clause 7.5: Production and Service Provision

# ISO 13485:2016 Clause 7.5: Production and Service Provision

*Educational content only. Always refer to the actual ISO 13485:2016 standard for official requirements.*

---

## What This Clause Is About

Clause 7.5 governs how medical devices are actually made and serviced. It exists to answer a straightforward question: how does a company ensure that every unit coming off the line (or out of the service department) is built the same way, with the right materials, by qualified people, under controlled conditions? Without this clause, you'd have chaos — different technicians doing things differently, no records proving what happened, and no way to trace a defect back to its source.

---

## What It Requires (The Essentials)

**1. Controlled conditions for manufacturing**
You must have documented procedures, work instructions, and specifications in place before production starts — not improvised on the floor. This includes defining the right equipment, the right environment (temperature, cleanliness, humidity where relevant), and the acceptance criteria that tell workers when something passes or fails.

**2. Cleanliness and contamination control**
If your device contacts the patient, you need controls for how it's handled and packaged. This includes clean room requirements, gowning procedures, and rules about what can and can't enter a production area.

**3. Installation and servicing activities**
If your company installs devices at customer sites or provides field service, those activities need the same level of control as manufacturing. You need documented procedures, trained personnel, and records of what was done.

**4. Validation of processes where output can't be fully verified**
Some processes can't be checked just by looking at the finished product — sterile barrier sealing is a classic example. You can't see whether a seal will hold under real-world conditions just by inspecting it. Those processes must be validated (proven to work consistently) before you rely on them.

**5. Traceability and identification throughout production**
Every product needs to be identified at every stage — what it is, whether it passed inspection, and what materials went into it. This is what allows you to run an effective recall if something goes wrong.

## What This Looks Like in Practice

Imagine a 60-person company that makes infusion pumps. Their production floor has a Manufacturing Engineer who writes and owns the **Device History Record (DHR)** package — a set of documents that travels with each unit through production. Inside that package are work instructions, a traveler (a checklist technicians sign off at each step), and test data sheets.

Before a new technician touches a unit, they go through documented training and a skills qualification sign-off kept by the QA team. The work instructions on the floor are controlled copies — printed from the document management system with a header showing the current revision. If a technician grabs an old printout from a drawer, that's a nonconformance.

The company also does final seal validation on their packaging line every year. The Process Engineer runs burst tests and seal integrity tests across a range of conditions, documents the results in a **Process Validation Report**, and QA approves it before the line runs. If they change packaging suppliers, they revalidate — because the process changed.

When a pump goes out for field service, the Field Service team uses a **Service Order** that captures what was done, what parts were replaced (with lot numbers), and what functional tests were performed post-service. That record gets filed and tied to the device's serial number.

## Common Mistakes and Audit Findings

**1. Work instructions that don't match what's actually happening on the floor**
This is the most common finding. The document says torque the screw to 2 N·m; the technician has been doing it by feel for years. Auditors will watch operators work and then pull up the procedure. If they don't match, you have a problem.

**2. Missing or incomplete DHR entries**
Skipped signatures, blank fields, missing test data — DHRs get rushed at end of month when shipment pressure is high. A single missing entry can put a batch on hold during an audit.

**3. Unvalidated processes being treated as verified**
Teams sometimes assume that 100% inspection is good enough for processes that actually require validation. Inspecting every seal doesn't prove the sealing process is in control — validation does.

**4. Service records that are vague or missing**
"Repaired pump per customer complaint" is not an adequate service record. Auditors want to see exactly what was found, what was done, what parts were used, and what verification was performed before returning the device to service.

## Key Terms to Know

**Device History Record (DHR):** The collection of records that proves a specific device was manufactured according to its Device Master Record. Think of it as the birth certificate for each unit.

**Device Master Record (DMR):** The complete set of specifications and procedures that define how a device is supposed to be made. The DHR shows it was actually made that way.

**Traveler:** A shop floor document that follows a unit through production, capturing each step, who did it, and whether it passed.

**Process Validation:** Documented evidence that a process consistently produces a result that meets its requirements — required when you can't fully verify the output by inspection alone.

**Cleanliness Requirements:** Defined standards for the environment where a device is manufactured — can range from basic housekeeping rules to classified clean rooms.

**Traceability:** The ability to link a finished device back to the raw materials, components, personnel, and processes that created it. Critical for effective recalls.

**Acceptance Criteria:** The specific, measurable standards a product or process output must meet to be considered acceptable. "Looks good" is not acceptance criteria.

## Check Your Understanding

**Q1. What's the difference between a DHR and a DMR?**
The DMR is the recipe; the DHR is the proof you followed it. The DMR contains specifications and procedures. The DHR contains records for a specific manufactured unit showing it was built to those specs.

**Q2. Why can't you simply inspect your way through a sterilization process instead of validating it?**
Because you can't verify sterility by looking at the product. You can't test every unit destructively. Validation proves the process itself reliably achieves the required outcome under defined conditions.

**Q3. Scenario: A field service technician replaces a component in a patient monitor and writes "replaced board, tested OK" in the service order. Is this adequate? Why or why not?**
No. It lacks specifics: which board, what lot number, what tests were performed, what the test results were, and who performed the work. An auditor — or worse, an investigator — can't reconstruct what happened from this record.

**Q4. What does "controlled conditions" mean in the context of production?**
Defined procedures, qualified personnel, appropriate equipment, suitable environment, and acceptance criteria — all in place and documented before production begins.

**Q5. Scenario: Your company changes its assembly adhesive supplier mid-year. A technician says the new adhesive "works the same." What should happen before production continues?**
A change control process should be initiated. Depending on how the adhesive is used, the bonding process may require revalidation to confirm the new adhesive performs consistently under production conditions.
