# The Quality Policy That Lived in a Binder and Never in the Building

*ISO 13485:2016 · Clause 5.3: Quality Policy*

---

## The Incident

In 2019, FDA investigators issued a Warning Letter to a small (~60-person) cardiovascular device manufacturer in the Midwest producing cardiac rhythm monitoring equipment. Among the observations: the company's quality policy had not been communicated to employees at relevant levels, and there was no objective evidence that personnel understood it or that management reviewed it for continuing suitability.

When investigators interviewed production staff, assemblers couldn't describe the company's quality commitments. One supervisor referenced a version of the policy that had been superseded two years earlier. The document existed — framed, laminated, posted in the lobby — but it was functionally invisible to the people building the devices.

The consequence wasn't just a citation. It fed into a broader pattern of QMS breakdown. Combined with design control and CAPA deficiencies, the company received a consent decree restricting manufacturing operations for 14 months. Revenue loss ran into the millions.

The quality policy failure wasn't the proximate cause of the consent decree, but it was a signal the investigators read correctly: this was a QMS that existed on paper, not in practice.

## What ISO 13485:2016 §5.3 Actually Requires

Clause 5.3 sits under top management responsibility. Here's what you actually need to make happen:

1. **Write a policy that fits your organization.** It must be appropriate to your company's purpose. A startup making Class II wound care dressings has different strategic context than a 500-person implantable device manufacturer.

2. **Commit to requirements and QMS maintenance.** The policy must include a commitment to meeting applicable requirements and maintaining the effectiveness of the QMS — not just complying with ISO 13485, but including customer and regulatory requirements.

3. **Set up a framework for quality objectives.** The policy doesn't have to list every objective, but it must provide the foundation for setting them. Think of it as the "why" that drives the measurable "what."

4. **Communicate it.** This is the step that breaks down most often. Personnel at all relevant levels must understand it — not just receive it in onboarding paperwork.

5. **Review it.** Top management must confirm it's still relevant. Markets change. Regulations change. Your policy can't be frozen in 2017 while your product portfolio expands.

## What Went Wrong — Clause Analysis

The cardiac monitoring company violated multiple elements of §5.3 simultaneously, which is common because they're connected.

**Communication failure (§5.3 + §5.3c):** The standard requires that the policy be communicated and understood within the organization. Production staff not being able to describe quality commitments is a direct citation target. Posting a laminated document doesn't satisfy "communicated and understood."

**Outdated policy in use (§5.3d):** Supervisors referencing a superseded version signals that the review requirement — confirming continuing suitability — wasn't functioning. Management review should catch this. The fact that it didn't indicates the management review process (§5.6) was also weak, but the policy failure is the visible symptom.

**Disconnect between policy and practice:** The policy wasn't shaping behavior. That's the real failure. When §5.3 works, employees can connect their daily decisions to the organization's quality commitments. Here, the policy was decorative.

## A Second Scenario

LifeFlow Diagnostics is a 35-person startup that just received 510(k) clearance for a point-of-care urinalysis analyzer. They're preparing for their first ISO 13485 certification audit. Their QA engineer, Priya, is 18 months into her first industry role.

Priya's manager hands her the quality policy draft — three paragraphs, generic language about "commitment to quality and customer satisfaction," borrowed heavily from an online template. No mention of regulatory compliance. No mention of their specific device context. It was written for a company that makes industrial sensors.

Priya flags it. Her manager says, "Auditors just want to see that it exists."

Priya pushes back with specifics. She rewrites the policy to reference LifeFlow's commitment to meeting FDA 21 CFR Part 820 requirements, customer needs in clinical settings, and maintaining QMS effectiveness as the company scales. She then schedules a 20-minute all-hands where the CEO reads the policy aloud and explains why each commitment matters to their device — and to patients.

At the certification audit, the auditor interviews two lab technicians. Both can describe the core commitments and connect them to their work. The auditor notes it favorably in the audit report.

Three months later, when a complaint triggers a CAPA, both the technicians and Priya's team frame their investigation against the quality commitments in the policy. That's the policy doing its job.

## What Auditors Look For

Auditors — both FDA investigators and notified body auditors — are pattern-matching for disconnects between the documented policy and organizational behavior.

1. **Personnel interviews that don't match the document.** Auditors will pull a production operator, a design engineer, and a supplier quality person into separate rooms and ask: "What is your company's quality policy?" Inconsistent or blank answers are a direct finding.

2. **No evidence of communication.** Training records that show the policy was distributed aren't sufficient if there's no evidence employees understood it. Some auditors ask for sign-off records with comprehension confirmation, or training assessments.

3. **Policy not reviewed as part of management review.** During management review record examination, auditors check whether suitability of the quality policy was an agenda item. Missing this is a routine minor finding that accumulates badly.

4. **Policy language that doesn't reflect the regulatory environment.** A quality policy for a company making IVDs that doesn't reference applicable regulatory requirements (e.g., IVDR, 21 CFR Part 820) looks like a copy-paste job — and signals the QMS may not be substantively built for the actual product.

## Key Terms

**Quality Policy:** A formal statement from top management expressing the organization's overall intentions and direction related to quality. Example: A pacemaker manufacturer's quality policy might commit to meeting ISO 13485, FDA QSR, and patient safety standards.

**Top Management:** The person or group that directs and controls the organization at the highest level. In a 25-person startup, this is often the CEO and one or two VPs. They must own the quality policy — not delegate its creation to QA and sign it without reading it.

**Continuing Suitability:** Whether the policy still makes sense given where the company is today. Example: A company that added a software-as-a-medical-device (SaMD) product line should update a policy that only references hardware manufacturing.

**Quality Objectives:** Measurable goals derived from the quality policy. The policy says "we commit to continuous improvement"; a quality objective says "reduce customer complaint rate by 15% in 12 months." The policy is the foundation.

**Communicated and Understood:** ISO 13485's standard for policy deployment — not just sent, but confirmed understood. Example: A training record with a quiz question about the policy commits this to documentation.

**QMS Effectiveness:** Whether the quality management system actually achieves its intended results — safe, compliant devices. The policy must commit to maintaining this, not just maintaining the QMS's existence.

**Management Review:** The periodic structured review (§5.6) where top management evaluates the QMS, including the quality policy's continued relevance. This is where policy suitability gets formally assessed.

## Check Your Understanding

**Q1. In the cardiac monitoring case, what was the most direct §5.3 violation?**
**A:** Failure to communicate and ensure understanding of the quality policy among personnel at relevant levels. Production staff couldn't describe quality commitments, and supervisors were using a superseded version.

**Q2. Priya's manager said "auditors just want to see that it exists." What's the specific risk with that approach?**
**A:** A policy that exists but isn't communicated, understood, or tailored to the organization's context will fail personnel interviews during an audit. It also won't drive behavior — which means the QMS is documentation theater, not a functional system.

**Q3. What is the relationship between the quality policy and quality objectives?**
**A:** The quality policy provides the framework — the commitments and direction — from which quality objectives are derived. Objectives must be consistent with the policy. A policy that commits to regulatory compliance should generate objectives around complaint rates, CAPA closure timelines, or audit findings.

**Q4. A notified body auditor asks your production supervisor to describe the company's quality policy. The supervisor says "we make safe products." Is this sufficient?**
**A:** No. While it's directionally correct, it doesn't reflect the actual commitments in the documented policy (regulatory requirements, QMS maintenance, continuous improvement framework). This response would likely trigger a finding about communication and understanding. The supervisor should be able to reference the policy's core statements specifically.

## Career Impact

Quality professionals who own Clause 5.3 early in their careers develop a skill that most of their peers skip: the ability to connect documentation to culture. When you're the person who rewrites a generic policy to actually reflect your company's device, regulatory context, and patient commitment — and then builds a communication plan that makes it real for production staff — you're doing something most QA coordinators never do. You become someone leadership trusts with cross-functional work, because you understand that compliance isn't a paperwork exercise. That reputation compounds fast. The quality policy is a two-page document, but knowing how to activate it makes you the engineer who turns a QMS from a binder into a system that actually works.
