# ISO 13485:2016 — Clause 5.3: Quality Policy

# ISO 13485:2016 Clause 5.3: Quality Policy

*Educational content only. Always refer to the actual ISO 13485:2016 standard for official requirements.*

---

## What This Clause Is About

A quality policy is the organization's written commitment to quality — it tells everyone, from the CEO to a new line operator, what the company actually stands for when it comes to making safe, effective medical devices. Without it, quality becomes abstract and inconsistent, something different people define differently. This clause ensures that commitment exists, is communicated, and is kept current.

---

## What It Requires (The Essentials)

**1. Top management owns this.**
The quality policy must be established by top management — not the QA team, not a consultant. The CEO or equivalent has to drive it. This matters because quality decisions at the top of an organization affect everything downstream.

**2. It must fit the organization's purpose.**
A startup making single-use surgical drapes and a mid-size company making implantable cardiac monitors have very different contexts. The policy has to reflect what your company actually does, not just copy a generic template from the internet.

**3. It must include a commitment to comply with requirements and maintain the QMS.**
This sounds obvious, but it must be explicitly stated in the document. "We make great products" doesn't cut it. The policy needs to reference compliance with applicable requirements (including ISO 13485) and ongoing improvement of the QMS.

**4. It must provide a framework for setting quality objectives.**
Think of the quality policy as the headline and quality objectives as the specific, measurable goals that flow from it. The policy doesn't set the objectives itself, but it should make clear what direction the objectives should point in.

**5. It must be communicated, understood, and reviewed.**
Printing the policy and putting it in a binder in a back office doesn't count. Employees need to actually understand it, and leadership needs to revisit it periodically to make sure it still makes sense for the business.

## What This Looks Like in Practice

Imagine a 60-person company called Summit Medical Devices that manufactures orthopedic trauma fixation plates. Their quality policy is a single page, approved and signed by their CEO. It reads something like: *"Summit Medical Devices is committed to designing and manufacturing orthopedic fixation devices that meet the needs of surgeons and patients, comply with applicable regulatory requirements, and continuously improve our quality management system."*

That document lives in their QMS as a controlled document — let's say it's titled **QP-001 Quality Policy** — and it's version-controlled just like any other procedure. The Quality Manager is responsible for maintaining it, but any revision requires the CEO's signature.

New employees see the quality policy on day one during onboarding. The HR/QA team walks through it briefly and employees sign an acknowledgment form. The policy is also posted in the production area and the main conference room.

Once a year, during management review (a formal meeting where leadership evaluates how the QMS is performing), the team revisits the policy. The Quality Manager prepares a one-page summary asking: does this policy still reflect what we do? Are there new regulatory requirements? Has the product line changed? If something's off, they initiate a document change request and the CEO signs the updated version.

## Common Mistakes and Audit Findings

**1. Policy written by QA, not owned by leadership.**
Auditors look at whether top management can actually speak to the quality policy. If your CEO says "I think QA handles all that," that's a red flag. Leadership has to genuinely own it, not just sign it.

**2. Policy is too vague to mean anything.**
Statements like "We are committed to quality" without any reference to regulatory compliance, customer focus, or QMS maintenance are often flagged. Vague language passes a quick read but fails under scrutiny.

**3. No evidence of communication or understanding.**
A notified body auditor might ask a production technician: "Can you tell me what your company's quality policy says?" If the answer is a blank stare, that's a finding. The company can't just post the policy — there has to be evidence of training and communication.

**4. Policy hasn't been reviewed in years.**
If your company added a new product line, entered a new market, or had significant regulatory changes, and the quality policy hasn't been touched since 2019, an auditor will ask why. Outdated policies signal a passive, reactive QMS.

## Key Terms to Know

**Quality Policy** — A formal, documented statement from top management expressing the organization's overall intentions and direction related to quality.

**Top Management** — The person or group that directs and controls the organization at the highest level (e.g., CEO, President, executive leadership team).

**QMS (Quality Management System)** — The complete set of policies, processes, and procedures a company uses to ensure their products consistently meet quality and regulatory requirements.

**Quality Objectives** — Specific, measurable goals that the organization sets to pursue quality. They should flow from the quality policy.

**Controlled Document** — A document managed under a formal process that tracks version history, approvals, and distribution to ensure everyone is working from the current version.

**Management Review** — A scheduled, formal meeting where top management evaluates the performance of the QMS and makes decisions about resources and improvements.

**Applicable Requirements** — Regulations, standards, and customer requirements that the company must follow — for example, ISO 13485, FDA 21 CFR Part 820, or EU MDR.

## Check Your Understanding

**1. Who is responsible for establishing the quality policy under ISO 13485?**
*Answer: Top management. Not the QA department, not a consultant. Leadership at the highest level of the organization owns this.*

**2. Your company's quality policy states: "We are dedicated to delivering excellence." Does this meet the requirements of Clause 5.3?**
*Answer: No. It doesn't include a commitment to comply with applicable requirements or maintain the QMS, and it doesn't provide a framework for quality objectives. It's too vague.*

**3. What is the relationship between the quality policy and quality objectives?**
*Answer: The quality policy sets the direction and framework; quality objectives are the specific, measurable targets that the organization sets in alignment with that direction. You need the policy first.*

**4. Scenario: During an internal audit, you interview a receiving inspector who has been with the company for two years. She doesn't know what the quality policy says and has never seen it. What clause might this create a finding against, and what would you recommend?**
*Answer: This is a Clause 5.3 finding related to communication and understanding of the quality policy. Recommend adding the quality policy to onboarding training, implementing periodic refreshers, and documenting evidence of employee acknowledgment.*

**5. Scenario: A notified body auditor asks your CEO to walk her through why the quality policy was last updated three years ago, even though the company launched two new product categories last year. What's the likely concern?**
*Answer: The auditor is questioning whether the policy still fits the organization's purpose and reflects current scope. The likely finding is that the policy wasn't reviewed following significant changes to the business, which is required under the clause.*

## How This Connects to Your Career

Understanding Clause 5.3 makes you better at your job in a specific way: it trains you to think about quality as something leadership-driven, not just paperwork-driven. Early in your career, it's easy to treat the quality policy as a formality — just another document to file. But when you understand why it exists, you become the person in the room who can connect a corrective action or a quality objective back to what the company actually committed to at the top level. Employers value QA/RA professionals who understand the *why* behind the system, not just the *what*, because those are the people who help leadership make better decisions rather than just maintain compliance checklists.
