# ISO 13485:2016 — Clause 7.4: Purchasing

# ISO 13485:2016 Clause 7.4: Purchasing

*Educational content only. Always refer to the actual ISO 13485:2016 standard for official requirements.*

---

## What This Clause Is About

Medical devices are only as good as the components and services that go into them. Clause 7.4 ensures your company has a controlled process for selecting suppliers, telling them exactly what you need, and verifying they actually delivered it. Without this, a single bad supplier can compromise your entire product — and your patients.

---

## What It Requires (The Essentials)

**1. You must evaluate and select suppliers based on their ability to meet your requirements.**
You can't just pick the cheapest vendor. Before you buy anything that affects product quality, you need documented evidence that the supplier can actually deliver what you need. This means defining criteria for how you evaluate them.

**2. You must maintain a list of approved suppliers.**
This is typically called an Approved Supplier List (ASL). Only suppliers on this list should be used for purchasing items that affect product quality. If a supplier isn't on the list, you can't use them without going through the approval process first.

**3. You must define the extent of control based on risk.**
Not all suppliers are equal. A supplier providing custom sterile packaging for an implant gets much tighter oversight than a supplier providing office furniture. Your level of control should match the risk that supplier's product or service poses to your device's safety and effectiveness.

**4. Purchasing documents must clearly describe what you're ordering.**
Purchase orders and specifications need enough detail that the supplier knows exactly what to deliver. This includes product specifications, quality requirements, and any regulatory requirements that apply.

**5. You must verify that purchased products and services meet your requirements.**
Receiving inspection — checking what arrived against what you ordered — is required. How rigorous that inspection is depends on your risk assessment and your supplier's track record.

## What This Looks Like in Practice

Imagine a 40-person company called Meridian Devices that makes a Class II orthopedic instrument set. Here's how their team handles purchasing in the real world.

The **Regulatory Affairs/Quality Engineer** maintains a document called *QP-024 Supplier Qualification and Monitoring Procedure*. This procedure defines three supplier tiers based on risk: Critical suppliers (components used directly in the finished device), Major suppliers (packaging, calibration services), and Minor suppliers (non-product items like janitorial services — minimal oversight required).

When the engineering team wants to use a new contract machinist for titanium instrument components, the Quality Engineer opens a **Supplier Qualification Request**. They send the machinist a **Supplier Questionnaire** and request copies of their ISO 9001 certificate, inspection reports, and a sample part. The Quality team scores the responses against documented criteria. If the machinist passes, they get added to the **Approved Supplier List (ASL)** — a controlled spreadsheet showing supplier name, category, scope of approval, approval date, and re-evaluation due date.

The **Purchasing Coordinator** is trained on a simple rule: before issuing a Purchase Order, check the ASL. If the supplier isn't listed, stop and notify Quality. Purchase Orders for critical components reference the specific drawing number, revision level, material specification, and any required certifications (like a Certificate of Conformance).

When parts arrive, the **Receiving Inspector** pulls up *QP-031 Receiving Inspection Procedure*, checks the packing slip against the PO, and performs dimensional checks per the inspection plan. Results get recorded on a **Receiving Inspection Record**, which is filed and linked to the batch record.

Every year, the Quality Engineer pulls supplier performance data — nonconformances, late deliveries, failed receiving inspections — and conducts a **Supplier Re-evaluation**. A machinist with three escapes in one year might get downgraded or removed from the ASL entirely.

That's the full loop: qualify, approve, specify, receive, verify, monitor.

## Common Mistakes and Audit Findings

**1. The Approved Supplier List isn't current.**
A supplier was added two years ago but never re-evaluated. Or a supplier the company stopped using is still listed as active. Auditors will ask to trace a recent purchase back to the ASL, and gaps here fail fast.

**2. Purchase orders lack sufficient technical detail.**
A PO that says "titanium rod, 10mm" without referencing the drawing number and revision level isn't adequate. If your supplier ships to the wrong revision and you didn't specify it, that's on you.

**3. Risk-based supplier tiering exists on paper but not in practice.**
The procedure says you'll control suppliers differently based on risk, but in reality every supplier gets the same three-question questionnaire. Auditors look for evidence that your highest-risk suppliers actually receive more scrutiny.

**4. Receiving inspection records are incomplete or missing.**
"We checked it when it came in" isn't a quality record. You need documented evidence of what was checked, what criteria were used, who did it, and what the result was.

## Key Terms to Know

**Approved Supplier List (ASL):** A controlled document listing all suppliers authorized to provide materials or services that affect product quality.

**Supplier Qualification:** The process of evaluating a new supplier to determine whether they can consistently meet your requirements before you start buying from them.

**Purchasing Document:** Any document that communicates your requirements to a supplier — including purchase orders, specifications, and contracts.

**Certificate of Conformance (CoC):** A document from the supplier stating that the delivered product meets specified requirements. Often required as part of receiving inspection for critical components.

**Receiving Inspection:** The process of checking incoming goods or services against your purchase requirements before they're accepted into inventory or used in production.

**Re-evaluation:** Periodic reassessment of an existing supplier's performance to confirm they still meet your standards and should remain on the ASL.

**Risk-Based Control:** Applying more rigorous oversight to suppliers whose products or failures pose greater risk to device safety or quality.

## Check Your Understanding

**1. Why does ISO 13485 require risk-based supplier controls rather than treating all suppliers the same?**
*Answer:* Because the impact of a supplier failure varies dramatically. A component supplier for an implantable device has far more potential to harm a patient than a supplier providing shipping boxes. Matching your control level to the actual risk makes your QMS efficient and appropriate.

**2. A new engineer places a rush order with a machine shop that isn't on your ASL because "we've used them before informally." What's the problem?**
*Answer:* Past informal use doesn't equal qualification. Without an approved supplier record, there's no documented evidence the supplier meets your quality requirements. This could be a nonconformance finding in an audit and could introduce uncontrolled risk into your product.

**3. What is the purpose of a Certificate of Conformance?**
*Answer:* It's a supplier's written declaration that the delivered product meets the specified requirements. It's one tool used during receiving inspection, though it doesn't replace physical inspection for high-risk components.

**4. True or False: Receiving inspection always requires physical measurement of every part.**
*Answer:* False. The extent of receiving inspection depends on your risk assessment and supplier performance history. A well-established critical supplier with a strong track record might allow reduced sampling. A new supplier or one with recent escapes warrants more intensive inspection.

**5. Your company's ASL shows a packaging supplier last re-evaluated 18 months ago, and your procedure requires annual re-evaluation. An audit is next week. What should you do?**
*Answer:* Initiate the re-evaluation immediately and document it. Notify your Quality Manager so they're aware of the gap. Be transparent with the auditor — attempting to hide it is worse than the finding itself. Use this as an opportunity to add ASL re-evaluation due dates to a quality calendar so it doesn't slip again.

## How This Connects to Your Career

Supplier management is one of the most visible and recurring parts of a QA/RA professional's daily work. Companies that scale — adding new suppliers, expanding into new markets, launching new products — have to run this process constantly, and they need people who understand it deeply. If you can walk into a supplier audit, evaluate a qualification package, write a clear purchasing specification, and explain to a procurement team *why* these controls exist, you become the kind of cross-functional partner that both Quality and Operations actually want in the room. Many early-career professionals know the theory but stumble on the practical handoffs between purchasing, receiving, and quality. Master those connections and you'll stand out immediately.
