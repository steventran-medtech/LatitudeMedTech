# ISO 13485:2016 — Clause 5.6: Management Review

# ISO 13485:2016 Clause 5.6: Management Review

*Educational content only. Always refer to the actual ISO 13485:2016 standard for official requirements.*

---

## What This Clause Is About

Leadership at a medical device company can get consumed by day-to-day fires and lose sight of whether the quality system is actually working. Management Review is the structured checkpoint that forces senior leaders to step back, look at real data, and make decisions about whether the QMS needs resources, changes, or improvements. Without it, you get a quality system that exists on paper but doesn't evolve.

---

## What It Requires (The Essentials)

**1. It has to happen at planned intervals.**
Top management must review the QMS regularly — not just when an audit is coming. Most companies do this annually, some quarterly. The schedule must be defined in advance.

**2. Specific inputs are required.**
You can't just hold a meeting and call it a management review. The standard specifies what data must be brought to the table. This includes audit results, customer feedback, product performance data, corrective actions, regulatory changes, and others. If key inputs are missing, the review is incomplete — even if everyone showed up.

**3. Outputs must include decisions and actions.**
The meeting must produce documented decisions about improvements to the QMS, improvements to products and processes, and resource needs. Vague discussion doesn't count. You need documented action items with owners and due dates.

**4. Records must be maintained.**
The entire review — inputs, discussion, decisions, and action items — must be documented. This record is one of the first things an auditor will ask for.

**5. Top management must actually be involved.**
This isn't a meeting the QA Manager runs alone. The standard requires top management participation, meaning the people with authority to allocate resources and make organizational decisions.

## What This Looks Like in Practice

At a 45-person company that makes infusion pumps, the QA Manager (let's call her Sarah) owns the Management Review process. About six weeks before the annual review, she sends a data-collection request to department heads: Complaints from Customer Service, CAPA status from QA, audit results from the internal audit program, regulatory updates from RA, supplier performance metrics from Operations.

Sarah compiles everything into a **Management Review Input Report** — a 15-20 page document that summarizes each required input with trend data and context. She's not making decisions; she's organizing the picture so leadership can.

The meeting itself includes the CEO, VP of Operations, VP of Engineering, and the RA Director. It runs about three hours. Sarah facilitates and takes notes, but she's not the decision-maker in the room.

What comes out the other side is a **Management Review Minutes and Action Log** — a document that captures what was discussed, what decisions were made ("we're adding a second QA auditor headcount in Q2"), and what follow-up actions are assigned, to whom, and by when. That document gets signed by the CEO and stored in the QMS.

At the next management review, one of the first agenda items is reviewing whether last year's action items were actually completed. That closed loop is what makes the process real rather than ceremonial.

## Common Mistakes and Audit Findings

**1. Treating it as a checkbox exercise.**
The most common finding: the meeting happened, records exist, but the outputs are generic ("continue to monitor quality metrics"). No specific decisions, no resource commitments, no meaningful action items. Auditors recognize this immediately. A management review with no actions is a red flag that leadership isn't truly engaged.

**2. Missing required inputs.**
Early-career professionals often help compile the input package and don't realize there's a specific list in the standard. If regulatory changes, post-market surveillance data, or supplier performance are missing, the review is incomplete — even if it looked thorough internally. Know the full input list cold.

**3. Actions that never close.**
Management reviews generate action items. Those action items need to actually get completed and verified. A common finding is that action items from the previous review are still open with no documented follow-up. If you're assigned an action, treat it like a CAPA.

**4. Wrong people in the room.**
Some companies have the QA team run the review without executive participation, then get a signature after the fact. The standard requires top management to *participate*, not just approve. An auditor may ask who attended and what they contributed.

## Key Terms to Know

**Top Management:** The person or group that directs and controls the organization at the highest level — typically the CEO, President, or equivalent. This is specifically who the standard holds responsible for Management Review.

**QMS (Quality Management System):** The complete set of policies, procedures, processes, and records a company uses to ensure product quality and regulatory compliance.

**Management Review Inputs:** The specific data categories the standard requires you to bring into the review — audit results, customer feedback, process performance, CAPA status, regulatory changes, etc.

**Management Review Outputs:** The documented decisions and actions that result from the review — resource allocations, improvement decisions, changes to quality objectives.

**CAPA (Corrective and Preventive Action):** A formal process to identify the root cause of problems and fix them so they don't recur. CAPA status is a required input to Management Review.

**Post-Market Surveillance (PMS):** Ongoing monitoring of a device's performance after it's on the market — including complaints, adverse events, and field data. This feeds into Management Review inputs.

**Quality Objectives:** Measurable goals tied to quality performance (e.g., complaint rate below 0.5%). Management Review evaluates progress against these.

## Check Your Understanding

**1. Who is required to participate in Management Review?**
Top management — meaning the executives with authority to make organizational decisions and allocate resources, not just the QA team.

**2. What is the difference between Management Review inputs and outputs?**
Inputs are the data brought *into* the review (audit results, complaints, CAPA status, etc.). Outputs are the decisions and action items that come *out* of the review.

**3. A Management Review was held, and the minutes state "the QMS is functioning adequately — no changes needed." Is this acceptable?**
Probably not. While it's possible that no major changes are needed, a review that produces no actions at all is a red flag. Auditors expect to see active decision-making, resource discussions, and quality objective evaluations — even if the conclusion is that existing programs are working well, that conclusion should be supported by specific data.

**4. Scenario: You're preparing the Management Review input package and realize no one collected supplier performance data. The meeting is in two weeks. What do you do?**
Contact the Operations or Procurement team immediately to pull the data. If it genuinely can't be ready in time, flag this to the QA Manager before the meeting — not during. A missing required input should be documented and addressed, not silently omitted.

**5. Scenario: An action item from last year's Management Review was assigned to the VP of Engineering and was never completed. The new review is coming up. How should this be handled?**
It must be addressed openly at the new review. The status should be reported in the input package, and leadership needs to either close it with evidence of completion or make a documented decision about its disposition. Pretending it doesn't exist is not an option.

## How This Connects to Your Career

Early-career QA/RA professionals often underestimate how visible Management Review work is to leadership. When you compile the input package, write the minutes, or track action items, you're producing documents that the CEO signs and auditors scrutinize. Doing this well — making sure inputs are complete, outputs are specific, and actions actually close — demonstrates exactly the kind of system-level thinking that separates professionals who grow quickly from those who stay in task-execution mode. Companies preparing for ISO 13485 certification or facing notified body surveillance audits lean heavily on the people who own this process. If that person is you, and you do it right, you become indispensable.
