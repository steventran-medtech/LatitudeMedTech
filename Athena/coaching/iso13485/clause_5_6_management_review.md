# The Annual Meeting That Never Happened — How a Glucose Monitor Maker's Missing Management Reviews Led to an FDA Warning Letter

*ISO 13485:2016 · Clause 5.6: Management Review*

---

## The Incident

In 2019, FDA investigators issued a Warning Letter to a small medical device manufacturer producing continuous glucose monitors (CGMs) — roughly 80 employees, FDA-registered, with an established QMS on paper. During a routine inspection, investigators found that the company had not conducted a formal management review in over 22 months. Their procedure required reviews at least annually.

What made it worse: during those 22 months, the company had accumulated 47 customer complaints related to sensor adhesion failures, three field safety corrective actions, and a spike in nonconforming product at final inspection. None of this data had been formally reviewed by top management as a system. Individual department heads had handled complaints and corrections in silos — but no one had ever stepped back to assess whether the QMS itself was working.

FDA's 483 observation read, in part: *"Management review records do not demonstrate that top management reviews the quality management system at planned intervals to ensure its continuing suitability, adequacy, and effectiveness."*

The consequence: a public Warning Letter, a halt on new product submissions, and a third-party auditor mandated before the company could resume normal regulatory activity.

## What ISO 13485:2016 §5.6 Actually Requires

Think of Clause 5.6 as the annual physical for your QMS. Top management — not just QA staff — must sit down at planned intervals and honestly evaluate whether the quality system is working.

Here's what that means in practice:

1. **Hold the meeting on a defined schedule.** Your procedure must specify the frequency (usually annual, sometimes more). "We do it when we get around to it" is not compliant.

2. **Review specific inputs.** The standard prescribes what must be discussed: audit results, customer feedback, process performance, product conformance, supplier performance, corrective actions, follow-up from prior reviews, regulatory changes, and risk management updates.

3. **Generate documented outputs.** Decisions and actions must be recorded — especially anything related to product improvement, resource needs, or QMS changes. A meeting that produces no documentation didn't happen as far as an auditor is concerned.

4. **Close the loop from the previous review.** You must show that action items from your last management review were addressed. If you said you'd hire a second QA engineer six months ago, show that you did — or explain why you didn't.

5. **Keep records.** Minutes, attendance, input data summaries, and output action items must all be retained.

*Always refer to the actual ISO 13485:2016 standard for official requirements. This lesson is educational, not regulatory advice.*

## What Went Wrong — Clause Analysis

The CGM company failed on multiple elements simultaneously.

**§5.6.1 (General):** Reviews weren't conducted at planned intervals. Their own procedure said annually. Twenty-two months is not annually.

**§5.6.2 (Review Input):** Even in informal conversations, no one was pulling together the full picture — complaint data, CAPAs, nonconforming product trends, and supplier issues — and presenting it to top management as a unified view of QMS health. Each problem was treated as a separate operational fire, not as a signal that the system needed attention.

**§5.6.3 (Review Output):** There were no documented decisions or resource commitments tied to quality improvement. The adhesion failure trend had a fix available — a reformulated adhesive from an alternate supplier — but no formal management decision was ever made to pursue it. Without a management review output, that decision lived in someone's head, not in the QMS.

The gap: management review is not a passive report-reading exercise. It's a structured governance moment where top management takes ownership of QMS performance. When that moment never happens, quality problems compound invisibly.

## A Second Scenario

**MedCore Systems** is a 55-person contract manufacturer in Minnesota producing orthopedic bone cement delivery systems. Their QA Engineer, Priya, is two years into her first industry role and has been assigned to own the management review process.

Priya schedules the annual review for December. She builds a slide deck summarizing audit findings, complaint rates, and CAPA status. She sends it to the VP of Operations the week before. The meeting happens — 45 minutes, leadership mostly on phones — and someone says, "Looks fine, let's move on." Priya captures attendance and a one-line note: *"QMS reviewed, no major issues identified."*

Three months later, a notified body auditor asks Priya to walk her through the management review. The auditor finds: no documented review of supplier performance data, no discussion of regulatory changes affecting the European market (MDR transition timelines), and no action items despite three open CAPAs that were past due.

The auditor raises a major nonconformity.

Priya's fix, applied the following year: She builds a structured management review template that forces input data into sections tied directly to §5.6.2. She pre-assigns a data owner to each section two weeks before the meeting. She schedules a 90-minute block with agenda enforcement. And she closes the meeting only after leadership has explicitly assigned owners and due dates to every action item. The next notified body audit finds zero findings on Clause 5.6.

## What Auditors Look For

Auditors are efficient. They know exactly where management review compliance tends to collapse.

1. **"Show me the last three management review records."** If you can only produce one, or if the records are thin — a one-page summary with no input data attached — that's an immediate flag.

2. **"Were all required inputs addressed?"** Auditors will cross-reference your records against §5.6.2's input list. If supplier quality performance or regulatory updates aren't in your records, expect an observation.

3. **"What actions came out of your last management review, and can you show me they were completed?"** A management review that produces no action items in a company with open CAPAs and complaint trends is almost certainly incomplete. Auditors are skeptical of "no actions required" conclusions.

4. **"Who attended?"** If QA attended but no one from executive leadership or operations did, auditors will question whether "top management" was actually involved — which is a direct requirement of the clause.

## Key Terms

**Top Management:** The person or group that directs and controls an organization at the highest level. In a 50-person device company, this usually means the CEO, COO, or VP-level leaders — not just the QA Manager.

**QMS Suitability:** Whether the quality system is the right fit for the company's current size, products, and regulatory context. A QMS built for a Class I product may not be suitable after a Class II launch.

**QMS Adequacy:** Whether the system has enough resources, procedures, and controls to do what it's supposed to do. Chronic CAPA overdue rates often signal inadequacy.

**QMS Effectiveness:** Whether the system is actually achieving quality objectives. Effectiveness is measured — complaint rates, audit findings, first-pass yield — not assumed.

**Review Input:** The specific categories of data that must feed into a management review. Think of these as the vital signs the doctor checks at your physical.

**Review Output:** Documented decisions and action items that result from the management review. Without outputs, the review has no consequence.

**CAPA (Corrective and Preventive Action):** A formal process to identify the root cause of a problem and prevent recurrence. CAPA status is a required management review input.

## Check Your Understanding

**1. In the CGM case, what was the single most consequential failure under §5.6?**
*Answer: Management reviews weren't conducted at planned intervals. All downstream failures — missing input analysis, absent output decisions — stemmed from the fact that the reviews simply didn't happen on schedule.*

**2. Priya's first management review record showed "no major issues identified" despite three overdue CAPAs. What element of §5.6 did this violate?**
*Answer: §5.6.2 requires status of CAPAs as a review input, and §5.6.3 requires documented outputs, including any needed improvements. A meeting that doesn't engage with known problems and generate action items doesn't satisfy either requirement.*

**3. Your company's CEO says, "The QA Manager attends management review and reports to me afterward — that counts, right?" How do you respond?**
*Answer: No. The standard requires top management to conduct the review — not to receive a summary of it. The CEO or equivalent leadership must be a participant, not a recipient.*

**4. What's the difference between QMS adequacy and QMS effectiveness?**
*Answer: Adequacy asks whether you have enough — procedures, resources, controls. Effectiveness asks whether those things are working. A company can have adequate resources and still have an ineffective QMS if quality objectives aren't being met.*

## Career Impact

If you own management review, you are giving top management the information they need to make quality decisions — and holding them accountable to make those decisions on record. That's not an administrative task; it's a governance function. Early-career professionals who understand this clause deeply tend to become the person in the room who bridges QA data and leadership decision-making. That's a rare and visible skill. When you can walk a CEO through what the complaint trend means for product risk, connect it to an open CAPA, and get a resource commitment documented before the meeting ends, you've demonstrated something most QA professionals take years to learn.
