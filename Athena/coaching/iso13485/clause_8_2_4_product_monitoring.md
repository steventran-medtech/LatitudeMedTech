# ISO 13485:2016 — Clause 8.2.4: Product Monitoring

# ISO 13485:2016 Clause 8.2.4: Product Monitoring and Measurement

*Educational content only. Always refer to the actual ISO 13485:2016 standard for official requirements.*

---

## What This Clause Is About

Once a medical device is designed and manufactured, you need proof that what you're making actually meets your specifications — every time. Clause 8.2.4 requires you to monitor and measure the characteristics of your product at appropriate stages to verify requirements are being met. Without this, you're essentially shipping devices on faith rather than evidence.

---

## What It Requires (The Essentials)

**1. Monitor and measure product characteristics at appropriate stages.**
You can't just check the finished device and call it done. You need to define *where* in production you're checking things — incoming materials, in-process steps, and final product. "Appropriate stages" means stages where catching a problem early actually matters.

**2. Maintain evidence that acceptance criteria have been met.**
Checking isn't enough. You need records proving the check happened, who did it, what the result was, and whether it passed. A verbal "it looked fine" doesn't cut it.

**3. Product must not be released until all planned activities are satisfactorily completed.**
This sounds obvious, but it has teeth. If your acceptance testing isn't finished, the device doesn't ship. There's no shortcut unless a documented, approved concession process is in place.

**4. Define acceptance criteria before you start.**
You can't write the passing grade after you see the test results. Acceptance criteria must be documented in advance — typically in inspection procedures, work instructions, or device history records (DHRs).

**5. Records must identify the person authorizing release.**
Someone with authority has to sign off. That person and their authorization must be traceable in the records.

## What This Looks Like in Practice

Say you work at a 50-person company that makes infusion pumps. Here's how Clause 8.2.4 plays out in their day-to-day operations.

**Incoming inspection:** When tubing assemblies arrive from a contract manufacturer, the Receiving Inspection Technician pulls up Work Instruction WI-QC-012 and follows a sampling plan to check dimensions, lot traceability, and visual appearance. Results are logged on an Incoming Inspection Record (IIR-XXXX) in their electronic quality management system (eQMS). If the lot passes, it gets a green "Released" label and moves to the stockroom. If it fails, it goes on hold and a nonconformance report (NCR) is opened.

**In-process inspection:** On the production floor, operators follow a Batch Manufacturing Record (BMR) that includes mandatory inspection checkpoints — for example, verifying torque on a connector after assembly. The operator records the measurement, their ID, and the date. A line supervisor reviews and initials the checkpoint before the unit moves to the next step.

**Final inspection and release:** At the end of the line, a QA Inspector runs through a Final Inspection Checklist per SOP QC-045. This includes functional testing, labeling verification, and a packaging integrity check. The completed Device History Record (DHR) — which includes all the inspection records from every stage — is reviewed by the QA Manager, who signs off on product release. Nothing ships without that signature.

All of this documentation lives in the eQMS and is traceable by lot number and serial number.

## Common Mistakes and Audit Findings

**1. Acceptance criteria written after testing is complete.**
Auditors will look at the dates on your acceptance criteria versus the dates on your test results. If the criteria were created after the test was run, that's a major finding. It looks like you wrote the rules to match what you already got.

**2. Missing or incomplete DHR entries.**
Early-career QA professionals sometimes think a completed test is enough. But if the record doesn't show who performed it, when, and what instrument was used, it's incomplete. Notified bodies flag this constantly.

**3. Product released without all required sign-offs.**
Under schedule pressure, it's tempting to ship while waiting on a final piece of paperwork. Unless your procedure has a formal, documented concession process, this is a nonconformance. "We knew it was fine" is not a record.

**4. No defined sampling plan for incoming inspection.**
Saying "we inspect incoming materials" isn't enough. You need to document *how many* units you're checking and by what statistical rationale. Random spot-checks with no defined plan are a gap auditors will find.

## Key Terms to Know

**Acceptance Criteria:** Pre-defined standards that a product must meet to be considered conforming. Must be documented before testing begins.

**Device History Record (DHR):** The compiled records showing that a specific device was manufactured according to its Device Master Record. Think of it as the biography of one specific unit or lot.

**In-Process Inspection:** Checks performed during manufacturing, not just at the beginning or end. Designed to catch problems before they compound.

**Incoming Inspection:** Verification of materials, components, or subassemblies received from suppliers before they enter your production process.

**Nonconformance (NC):** A product or process that doesn't meet a specified requirement. Triggers a formal investigation and disposition process.

**Product Release:** The formal authorization to distribute or use a product after all acceptance activities are completed and verified.

**Sampling Plan:** A documented procedure defining how many units from a lot will be inspected and what statistical basis justifies that number (e.g., AQL — Acceptable Quality Level).

**Work Instruction (WI):** A step-by-step document telling an operator exactly how to perform a specific task. More detailed than a procedure.

## Check Your Understanding

**Q1: What must be established before product acceptance testing begins?**
*Answer:* Acceptance criteria must be defined and documented in advance. You cannot determine pass/fail standards after seeing results.

**Q2: Why does Clause 8.2.4 require records to identify the person who authorized release?**
*Answer:* Traceability. If a problem is discovered post-market, you need to know who made the release decision and on what basis. Accountability requires a name, not just a stamp.

**Q3 (Scenario): A production supervisor approves shipment of a lot because the final functional test is "almost done" and the results so far look good. What's the problem?**
*Answer:* Product must not be released until all planned acceptance activities are completed. Partial test completion is not acceptable unless a formal concession is documented and approved per the company's procedure. This is a nonconformance.

**Q4: What's the difference between incoming inspection and in-process inspection?**
*Answer:* Incoming inspection verifies materials/components when they arrive from suppliers. In-process inspection checks product characteristics at defined stages during manufacturing.

**Q5 (Scenario): An auditor asks to see your sampling plan for incoming inspection. You explain that your inspector checks "a few units" from each shipment. What finding will the auditor likely raise?**
*Answer:* The auditor will flag the lack of a documented, statistically justified sampling plan. "A few units" with no defined criteria or statistical basis doesn't meet the requirement for planned, documented monitoring and measurement.
