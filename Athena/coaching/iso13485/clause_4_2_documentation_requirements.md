# ISO 13485:2016 — Clause 4.2: Documentation Requirements

# ISO 13485:2016 Clause 4.2: Documentation Requirements

*Educational content only. Always refer to the actual ISO 13485:2016 standard for official requirements.*

---

## What This Clause Is About

Medical device companies live and die by their documentation. Clause 4.2 establishes the ground rules for what documents your Quality Management System (QMS) must include, how they're structured, and how they're controlled. Without these rules, you end up with outdated procedures floating around, people following different versions of the same work instruction, and no way to prove to a regulator that your processes are actually what you say they are.

---

## What It Requires (The Essentials)

**1. Your QMS needs specific documented information.**
The standard requires a Quality Manual, a Quality Policy, Quality Objectives, and documented procedures and records in areas the standard explicitly calls out. Think of this as your minimum required paperwork — the floor, not the ceiling.

**2. You must control your documents.**
Documents need to be approved before use, kept current, and protected from unauthorized changes. If your engineering team is still working from a procedure printed two years ago, that's a problem — and an audit finding waiting to happen.

**3. Records are a separate category with separate rules.**
A *document* tells people what to do (a work instruction, a procedure). A *record* proves something was done (a completed inspection log, a signed training form). Clause 4.2 requires you to distinguish between the two and control them appropriately. Records must be legible, identifiable, and retrievable.

**4. You need a Document Control Procedure.**
The standard requires a documented procedure explaining *how* you manage documents — how they get approved, how changes are made, how old versions get pulled, and how you ensure people are using current versions.

**5. External documents need to be identified and controlled too.**
Standards, customer drawings, and supplier specifications that affect your quality system are "external documents." They don't get ignored just because you didn't write them.

## What This Looks Like in Practice

Imagine a 40-person Class II device company making surgical instruments. Their QMS documentation is managed by a QA Specialist named Mara, using a basic electronic document management system (eDMS).

When a Manufacturing Engineer wants to update a work instruction for a cleaning process, here's what actually happens:

1. The engineer submits a change request in the eDMS, describing what needs to change and why.
2. Mara reviews the change against their Document Control Procedure (SOP-001) to make sure it's categorized correctly and routed to the right approvers.
3. The updated work instruction goes to the Manufacturing Manager and the QA Manager for approval. Both sign off electronically.
4. Mara releases the new version in the eDMS. The old version is automatically archived — it's still accessible for reference but clearly marked "Obsolete."
5. The eDMS notifies the production floor. The supervisor confirms affected operators have reviewed the updated instruction, and that confirmation becomes a *record*.

The key point: at any given moment, there's one approved version in use, everyone knows where to find it, and there's a paper trail showing how it got there.

## Common Mistakes and Audit Findings

**1. Treating all documents the same as records.**
Early-career professionals often use "document" and "record" interchangeably. They're not the same. A batch record is a record. A manufacturing procedure is a document. Controlling them incorrectly — or filing a procedure as if it were a record — creates confusion and gaps that auditors find quickly.

**2. Uncontrolled documents on the production floor.**
Printed copies of procedures left at workstations without any way to verify they're current is one of the most common findings in manufacturing environments. If it's printed and uncontrolled, an auditor will ask how you know it matches what's in your system. "We think it's current" is not an acceptable answer.

**3. The Quality Manual doesn't reflect actual practice.**
Some companies write a Quality Manual to pass certification, then run their business differently. When a notified body auditor asks how you handle nonconforming product and your Quality Manual says one thing but your team does another, that's a major finding.

**4. Missing or incomplete approval records.**
Documents approved via email threads, verbal sign-off, or informal review — with no documented evidence — don't satisfy the requirement. The approval happened; you just can't prove it. Auditors will ask for objective evidence (proof), not explanations.

## Key Terms to Know

**Quality Manual** — A top-level document describing the scope of your QMS, how it's structured, and how the various processes interact. Think of it as the map of your quality system.

**Document** — Any written information that describes what should be done, how it should be done, or what requirements apply. Procedures and work instructions are documents.

**Record** — Evidence that something was actually done. Records are filled in, not written ahead of time. They prove conformance or execution.

**Document Control** — The system and process for managing documents — approving them, revising them, distributing them, and retiring old versions.

**eDMS (Electronic Document Management System)** — Software used to store, control, and manage documents and records. Common examples in medical device companies include Greenlight Guru, Veeva, and MasterControl.

**Obsolete Document** — A document that's been superseded by a newer version. Must be pulled from active use but typically retained in an archive.

**External Document** — A document your company didn't author but that affects your QMS — like a referenced ISO standard, a customer specification, or a regulatory guidance document. These need to be identified and kept current.

**Objective Evidence** — Verifiable proof that something occurred. In the context of documents, it means a visible record of review and approval — not someone's memory.

## Check Your Understanding

**1. What is the difference between a document and a record?**
A document describes what to do or how to do it (a procedure, a work instruction). A record proves that something was done (a completed form, a signed log). They're controlled differently.

**2. Why does Clause 4.2 require a Document Control Procedure?**
Because the standard requires documented evidence of *how* you manage documents — the process itself must be defined and followed consistently, not improvised case by case.

**3. Scenario: An operator finds two printed copies of the same cleaning procedure on the production floor. One is dated last month; one is dated two years ago. What's the problem, and what clause does it likely violate?**
The presence of an outdated document in active use is a document control failure. It means uncontrolled copies are circulating, and you can't ensure people are following current requirements. This is a direct Clause 4.2 finding.

**4. Does Clause 4.2 apply to documents you received from your customer or a standards body?**
Yes. External documents that affect your QMS must be identified and controlled to ensure the current version is in use.

**5. Scenario: Your team approved a new SOP over email last quarter but never entered it into the eDMS. It's been in use since then. What's the problem?**
The approval isn't documented in a controlled system, the document isn't formally released, and there's no distribution record. Even if the content is good, you have no objective evidence of proper control. An auditor would likely flag this as a nonconformance.

## How This Connects to Your Career

Document control sounds administrative — and early on, it can feel that way. But understanding Clause 4.2 deeply makes you the person who prevents audit failures before they happen. QA professionals who can design a practical document control system, train teams on it, and explain it clearly during an audit are genuinely valuable. Employers — especially smaller companies that don't have large QA departments — need people who can own this function without supervision. When you understand *why* these requirements exist, not just *what* they say, you can make judgment calls confidently, spot gaps in existing systems, and build credibility with your team fast.
