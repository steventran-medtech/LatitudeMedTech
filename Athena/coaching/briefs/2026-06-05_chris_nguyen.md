## What We Know

Chris Nguyen, Quality Systems Specialist at a mid-size biotech company (pharma/biologics focus, San Diego). Seven years of QA experience, all in pharmaceutical GMP/GDP environments — SOPs, batch record review, supplier audits, FDA inspection prep under 21 CFR Part 211. No medical device experience. Reached out directly via email after reading the MedTech Meridian piece on how pharma QA professionals translate into device careers. Specifically asked: "My resume only shows pharma — will device companies even look at me?"

## Likely Profile

This is a strong career transition candidate. Seven years of pharma QA is substantial and genuinely transferable — GMP discipline, audit readiness, and SOP authorship are respected in device QA. The gap is knowledge of device-specific frameworks: 21 CFR Part 820, ISO 13485, design controls, DHF/DMR structure, complaint handling under MDR. Chris likely underestimates how much of the pharma background is directly applicable and overestimates how foreign device QA will be.

Core fear: "I'll be starting over." The coaching reframe should be: "You're not starting over — you're translating." The real work is vocabulary, framework gaps, and resume repositioning.

## Recommended Program Match

**Primary: Career Transition ($2,299 / 4 months)**
Only option that fits. The 4-month scope is necessary for full repositioning: Part 820 / ISO 13485 fluency, resume and LinkedIn overhaul to surface transferable pharma skills in device language, and targeted job search strategy for SoCal device companies (Masimo, Edwards, Natus, Alphatec, NovaBay, etc.) where pharma QA experience actually commands a premium in hybrid QA/QMS roles.

Note: the $2,299 price point may require justification — frame it against the alternative (a lateral move into a device QA Associate role at a $20K comp cut versus a structured transition that lands at parity or above).

## Discovery Call Objectives

1. Establish current comp and target comp — is Chris willing to take a temporary step back for the transition, or is that a hard constraint?
2. Understand the full breadth of pharma QA exposure: has Chris touched supplier qualifications, validation, or quality systems architecture beyond batch records?
3. Probe device industry knowledge — has Chris done any self-study (Part 820, ISO 13485 reads), or is this truly ground zero?
4. Identify the employer type: startup (needs generalists, values pharma discipline) vs. large OEM (can be slower to see the value — harder transition).

## Suggested Opening Questions

1. "You asked whether device companies will look at you — walk me through what's prompted this question right now. Is something happening at your current company, or has something changed in what you want?"
2. "Seven years of pharma QA — can you give me a sense of the scope? Are you mostly in batch records and SOPs, or have you been into supplier audits, CAPAs, quality system architecture?"
3. "Have you done any reading on the device side — Part 820, ISO 13485, anything — or is this mostly unfamiliar territory?"
4. "What kind of device company interests you? Startup, mid-size OEM, or does it matter less than the role itself?"
5. "If you landed a device QA role tomorrow, what would you need to feel confident on your first day that you currently don't have?"

## Watch-Outs

- If Chris is primarily motivated by comp and expects device QA to be a clear step up, be honest: lateral is more realistic for an initial transition, and the comp gains come after 1-2 years in device QA with a strong portfolio. Don't let unrealistic expectations set the coaching up to fail.
- Seven years in pharma with no device exposure means the transition requires genuine study, not just resume rewording. If Chris is looking for a quick fix, the Career Transition program is not that — clarify the commitment level upfront.
- The pharma biotech's pipeline health matters. If Chris's company is in a downswing or layoffs are coming, this transition may be urgent rather than planned — adjust the coaching timeline accordingly.
