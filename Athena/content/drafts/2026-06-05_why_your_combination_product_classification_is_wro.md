# Why Your Combination Product Classification Is Wrong—And Why FDA Won't Tell You Until Review Day

A San Diego injectable device company burned through $2.2 million in 505(b)(2) NDA preparation before a pre-submission meeting revealed the product was actually a 510(k)-pathway device with a drug component. The reclassification invalidated their clinical design. Eighteen months, gone. Three separate San Diego companies have repeated this pattern in two years—every time tracing back to one misread sentence in a 1989 FDA policy statement teams treat as settled law when it's a deliberate gray zone.

---

## The Core Mechanics

The FDA's 1989 *Intercenter Agreement Between CDER and CDRH* established the primary mode of action (PMOA) framework: whichever component provides the intended therapeutic effect *primarily* determines the regulatory pathway and lead center. "Primarily" has never received a numerical definition. FDA has never said "if 60% of efficacy comes from the drug component, it's CDER." It is intentionally qualitative.

Under 21 CFR §3.2(e), combination products span four product types. Assignment follows either a Request for Designation (RFD) through the Office of Combination Products (OCP) or an informal pre-sub. Critical point: **OCP advisory opinions are not binding pre-approvals.** Per 21 CFR §3.7, RFD decisions can be reconsidered if the product changes—and "change" includes updated labeling intent, formulation shifts, or how you describe mechanism of action in a clinical protocol.

The GMP compliance split is operationally brutal:

| Classification | Lead Center | Primary GMP | Secondary Requirement |
|---|---|---|---|
| Device-primary | CDRH | 21 CFR Part 820 | Drug stability per ICH Q1A |
| Drug-primary | CDER | 21 CFR Parts 210/211 | Device DHF per §820.30 |
| Biologic-primary | CBER | 21 CFR Part 600 | Device DHF + drug GMP |
| Co-packaged | Lead center varies | Both 210/211 and 820 | Full dual compliance |

The Haleon Gas-X Extra Strength Softgel recall (2025, propylene glycol contamination) illustrates the structural gap: pharmaceutical lot release protocols check drug purity; device acceptance criteria check dimensional specs. Neither standard, applied alone, catches a drug vehicle contaminant introduced at the device-component manufacturing stage.

## Documentation Strategy

Three actions protect you. First, write a **Design Intent Record**—separate from the DHF, explicitly stating which component drives therapeutic effect—before your first pre-sub. Second, keep **labeling architecture** internally consistent with that record; if your label says "the drug coating elutes to prevent restenosis," you've told CDER this is drug-primary regardless of your RFD argument. Third, run **stability protocols** concurrently: ICH Q1A(R2) for the drug substance and ISO 11607 accelerated aging for device packaging, with a unified release specification one QA owner can defend.

## What to Do This Week

**Action 1:** Compare your most recent RFD response, current labeling draft, and clinical protocol. If those three documents use different words to describe how your product works, write a reconciliation memo before your next FDA touchpoint. Anchor language to the 2017 FDA *Guidance for Industry and FDA Staff: Classification of Products as Drugs and Devices.*
