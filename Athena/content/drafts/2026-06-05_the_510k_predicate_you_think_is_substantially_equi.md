# The 510(k) Predicate You Think Is "Substantially Equivalent" Might Get You a Complete Response Letter

Three weeks into FDA review, a San Diego orthopedic device startup got the letter nobody budgets for. They'd spent six months building a 510(k) submission around a predicate cleared in 2021—a device with nearly identical intended use, comparable materials, similar dimensional specs. By every internal checklist they'd run, the substantial equivalence argument was airtight. Then the Complete Response Letter (CRL) arrived. FDA flagged three performance parameters where the predicate's cleared documentation didn't demonstrate testing conditions comparable to the new device's risk profile. Not because the new device was riskier in some obvious way—but because FDA's expectations for *what "comparable" means* had quietly moved since that predicate sailed through review. The team scrambled. Eight months of additional work. Roughly $400K in consulting fees, clinical testing, and delayed revenue. And the hardest part? Every single issue FDA raised was discoverable before submission—if someone had known to look.

I've watched this pattern repeat across three San Diego-area clients in the past eighteen months. It's not bad luck. It's a structural shift in how FDA is evaluating predicate comparability, and most QA/RA teams I talk to haven't updated their internal processes to match it.

## The Real Mechanics

The statutory standard for substantial equivalence hasn't changed since the Medical Device Amendments of 1976: same intended use, and either same technological characteristics *or* different technological characteristics that don't raise new questions of safety and effectiveness (21 CFR §807.87(f); Section 513(i) of the FD&C Act). What *has* changed is FDA's operational interpretation of what "same technological characteristics" requires you to *prove*.

Think of it like this: the rulebook is the same, but the referees have started calling fouls they used to wave off.

**The intended use vs. technological characteristics split is no longer soft.** FDA's 2023 guidance update, "Refuse to Accept Policy for 510(k)s" (January 2023), and the agency's 2022 alignment with IMDRF principles on device equivalency have hardened the line. FDA reviewers now expect explicit documentation that your predicate was *tested and cleared under conditions that match your device's specific risk profile*—not just that the two devices share a product code. An older predicate's 510(k) Summary (the public document on FDA's 510(k) database) frequently doesn't contain this evidence because it wasn't required when that device was cleared. That omission, which passed in 2020 or 2021, is now a deficiency in your 2025 submission.

**The retroactive clinical data problem.** Under 21 CFR §807.87(h) and FDA's "Guidance for the Content of Premarket Submissions for Software Contained in Medical Devices" (2023), devices with software functions—which describes roughly 40% of submissions from San Diego's device corridor, where combination products and connected diagnostics cluster—are now expected to provide performance comparability data that accounts for cybersecurity architecture, data integrity protocols, and interoperability standards. A predicate cleared in 2020 for a cloud-connected diagnostic device was cleared before FDA's 2022 final cybersecurity guidance and before the updated Software as a Medical Device (SaMD) framework was operationalized domestically. That predicate's clearance documentation is silent on these parameters—not because the company hid anything, but because FDA didn't ask. Now FDA is asking *you* to retroactively bridge that gap in your submission. Most teams discover this in response to a CRL, not before.

**What the data looks like across predicate ages:**

| Predicate Clearance Year | Cybersecurity Documentation in 510(k) Summary | Clinical Performance Data Typically Present | Risk of Comparability Deficiency in 2025 Filing |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2018–2019 | Rarely | Rarely | High |
| 2020–2021 | Sometimes (pre-2022 guidance) | Sometimes | Moderate–High |
| 2022–2023 | Usually (post-guidance alignment) | Usually | Low–Moderate |
| 2024 | Yes (under current expectations) | Yes | Low |

The practical implication: a predicate cleared before 2022 for any software-driven, connected, or combination device carries a non-trivial risk of comparability documentation gaps under current review standards. This isn't speculation—it's what I'm seeing come back in CRLs.

**What auditors look for.** During FDA's Acceptance Review and Substantive Review, reviewers cross-reference your predicate's original 510(k) Summary against the performance testing you've described in Section 10 of your submission (the Substantial Equivalence section). If your predicate summary lacks performance data on a parameter that FDA now considers standard for your device type—and you haven't proactively addressed that gap with your own data or a well-constructed rationale—you're creating a Major Deficiency waiting to be written.

## What to Do This Week

**Action 1:** Pull your predicate's 510(k) Summary from FDA's 510(k) Premarket Notification Database (accessdata.fda.gov/scripts/cdrh/cfdocs/cfpmn/pmn.cfm). Compare its documented testing parameters against FDA's current Product Code-specific guidance for your device type. If your predicate was cleared before 2022 and involves any software function, connected feature, or combination product element, flag every parameter where the summary is silent. That silence is your risk register.

**Action 2:** Run a formal predicate comparability audit against the three-question framework in FDA's 2020 guidance "Designing for Safety and Effectiveness"—intended use match, technological characteristics match, and no new safety/effectiveness questions raised. Document your answers with specific citations. If you can't answer question two without referencing your own testing data to compensate for predicate documentation gaps, you need to either strengthen your performance testing section or identify a more recent predicate before submission.
