## The Real Mechanics

The IMDRF Annexes Combined document consolidates a series of standardized reporting fields and investigative logic that companies are expected to use when submitting adverse event data to regulators across participating jurisdictions. Think of it like a universal grammar — the same sentence structure for communicating what went wrong, regardless of whether you're filing with FDA's MedWatch or submitting to Japan's PMDA. The 2025 updated Annexes D, E, F, and G are where the operational specificity lives.

**Annex D — Cause Investigation (Investigation Conclusion):** This annex defines the structured vocabulary and logic sequence for documenting root cause conclusions in adverse event reports. For QA professionals, this means your internal CAPA (Corrective and Preventive Action) process — governed by 21 CFR §820.100 and ISO 13485 §8.5.2 — now needs to produce outputs that map directly to Annex D's conclusion fields. Auditors at companies integrating multi-market submissions are already checking whether CAPA closure summaries are Annex D-compatible. If your CAPA records use freeform root cause narratives that don't align with Annex D's defined cause categories, you have a documentation gap.

**Annex E — Clinical Signs and Symptoms or Conditions:** This annex standardizes how clinical manifestations are coded and reported. The practical implication for RA professionals: your post-market surveillance (PMS) data collection instruments — complaint forms, MDR templates, trend analysis tables — need to capture clinical terminology in a format that can be translated into Annex E fields without manual rework. Under 21 CFR §803 (Medical Device Reporting) and EU MDR Article 87, speed and accuracy in adverse event filing matter. Annex E alignment reduces that translation friction significantly.

**Annex F — Health Effects (Health Impact):** Where Annex E addresses clinical presentation, Annex F addresses severity and health impact classification. This is where your risk management process (ISO 14971 §5 and §6 — probability and severity estimation) intersects with regulatory reporting. A candidate who can explain how their company's risk matrix severity categories map — or fail to map — to Annex F's health impact taxonomy is demonstrating exactly the cross-functional fluency that senior roles require.

**Annex G — Component Testing Methodology:** This is the one that ended the eight-year veteran's candidacy. Annex G provides standardized fields for documenting component-level test results within adverse event investigations. The traceability implication is direct: under 21 CFR §820.50 (Purchasing Controls) and ISO 13485 §7.4, component supplier records must be maintainable in a way that supports Annex G-compliant reporting. If a component fails in the field and triggers an MDR, your ability to pull traceable, Annex G-formatted test data quickly is now a regulatory expectation — not just an internal best practice.
