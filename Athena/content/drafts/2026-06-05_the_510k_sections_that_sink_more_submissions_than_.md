## The Real Mechanics

The FDA's 510(k) pathway clears a device when a submitter demonstrates substantial equivalence (SE) to a legally marketed predicate device — meaning the new device has the same intended use and the same or different technological characteristics that don't raise new safety questions (21 CFR §807.87; FDA Guidance: The 510(k) Program, 2014). Three sections account for the overwhelming majority of rejection patterns I see.

**1. Predicate Selection: The Foundation You Can't Paper Over**

Predicate selection is not a literature review exercise. It's a legal and technical argument. Sponsors routinely make two errors here: selecting a predicate that's *technically similar* but has a materially different intended use, or stacking predicates (the "split predicate" approach) in a way that FDA reviewers read as cherry-picking.

FDA's guidance on the use of split predicates allows referencing one device for intended use and another for technological characteristics — but this approach has been under heightened scrutiny since FDA's 2014 guidance explicitly warned that it "may not be appropriate in all situations." If your intended use predicate cleared in a low-risk context and your technological characteristics predicate is from a higher-risk device, you've built your argument on sand.

The fix: Run a predicate search in FDA's 510(k) database (accessible at accessdata.fda.gov) before you draft a single section. Confirm the predicate's cleared intended use language verbatim. If you can't quote it directly, you haven't found your predicate yet.

**2. Substantial Equivalence Arguments That Don't Hold**

Substantial equivalence (SE) is a term of art, not a casual assertion. Under 21 CFR §807.87(f), you must affirmatively demonstrate SE — not merely assert it. The most common failure mode: sponsors write comparison tables that show similarities but don't address differences. FDA reviewers are specifically trained to find the one row you left out.

If your device has a technological difference from your predicate, you must either (a) show that the difference doesn't raise new safety or effectiveness questions, or (b) provide performance data demonstrating the device is at least as safe and effective. Failing to address a difference doesn't make it disappear — it makes the reviewer send you an AI request asking why you ignored it.

The second failure mode: using vague performance language ("comparable to predicate") without citing the specific test methods, standards, or acceptance criteria. FDA wants to see the actual comparison, not your conclusion about the comparison.

**3. Performance Testing Gaps**

This is where the Carlsbad team's fourth rejection likely lives. Performance testing must be grounded in recognized consensus standards (e.g., ISO, ASTM, IEC) or a scientifically justified alternative. Two gaps recur constantly:

- **Biocompatibility**: Testing cited under ISO 10993-1 (Biological Evaluation of Medical Devices) is frequently incomplete because sponsors apply the wrong device categorization or fail to address extractables and leachables for devices with prolonged contact.
- **Electrical safety and EMC**: For active devices, omitting IEC 60601-1 (general safety) or the applicable 60601-1-X collateral/particular standards is an immediate flag. I've seen submissions cite the 2005 edition of a standard when FDA has recognized the 2012 amended version since 2017.

The table below shows the three failure zones, the regulatory hook, and the most common specific gap:

| Failure Zone | Regulatory Anchor | Most Common Specific Gap |
|---|---|---|
| Predicate Selection | 21 CFR §807.87(e); FDA 510(k) Program Guidance (2014) | Intended use mismatch; inappropriate split predicate |

## What to Do This Week

**Action 1:** Pull three cleared 510(k)s in your device classification from FDA's 510(k) database (accessdata.fda.gov/scripts/cdrh/cfdocs/cfpmn/pmn.cfm). Download the summary for each. Map their substantial equivalence argument structure: how do they characterize the intended use, how do they address differences, and which performance standards do they cite? This is the fastest way to calibrate what an acceptable argument looks like for your product code.

**Action 2:** Cross-reference every performance standard you're currently citing against FDA's recognized consensus standards database (accessdata.fda.gov/scripts/cdrh/cfdocs/cfstandards/search.cfm). If the version you're testing to isn't on FDA's recognized list, you need either the updated test data or a written justification explaining why the older version is scientifically equivalent. Do this before your test reports are finalized, not after.
