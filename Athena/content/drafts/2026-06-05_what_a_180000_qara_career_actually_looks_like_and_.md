## The Real Mechanics

**The credentials that actually move the needle**

Not all certifications are equal in the eyes of a hiring manager reviewing a stack of resumes. Here's what I've observed directly, confirmed by the offers my clients have received:

- **RAC (Regulatory Affairs Certification) from RAPS** is the baseline signal. Without it at the Senior Manager level, you're already behind. It demonstrates command of the regulatory framework under 21 CFR Part 820 (Quality System Regulation, now transitioning to Part 820 aligned with ISO 13485:2016) and familiarity with global submission pathways.

- **ASQ CQE (Certified Quality Engineer)** matters most if you're on the QA side and want to move into hybrid QA/RA roles — which, increasingly, is where the Director-level jobs live. Companies like Dexcom and Masimo aren't hiring a QA VP and an RA VP separately at the $5B revenue level; they want leaders who can straddle both.

- **ISO 13485 Lead Auditor certification** is undervalued and underused. I've seen this single credential shift salary conversations by $15,000 because it signals you can run the audit program, not just participate in it.

What I explicitly do *not* recommend chasing early: generic project management certifications (PMP, etc.) unless you're moving into program management. They don't speak to regulatory depth.

**The job titles that unlock six figures**

The title sequence matters. Here's the realistic map:

| Title | Typical Base (SoCal) | Key Unlock |
|---|---|---|
| QA/RA Specialist I–II | $62,000–$82,000 | Foundation: SOPs, CAPA, complaint handling |
| Senior QA/RA Specialist | $88,000–$108,000 | Submission ownership (510(k), PMA modules) |
| QA/RA Manager | $110,000–$135,000 | Team oversight + audit lead |
| Senior Manager / Principal | $130,000–$155,000 | Cross-functional project leadership |
| Director, RA/QA | $160,000–$195,000 | Strategy, regulatory intelligence, board-level exposure |

The jump from Specialist to Manager is where most people stall. The unlock is submission ownership — meaning you've shepherded a 510(k) or PMA module from gap analysis through FDA acceptance, not just supported someone else doing it. If your current role doesn't give you that exposure, you need to either negotiate for it internally or move.

**What hiring managers at the Director level actually screen for**

I've sat across the table from enough hiring managers at companies ranging from BD to early-stage Class II startups to know the real interview signals. They're not looking for someone who can recite 21 CFR §820.30(b) (design controls). They're looking for three things:

1. **Regulatory judgment under ambiguity.** The IMDRF (International Medical Device Regulators Forum) framework — including harmonized annexes covering cause investigation, clinical signs and symptoms, and health impact assessment — is increasingly shaping how global submissions are structured. A Director candidate who can articulate how IMDRF harmonization affects a U.S.-based 510(k) strategy is immediately differentiated. Most candidates can't do this.

2. **Commercial fluency.** Can you translate a regulatory constraint into a business trade-off? "We have a 90-day FDA review clock and three design iterations in queue — here's how I'd sequence them to protect launch" is a Director conversation. "We submitted and we're waiting" is not.

3. **Evidence of building something.** A QMS (Quality Management System) built from scratch, a remediation program that closed a 483 observation, a training curriculum for a 40-person QA team. Artifacts. Not participation — ownership.

## What to Do This Week

**Action 1:** Pull up the FDA's 510(k) database at [accessdata.fda.gov](https://www.accessdata.fda.gov/scripts/cdrh/cfdocs/cfpmn/pmn.cfm) and find three cleared devices in the product category you work in or want to work in. Read the summaries. Map the predicate chain. This takes 90 minutes and gives you submission literacy you can reference in any interview.

**Action 2:** Search LinkedIn for "Senior Manager Regulatory Affairs" + "San Diego" filtered to postings in the last 30 days. Pull the three most common requirements across five postings. If RAC, 510(k) experience, and ISO 13485 auditor don't appear — I'd be surprised. Cross-reference against your resume and identify the single largest gap. Then register for the RAPS RAC exam if you haven't, or book a Lead Auditor course if RAC is already in hand. Cognitivo and Oriel STAT A MATRIX both run these quarterly.
